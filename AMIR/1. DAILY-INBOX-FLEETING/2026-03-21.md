## Obstacles vs. Objections

An obstacle is a thing that blocks one's way or prevents or hinders progress. When someone disagrees with you before you've asked for the sale, that is an obstacle. Example: 'Hey, why'd you hop on the call today?' They say, 'I just wanted to find out a little bit more about the program.' That's an obstacle — it happened in the first ten seconds, before you've ever asked for anything. It's easy to confront and destroy obstacles before you ask for the sale.

Once you've asked for the sale, you switch to objections. An objection is when they disagree after you've presented the price and asked them to buy. It's much easier to break someone's limiting beliefs before the stakes have been presented. After you're in the red zone, it's all closing — all objection handling.

## The Three Prerequisites

Before diving into the specific objections, here's an important foundation: the person must really want the goal, and they must believe three things. First, that the product will get them the goal — the way they want to get there. I learned that second half the hard way. At Gym Launch, people would sometimes say no to buying memberships, but when we offered them the ability to come back the next day and get help for free, we'd sell them $400 in supplements — even though they'd just said no to a $100-down service. It's because they wanted to lose weight, but they wanted to lose weight their way. So you have to make sure the product gets them there the way they want to get there.

Second, that you and others will support them. 'Are you telling me the truth? Will other people around me support this decision?' Third, that it will work for them — not just for everyone else. 'Sure, I've seen people succeed, but they're different from me. I'm a special case.' When someone raises this, you have to provide proof so compelling that it'd be more unreasonable not to believe than to believe. Ask: 'How many people would you need to see for it to be more unreasonable to doubt than to believe? Ten? Fifty? Here's a thousand — tell me when to stop.' That's not an emotional close. That's a logical close.

## The Three Core Distortions

I went back to Dr. Albert Ellis, who invented cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT). He originally had eleven distortions of reality, then dropped it to nine, then seven, and finally settled on three. The reason this matters for sales: the objections people give you — the places where they cast their power — are distortions of reality. They are the things we use to upset ourselves.

Distortion one — Circumstances: 'I must get what I want when I want it. I must not get what I don't want. If I don't get what I want, I can't stand it.' This is when people blame their emotional state on external circumstances.

Distortion two — Others: 'Other people must treat me fairly and kindly. If they don't, they are no good and deserve to be condemned and punished.' That's the animal brain.

Distortion three — Self: 'I must do well, or else I am no good.'

These are the three core distortions that upset people. They are not real. If you can understand — not memorize, but truly understand — this, you can have high-stakes conversations while knowing exactly which distortion someone is suffering from, and you can help them through it.

## The Onion of Blame

People start on the outside — they blame circumstances: time, money, other things. The next layer in, they blame other people: my spouse, my kids, my employees. And finally, at the core, they say, 'It's me. It's my fault.' You might have to overcome multiple layers as you peel back the onion. Based on the objection they give you, you can know where you are and how close you are.

If someone just says, 'I have to think about it,' you're already talking to someone who is in power and is the decision-maker. You just need to help them say yes. If they say, 'I don't have the money,' they're casting their power away. These distortions manifest in five ways: Time, Value/Price/Money, Fit ('I'm a special snowflake'), Authority ('I don't have the authority to make the decision'), and Self/Avoidance ('I just won't make the decision — I'll let life make it for me').

# Part 4: Overcoming the Five Objections

## Objection 1 — Time ('I'm Too Busy / It's Not a Good Time')

There are three angles: macro ('It's a busy season for me'), micro ('I don't have any time in my day'), and the 'when/then' fallacy ('When I have time, I'll start').

Macro approach — never disagree with the prospect, because you're not in a fight: 'Totally understand. Do you want this to be something that lasts for the long term? Do you think you're going to be busy again in the future? Then don't you think it'd be best to start now, when you are busy? Because if you learn how to do it when you're busy, you'll be able to do it forever. If you can only learn it during a perfect circumstance, you're going to fall off when it gets busy again. And isn't that exactly the time you'd want the most support?'

Micro approach: 'I had the same issue — I used to complain all the time about not having time. My wife got so sick and tired of hearing it that she pulled out my phone and said, 'Look, I just found your time.' And I was like, damn. Here's the truth: the first thing any good program will do is cut out the 90% of the things you're doing that are causing you to feel overwhelmed — which is probably the real reason you feel like you don't have time. Jeff Bezos has the same amount of time as you. It's just about what you're doing that he's not. It's not about adding things to your plate — it's about removing what's not working. Because clearly, everything filling your time right now is not making you more money.'

The when/then fallacy: 'I totally understand — I was stuck like this for years.' Then step into their shoes and walk them through the epiphany. The when/then fallacy simply states: 'When I have X, then I will do Y' — but it flips the sequence in an illogical way. It's like saying, 'When I get better, I will go to the hospital.' Or, 'I'll start saving money once I'm rich.' Or, 'I'll pay for the program that helps me make more money once I have more money.' That's the point of the program. It's a logical distortion — it's not real. Walk them through a couple of absurd examples and let the lightbulb go off.

## Objection 2 — Money ('I Can't Afford It / It's Too Expensive')

Framework one — why a lot of money is actually a good sign: Ask, 'Is this a lot of money to you?' If they say yes: 'That's the exact reason you're going to be successful. The people who don't flinch at the price are often not all-in on it. But the question is: do you want to draw a line in the sand and go all in? Do you want to step over the line and be the person you want to be? This isn't about whether the program works — I've already proven that. The question is whether you work. If you put more on the line, you have a higher likelihood of success. The best stories always come from people in your exact situation. Don't let this be a reason not to do it — it should be the reason to do it.'

Framework two — relative value: 'It's a lot in absolute terms, but very little in relative terms. If all this does is add $10,000 a month to your income, is it worth it? If all this does is get you to your goal weight, is it worth it?' Once they say yes, it's not about the price anymore — it's about whether they believe you can deliver. So you can sidestep the price objection entirely.

Framework three — comparison: 'There's no four-year degree for what you're trying to do — that's why the alternative education industry exists. And some of those degrees cost $50,000 to $200,000 and take four years. You've got something that can help you make that entire amount in half the time, for a tenth of the price. When you frame it that way, it's actually a great deal.'

Framework four — what's money good for anyway: 'You're going to spend this money either way over the next 12 months. Not only that — you're going to buy the program. The question is whether you're going to pay for it in money or pay for it in time. You can learn every single lesson in this program on your own — whether it's losing weight, getting leads, selling online, whatever. You're going to learn these lessons. But do you want it to take 12 weeks or 12 years? So the question isn't whether you're going to buy it or not — it's just how you want to pay.'

And a classic: 'Who here knows the difference between a self-made billionaire when they had nothing and where you are right now? If you have nothing, you're both broke. They were broke. You're broke. The only difference is that that proves it was never about the resources they had — it was about how resourceful they were.' Phil Knight, in Shoe Dog, had already maxed out his credit lines, the banks said no, and the next lenders canceled on him. The company was about to fold. So he went to his vendors and told them they needed to pay his payroll for him. Resourceful, not resources. When you have nothing, it's the easiest excuse to give yourself. But every self-made millionaire started with nothing — which puts them in the exact same seat you're in. So the question is: do you want to have power, or not?

## Objection 3 — Fit ('I'm Not Sure It's for Me')

Framework one — new identity, new priorities: 'We vote with our dollars about the things we care about. Show me where someone spends their money and their time, and I can tell you what their future looks like. You've had priorities that are aligned with your old identity — the you that goes out, the you that spends money on things that don't move you forward. And then there's the you that you want to be. Right now, we can draw a line in the sand. Do you want to keep walking down the road you've been walking? If not, you have to step into a new identity. And with that new identity come new priorities. People who want to be successful spend money on themselves, on education, on their skill sets. Just like those little girls stepping into a new identity at Sephora — we too must step into the identity we want to be.'

Framework two — change the change: 'What you've been doing has been getting you what you've been getting. You've got to change the change. It's going to hurt — because change hurts. The question is whether the pain of staying the same is greater than the pain of change. Are you in enough pain? Because some people's rock bottom is at 12% body fat, and some people's is at 500 pounds — but everyone has one. All you really have to do is decide to move what 'rock bottom' means for you. Is your current situation more painful than the change you'd have to go through to get where you want to go?'

Framework three — the hypothetical: 'If this were perfect, would you do it?' You get a hypothetical agreement. If they say yes, flip it: 'Then what's the difference between perfect and what we've got?' People often can't actually tell you, because they don't know all the things you can deliver. Most of the time they'll say, 'I don't know,' and you say, 'Right — because it sounds like it has nothing to do with the program. It's all about you. So let's talk about that.' One step closer to the truth. If they do give a specific answer — 'I just don't want to do broccoli' — then you say, 'What if we did green beans instead?' And they say, 'Yeah, sure.' And you say, 'Sign here.' You would be amazed how many times something exactly like that happens.

## Objection 4 — Authority ('I Have to Talk to My Spouse / Partner')

The spouse or decision-maker is not there. So you try to sidestep it by isolating the objection. The partner doesn't know anything about you — so how could they object? Ask: 'What do you think they wouldn't like?' They'll tell you, and then you just overcome that specific thing — which has nothing to do with the spouse, because the spouse isn't there.

Walk through the logic: 'Do they approve of your current struggle? Are they happy that you're struggling? No. Then why would they not approve of something that's going to fix something they already don't approve of? They're not happy with how you're currently doing — why would they be against something that fixes a problem they're already unhappy about?'

Then: 'If the roles were reversed, and your husband or wife needed this program to make their dreams happen, would you support them? Then why wouldn't you support yourself?'

And the kicker: 'I think the real problem here is that you're asking for permission instead of support. Because it is your life, not theirs. If you give that power to them, and two years from now you're still in the same spot — who are you going to blame? Don't explain the decision as if you're seeking approval. Explain it this way: 'If I don't do this for me, I'm going to end up resenting you, and I don't want that. Instead of complaining to you every day about how we don't have money or how I don't like my body, I'm now doing something about it. I'm joining a community of people just like me. I'm not asking your permission. I'm just asking for your support.''

## Objection 5 — Avoidance ('I Need to Think About It')

Now we've exhausted all outside reasons. We're finally talking to someone who is in power. A good coach doesn't make decisions for people — they help people make decisions.

Past frame: 'This isn't a fast decision. You've been making this decision for the last six years. All we're deciding is that you're actually going to do something about it. How long have you wanted this? A long time. So this is not a fast decision at all.' Then go to sunk cost: 'You bought a ticket, got your flights, got a hotel, blocked the time, flew here, listened to this whole presentation — you're six inches from gold. This isn't a fast decision. You did all of these things because it's important to you. Don't let that distortion stop you from getting what you want.'

Then: 'Don't let a bad decision burn you twice. Once when you made the poor investment — but a second time when you let that bad investment stop you from a good one. It would be like having a bad eighth-grade relationship and deciding, 'You know what, relationships aren't for me.' There are good coaches and bad coaches, just like there are good and bad relationships. If you let the bad one control your next decision, you get burned twice.'

And: 'Do you think you might be in this position because you've struggled to make decisions in the past? Have you waffled when it came time to make the hard call? Have you been in this exact conversation before and said no? Do you think that might be why you're still here? Okay — let's change that. One decision can change your life.'

And: 'Are you tired of another year of 'almost'? Almost hitting your goals, almost getting to $10K a month, almost retiring your spouse, almost moving into the neighborhood you wanted? If you're tired of another year of almost, then we can't keep doing what we've been doing — because we're going to get another year of almost. The question isn't what it costs you. It's how much has it cost you not to decide up to this point? The cost of inaction. When we think about our lives in reverse, we never regret the things we did — we regret the things we didn't do, the opportunities we let pass by.'

Present frame: 'Let's walk through what thinking about it actually looks like. You're not going to go home, sit in a rocking chair, smoke a cigarette, and stare at the clouds wondering, 'Should I do this?' Of course not. You're going to get in your car, realize you have to pick up the kids, get groceries, do laundry, cook dinner. Three, four, five days from now you're going to put on that pair of jeans and it won't fit, and you'll think, 'Damn.' And just like that, you'll have made the decision — by not making one. So let's just make it now. It doesn't take time to make a decision. It takes information. And I'm the only source of information you've got. So let's talk.'

The three questions: 'Do you believe this product or service is going to help you achieve what you want — yes or no? Do you trust me to fulfill my word — to help you, make these calls, do these workouts, do whatever I said I'd do? Do you think it'll work for you?' If they say no to the last one, ask why not, and they have to defend it. Then: 'Do you have, or have access to, the money to get started?' If they believe in all three, it is your moral obligation to do everything in your power to help them get started. If I was giving you a Ferrari right now for $5,000, you'd find the money. If your landlord told you to come up with $10,000 or get evicted, you'd find the money. So find the money. Let's do it.

Future frame: 'Let's consider the options. Option one: you do the thing and get the result — life is awesome. Option two: you don't do the thing and you don't get the result, because you didn't do the thing. Option three: you do the thing and don't get the result. Because we have a guarantee, all of these are essentially risk-free — except for one. Only one of them has a true guarantee of not getting you where you want, and that's walking out the door. So which risk-free option do you want?'

And: 'You're not going to struggle forever — you're going to do something about this eventually. If you're going to fix it eventually, you might as well start fixing it now, so you can start enjoying the results sooner. Would you prefer making more progress faster or slower?'

And finally: 'Do you think making this decision is going to get you closer to or further from your goal? That's it. If we keep making decisions that get us closer, we will get there eventually. We don't need to be snipers — we just need to be directionally right. There's no one program that changed my life. But the decision to invest in education changed my life forever.'

# Part 5: Final Thoughts — Power and Education

## Creating vs. Maintaining Fortunes

Fortunes are created by taking a lot of risk with a little bit of money. Fortunes are maintained by taking a little bit of risk with a lot of money. Every self-made person takes a lot of risk with a little bit of money, and sometimes you need to take that step because it's the only way to build the fortune.

## Invest in What Can't Be Taken

Everything I've made in my life is the result of investing in my own education. It has gotten me far higher returns than any stock, any real estate, or any crypto — by a mile. Buy experience and skills, because no government can take that from you, no divorce can take that from you, nothing can take that from you.

We're Persian, and my parents had to flee Iran during the Revolution. They had land and houses — everything. My uncle had started the lottery in Iran. Very rich. He went to London; my dad went to the US. My dad had no assets, but he had skills. He was able to scrape together enough to buy a print shop, and he built from scratch. I went to visit my uncle in London, and it was incredibly sad. My dad had given my uncle's wife a nice scarf, and she just broke down. She said, 'I used to have rooms full of these. Now I have nothing.' My dad came to the United States with $1,000 — but because he had skills, he built everything from scratch. That's why I was always told: it's the only thing no one can take from you.

Why would you not invest in the one thing that can never be stolen, can never be taken, and compounds with time? It increases your capacity to make money and your capacity to live.

## The Bridge

I like to think of the investments I've made in myself as bricks on a bridge. I'm on the bottom left, and where I want to be is the top right. There are many skills it takes to cross the bridge — one brick at a time. Just like you have an arithmetic teacher in high school, and once you learn calculus you don't say the arithmetic teacher was a waste — you learn things in sequence. I always come back to the same question: is this going to get me closer to where I want to go? I don't need it to get me there. I just need to get closer. If I get better, I'll get there eventually — as long as I don't stop moving.

## The Time Tax of Ignorance

People ask me all the time, 'How do you move so quickly?' The answer is: I know how to buy time. And how do you buy time? You buy the time it took other people to make mistakes that taught them the lessons they have. Edison took however long to make a light bulb. We just bought the lesson from him — now we have light bulbs. That's how civilization moves forward. Right now, the ignorance of not knowing how to create a million dollars a year is costing you a million dollars a year. Think about it. Therefore, you should always be willing to invest money to increase the capacity for your income — because once you have it, that capacity pays you forever.

Someone asked Leila, 'What's the number one thing people from zero to $10K a month are messing up?' And the answer is: you don't know what you're doing. You're ignorant. Not because you don't have the processing power or the ability to reason — it's just because you don't know. So the goal is to pay down the time tax of ignorance as fast as humanly possible, and the only way to do that is by investing in education — specifically, experiences that build skills.

## The Closing Story

Ten years ago, I emailed 40 gym owners and said, 'Hey, I think I want to get into fitness — can somebody help me out?' One guy said, 'Sure, you can work for free.' That guy was Sam Bakhtiar — 'Seven Figure Sam,' as he used to be known. He actually passed away during COVID, which was really sad for me, because I really looked up to him.

When I got to his office, I had literally driven across the country from Baltimore to Chino Hills, California, and showed up without notice. He was like, 'You're a psychopath. I just met you from the internet.' I said, 'I'm here. I'm ready to learn.' He said, 'I'm going to lunch.' But when he came back, he said I should join his mastermind. I didn't even have a gym. He said it was $10,000. I was 22 and didn't have a lot of money. I hesitated. He said, 'You need to commit, man. You've been waffling back and forth.' And thank God he said that — because I did take a step over the line.

I had no idea what I was doing, but I was trying to start paying down the time tax of ignorance. I got around a whole group of other fitness professionals and learned way more from all of them than I ever learned from Sam himself. But because of that experience, my beliefs started to change. The way I saw the world changed. The traits I started to embody changed. The mastermind I got from Sam is not what made me $100 million — but it got me on the right path toward making it.

If you can make those choices, I think it's the small steps that matter — not believing something is going to be a savior, but believing that it's aligned with where you want to go, and believing in yourself that if you keep walking on this path, you'll get there. Thank you.
