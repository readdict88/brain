# The Belief Continuum

## Belief Is Not Binary

In this video I want to talk about how to close a higher percentage of deals, and how to close more deals overall. It all centers around a single concept: the belief continuum.

Picture this visual: you've got your salesman on one side and your prospect on the other. The prospect has an empty bubble — completely empty, or even slightly negative. The salesman has his belief bubble. Many people like to think about belief as a binary question: do you believe in what you are selling — yes or no? But it would be much more accurate to ask: to what extent do you believe? Because belief is not a binary question. It is a continuum. It is: how much do you believe in what you are selling?

Because if you thoroughly, 100%, believe with all of your heart and mind that what you are selling is true, I guarantee you that you will sell significantly more.

# The Two Equations of Selling

## Equation 1: Closing Percentage = Skill × Conviction

Many people — I would say a disproportionate number of people — focus on the skill of selling. And there absolutely is a skill to sales. You have to know exactly what to say and how to say it. But you can shortcut the path to success by having conviction. And the way you build conviction is by ==deeply understanding the needs of your prospects.==

When you are talking to somebody and you know exactly what their problem is — and your product perfectly matches that problem — you can have genuine conviction when you sell it. Most salespeople don't even listen to the prospect at all. They just want to deliver their pitch, which inherently means they can't be all that convicted in the product, because they're not even thinking about whether it solves the prospect's specific problem. You can't be convicted that something will solve a problem you haven't taken the time to understand.

This points to a key shift that needs to happen in how sales teams are trained. Most sales training is currently focused on educating salespeople about the product. It should be focused primarily on educating salespeople about the prospect and their problems — not the product. This is especially important if you're entering a market where you are not personally the avatar. One reason so many successful businesses are built by people who suffered the pain themselves first is that they desperately, clearly understand the thoughts, needs, and pains of the prospect — and they know exactly how the product solves those problems.

So the first equation, for closing percentage: Closing Percentage = Skill of Sales × Conviction. And here's the critical insight — if skill of sales is a scale of 1 to 10, I believe conviction can be a scale of 1 to 100. Even if your skill of sales is a 2, a conviction of 100 will still get you to close, because people can sense whether you genuinely believe what you are telling them. They can feel whether you believe that what you are selling is going to solve their problem.

Most salespeople, most sales teams, and most sales managers do not focus enough on this — on conviction, on the extent to which the salesman truly believes in what they are selling. If you think about what sales fundamentally is, it is a transference of belief. How can you transfer belief from an empty cup? How can you pour from a cup that is only one-third full into another empty cup and expect it to rise above the threshold a prospect needs to make a purchasing decision? Of course you can't.

So yes, you still need the script. You still need to know the right things to say, the right pauses, the right tonality, the right emphasis. That matters. There is absolutely a place for sales skill. But the single most underutilized lever — often completely unused — is conviction. The extent to which the salesman believes in what they are selling and its ability to solve the prospect's problems is what will most influence closing percentage, for an individual or a team.

## Equation 2: Total Units Sold = Skill × Conviction × Work Ethic

The second piece is work ethic. You've probably seen both really strong and really weak closers based on their closing percentage — but also based on their total number of deals closed. When someone has really high work ethic, they can compensate for a lower closing percentage simply through more activity: following up with their pipeline faster, following up more times, reaching out to people on their own time to create more opportunities. More at-bats.

So the expert salesman has both: a high closing percentage and a high number of total units sold. There are three variables — skill, conviction, and work ethic — but conviction counts twice. Here's why: if you genuinely believe that what you are selling is going to help people, you will follow up faster. You will follow up more consistently. You will follow up as though you are trying to save someone — because in your mind, you are. Conviction drives both closing percentage and work ethic simultaneously.

# The Bitcoin Thought Experiment

Here's a thought experiment. Imagine you could go back in time five years. You can talk to your past self, but you can't tell them who you are or that you're from the future. And you say to them: 'Hey, I want you to put 100% of the money in your bank account into something called Bitcoin' — or Amazon, or Tesla, or whatever blew up over the last five years.

Your past self looks at you and says, 'I'm good, thanks.'

How many times would you follow up? How many different ways would you try to convince them? How convicted would you be in every conversation? Would you even be focused on the skill of selling — on getting the wording exactly right — or would you be focused entirely on how true and certain what you're saying is?

Think about how you would approach that. That is what it looks like to have pure conviction — to know with absolute certainty that what you are selling is going to solve your prospect's problem and change their life. If you can make that perspective shift, everything about how you sell will change.

# What This Means for Sales Managers

If you are a sales manager trying to build a great sales team, I would implore you to focus far more of your time on the thing that counts twice in the selling equation: the extent to which your salespeople believe in what they are selling and who they are selling it to.

The skill of selling matters — I have plenty of videos on exactly what to say and how to say it. But in a forced-multiplication equation, skill and conviction are not equivalent. Conviction is the bigger lever, and it is the most neglected one.

The proof is everywhere. Look at every major religious movement in history. You are selling something very hard to sell: a completely new way of seeing the world. And in most cases, those people were not trained in the skill of selling. They were simply gotten to believe wholeheartedly in what they were spreading. That is what drives people to share, to follow up, to persist.

So if your salespeople are not genuinely drinking the Kool-Aid of the products or services you sell, that is your problem to fix — not their scripting, not their tonality, not whether they are properly clarifying the prospect's problem at the top of the call. Fix the conviction, and everything else follows.

Think about it this way: if your salespeople believed wholeheartedly in the solution they were selling, would they not naturally spend more time honing their skill — because they want to help more people? Would they not follow up with as many people as possible, and be quick to respond, because they know how much it's going to change that person's life?

As Simon Sinek says: start with why. I believe that the skill of sales is fundamentally a transference of belief — and we need to start with why for salespeople. Because the question is never whether they believe. The question is always: to what extent do they believe? Get that number to 100, and you will never have a problem selling again.
