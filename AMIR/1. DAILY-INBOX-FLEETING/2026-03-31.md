## What Branding Actually Is

Branding is a deliberate pairing of things through an outcome. Let's use Coca-Cola, drinking it, and liking it as our example. The 'yum' — that's the outcome, what people get. That's paired with drinking — the action, what they do to get it — with Coca-Cola, the product. So the next time you want some 'yum,' you're probably going to reach for a Coca-Cola, if that pairing was successful for you.

But sometimes businesses pair their stuff with things that people don't like. That's bad branding, and it leads to losses for the business. You may have seen Dylan Mulvaney doing a collaboration with Bud Light. There was a lot of press around this advertisement. This advertisement was actually a great advertisement — and you might think I'm crazy, but let me explain. It was good advertising because it let a lot of people know about Bud Light. By the way, that is the definition of advertising, not branding. Advertising is letting people know about your stuff. Branding is the pairing that occurs as a result. So it was good advertising but bad branding. Many customers hated this pairing, fewer people bought the product, which netted a loss for the business.

To fix this, Bud Light paired their product with stuff the audience liked — like Shane Gillis, who is a man's man comedian, and the UFC, a man's man sport — and sales began to recover.

## Good Branding vs. Bad Branding

Branding always occurs. But our goal is good branding. Good branding is a deliberate pairing of our business with good outcomes for our ideal customers. What you pair your business with determines two key things: one, who pays attention to your business, and two, whether they move toward your business — like the UFC example — or away from your business — like the Dylan Mulvaney example.

## The History of Branding: Cattle and Symbols

The earliest version of branding that I can think of happened on livestock. It was literally a brand — they would heat up metal, sear it into the side of cattle, and get a lovely little logo. Those symbols had a magical effect.

Let's say you're walking around and you see a cow with no brand. You say hello and move on. On the other hand, let's say the cow has a brand you recognize — say it's your neighbor's. If you like your neighbor, you might grab the cow and return it. If you hate your neighbor, the cow may stay lost in the wilderness forever and become lunch for your family. Either way, the brand affected what you did. It affected your behavior.

Now tactically — how do we translate that concept into getting people to buy? Let's say we have a weak brand because it's starting out. We don't have a lot of association. We want to pair it with people, experiences, and other things that our ideal customer likes. Nike pairing with LeBron and Tiger — champions, world-class, GOATs. People who like sports and competition would see that as a positive pairing. So the brand grows.

## How a Strong Brand Makes You Money

A weak brand, after being paired, becomes a strong brand. A strong brand gets put onto a product and transforms a generic product into a premium one. Then customers want to associate themselves with that winning product — and they do it with their money.

Warren Buffett said: 'The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by 10%, then you've got a terrible business.'

Good branding drives premium pricing. With a generic white t-shirt, you might get a 0.5% click-through rate on advertising. With a Nike branded t-shirt — the same identical shirt with the swoosh — you might get six times as many people to click and buy at a higher price. That is why these brands exist for such a long period of time and make so much money. They get cheaper customers, higher returns, and better response rates in advertising.

On top of that, good branding drives customer loyalty — they buy more stuff more often. Like the Apple example: once you buy one Apple product, you tend to keep buying them. A good brand also protects your business from competitors stealing that customer in the future.

## The Three Metrics of Brand Strength

Brand has three main metrics. One, influence — how likely it is to change someone's behavior. Two, direction — are they changing the way we wanted, or are they running away? Three, reach — how many people it changes behavior for.

A small, weak, neutral brand: very few people recognize it, and the people that do don't care much either way. On the other polar extreme, a large, strong, positive brand: lots of people recognize it, it changes behavior when they see it, and the behavior is generally toward the brand.

A lot of people have the misconception that any strong brand must be polarizing — that because lots of people love it, lots of people also have to hate it. That's not true. Taylor Swift: the vast majority of people who see her recognize her, and most of the time it's toward her. She is a large, positive, strong brand. The same goes for Apple and Mother Teresa — strong brands that most people respond positively to.

## How to Start and Grow Your Brand

If branding is the deliberate pairing of your thing with something your ideal customer likes, then to start a brand, we have to know what we want to pair it with to attract ideal customers.

I like to think about assembling the pairings for a brand like a table full of flowers. If you want to put a bouquet together, you start with lots of different flowers all over the place. On their own, those flowers are not a bouquet — just as products, values, experiences, and people on their own are not a brand. The flowers are like the brand elements that we pair with things our audiences like. With enough pairing, over and over again, they form a bouquet. Replace the word 'bouquet' with 'brand.'

A brand fundamentally doesn't actually exist as a physical thing. If I take the flowers out of the vase and scatter them, was there ever a brand to begin with? It's simply the association we make between those things that creates the one-of-a-kind brand. The deliberate pairing of those things makes the brand.

If I want to narrow my brand, I just talk a lot about one specific topic. If I want to expand, I talk about topics that are more adjacent, then broader and broader. Distant and random pairings hurt a brand because they're so hard to make associations with. Most people's brands happen by accident — it's just whatever they appear next to. Good branding happens on purpose.

## Recovering from Branding Mistakes

One bad pairing can absolutely hurt a brand. Just like one rotten flower sticking out the front of a beautiful bouquet of red roses changes how everyone sees the whole bouquet, one bad pairing changes how everyone sees the brand.

To recover from something like that, you just have to overwhelm customers with the stuff they like until eventually the bad pairing shrinks into irrelevance. We don't try to eliminate it — it happened, and there's nothing we can do about it. What we try to do is overwhelm it with way more of the stuff that the majority of our people actually like.

Kanye, for example, has said some things that people don't like. But he also comes out with products that people love. He made a Super Bowl ad. He sold shoes. He released an album after having some cancel culture stuff around him. And over time, people forgot the bad stuff and associated the good stuff back with him — and they still bought.

## The Product Also Builds the Brand

Almost all of what I've talked about so far has been external to the product. But the product does a lot of the branding after purchase, because customers will have far more experiences with the product than they ever had with your advertisement.

If I buy that amazing Nike t-shirt and there's a hole in the armpit when it comes in, I might say, 'This is the first time I ever bought a Nike product. This product sucks. Therefore, Nike sucks.' And it starts to transfer backwards. On the flip side, brand can influence how people see the product. If the product is good enough — no glaring problems — then the brand can carry you the extra distance to still make it a positive experience for the person.

My pro tip: if I'm going to charge a premium price, I absolutely want to make sure the product is dialed so that I only further reinforce how much they like my brand, rather than let my brand carry me, or at worst have it conflict with the impression I gave them before they bought. Warren Buffett said it well: 'Your premium brand had better be delivering something special, or it's not going to get the business.' The only tweak I'd add is: it's not going to keep the business. You'll get the first purchase. You just won't get the ones after that.

## The Six Changes

Here are the six changes we observed over the last 40-ish months that actually worked. Number one, going from edutainment to education. Number two, from for us to for you. Number three, wide to narrow. Number four, views to revenue. Number five, shorts to longs. Number six, assume more to assume nothing.

## Change 1: Edutainment to Education

I see content in three buckets. On one extreme, you've got entertainment — the entire point of which is to get people to watch. If it accomplishes that, it has succeeded. On the other side, you have education, where the point is to get someone to change what they do. If the video changes their behavior, you've succeeded at educating them. And lastly, you have edutainment, which tries to teach and entertain at the same time.

To be clear: you don't control the audience. You make something, and then education or entertainment occurs. Mr. Beast videos — the vast majority of people are entertained. They watch just to watch. On the other hand, a top educator on YouTube might entertain some enthusiasts around that subject, but mostly he educates students.

I'm going all in on education for three reasons. Number one: not all views are created equal. When we looked at all of our data — book sales, email opt-ins, and applications to become portfolio companies at acquisition.com — we found that entertainment people want more entertainment content, and education people want more education content. These people do not become those people at any appreciable rate that we would build a business around. Number two: I simply like doing it. Number three: the people I like serving — business owners — also like it.

This is where it got confusing for me. I had so many people saying, 'Man, I really like the entertaining content.' But here's where you have to read between the lines of the comments rather than just reading the comments themselves. They weren't necessarily the people I was trying to attract. I tend to do better serving people who want to educate themselves than people who just want to watch or consume for the sake of it.

## Change 2: For Us to For You

Recently a mutual friend came to the headquarters and said, 'Hey, I've been consuming your stuff for years, but recently I just haven't been watching as much.' He said it off-handedly — he wasn't trying to smash my ego. But when he said it, I really took it to heart. He said, 'It just doesn't resonate as much.' This felt like a gut punch because he's literally the person I could help most — a business owner doing $10 million plus a year.

That's when I realized that reading the comments had led me a little bit astray. Some people loved our different styles, but not the people I ultimately wanted to help or am best suited to serve. We were serving the wrong 'who,' and that had very significant downstream impacts on what we made and how we made it.

Tactically, making content for business owners meant five things. Number one, different packaging. Number two, different introductions. Number three, different content substance. Number four, different visuals. Number five, different pre-work.

On packaging: we went from vague to clear. If someone looks at a vague thumbnail, they don't know what they're going to get. If the thumbnail and headline show exactly what the video is about, and then they get exactly what they expected — satisfaction and trust increase dramatically.

On introductions: in education, the goal is to show them that you can do what you promised in the thumbnail. After rewatching my top 35 educational videos, I developed a framework I now call Proof, Promise, Plan. First, prove that you know what you're talking about — give people a reason to believe you. Second, tell them what they're going to get or learn from the video. Third, set the expectations of what will happen next. For example, this presentation opened with data showing I've done what I'm talking about (Proof), a promise that I'd save you $4 million and three and a half years (Promise), and an outline of the six changes (Plan).

On content substance: we went from vlog-style 'be real' content to lists, steps, and stories. When we looked at the educational content that did the best, it had more structure and less razzle-dazzle. On visuals: we went from over-production to effective production — from distracting effects to visualizing data in a way that actually helps understanding. On pre-work: instead of spending four weeks editing after almost no prep, we now spend more time on research and preparation upfront. The videos where we spent the most time on the front end required less editing on the back end and performed better.

## Change 3: Wide to Narrow

Instead of talking about relationships, college, food, and lifestyle, I'm narrowing down to business, business models, business leverage, and selling in a business. Notice that 'business' is in every single one of those topics.

Just as entertainment people want more entertainment, we also found that people who watch relationship content want more relationship content. People who want to learn about college are probably not business owners. People who want to learn about food watch other food videos. But those people were not necessarily the ones who vibe the most with my stuff. When I talk about business, I feel very comfortable. This is what I like doing, and it's what the people I like serving also like — so everybody wins.

## Change 4: Views to Revenue

Before this, we used to track views as our primary metric. But I found a better metric: ad revenue. For those who don't know, ad revenue is not how I make my money — my wife and I exited our first big company for $46.2 million in December of 2021, and since then we've invested in and bought companies that do over $200 million a year. But ad revenue turned out to be an incredibly useful leading indicator.

Ad revenue comes from two things: number of views times revenue per view (called RPM — revenue per mille, or revenue per thousand views). This metric matters because it takes into account the quality of the audience. For me, targeting business owners who have among the highest spending power, a high RPM means I'm getting more of the right people watching.

Looking backwards, the month with the highest RPMs and ad revenue also created the most book sales, the most email opt-ins, and the most business applications. This had nothing to do with how many views we got. In fact, we did an experiment where we spent 90 days making more edutainment and entertainment-style content. Our views went through the roof — two to three times what we're accustomed to. But even with that increase in views, our ad revenue dropped by half. We were getting the wrong people.

## Change 5: Shorts to Longs

For a long time, the prevailing idea was that short-form viewers would then watch long-form content, and those long viewers would translate into customers. But when we looked at the data, it looked more like this: shorts viewers watch more shorts, and long viewers watch more longs. These are different audiences.

For us, long-form content drives far more conversions — book sales, email opt-ins, and business applications. Business-related content unsurprisingly brings more business owners than non-business-related content. So we're putting more emphasis on long-form content. Despite the mega obsession with short-form content over the last few years, we're putting more emphasis on longs overall based on what the data shows.

## Change 6: Assume More to Assume Nothing

'Assume more' meant I made content assuming people already knew me. 'The Alex Hormozi Guide to Haters' — if someone has never seen my face or heard my name, why would they care? 'A Day in the Life of Alex Hormozi' — again, I assumed everyone knows me. These mistakes were costing me new viewers.

If you want your content to bring people who don't know you — which is why many of you make content — then you can't assume they do. Tactically, this means: introduce yourself every time; tell people why they should listen to you every time; fully explain any references you make, because inside jokes make people feel like outsiders, and outsiders don't spend money; and mentally act as though you're always talking to a stranger, because if your content does well, you are.

Warm people like the reminders, and cold people need the introduction. Also, if you've been posting consistently, more people have entered your audience — and they haven't seen that amazing piece of content that everyone from last year loved. Bring back your greatest hits. Let the new people in on the lore.

# Part 3: How I Got Started — The Agency Approach

## Shortcutting the Learning Curve

Building your personal brand can sometimes be overwhelming. I want to teach you a rarely-used method that I used to grow my audience by 5 million people in under 24 months. The crazy thing is that 24 months ago, I had less than 10,000 followers and knew nothing about YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, or Twitter.

Whenever I enter a new space — a new skill, a new platform, anything new — I realize there's a massive skill deficiency I have to overcome as fast as humanly possible. I can either deploy time or deploy money to save myself time. Within the world of organic content, I asked: who can I pay to shortcut my path to where they are?

I had three options. One: celebrities — but their strategies were very personalized and tailored to them, and I didn't think they'd apply to how I run acquisition.com. Two: coaches and gurus — but they might be using something that worked five years ago. Three: advertising agencies — businesses that sell advertising services on your behalf. That's the option I ultimately went with.

## How to Use Agencies the Right Way

Phase one: get a basic agency. For getting into YouTube, my first step was hiring a $300 to $5,000 per month agency. They just got me to commit to making three videos a week — trim the recording, add an intro and a thumbnail. The main objective of that first phase was simply to get the basics down and force me to create content on a regular cadence.

Once I had more context from spending three to six months working with them, I then moved to a more advanced agency that works with top creators, to learn all the more nuanced pieces of the platform.

Here's exactly what I say to the advanced agency: 'I want to do what you do in my business, but I don't know how. I'd like to work with you for six months so I can learn how you do it. Plus, I'll pay extra for you to break down why you make the decisions you do and the steps you take to make them. Then, after I get a good idea of how it all works, I'll start training my team on it. And once they can do it well enough, I'd like to change to a lower-cost consulting agreement. This way, you can still help us if we run into problems. Are you opposed to this?'

When you start that way, you're setting very clear intentions. You're saying, 'I'm going to commit to six months. This is what I want from you.' And you're also saying you'll continue to pay them after the engagement ends — in a much more profitable consulting arrangement — which is what a lot of agencies ultimately want.

## The Agency Trap — And How to Avoid It

Reading directly from '$100 Million Leads,' here's what usually happens with agencies: Step one, they get you excited about all the new leads they'll bring. Step two, you go through an onboarding process that feels valuable and sometimes is. Step three, they assign their best senior rep to your account. Step four, you see some results. Step five, they move your senior rep to their newest customer. Step six, a junior rep starts managing your account. Your results suffer. Step seven, you complain. Step eight, the senior rep comes back once in a while to make you feel better. Step nine, the results still never get back to where they were before, and you eventually cancel. Step ten, you search for another agency and repeat the cycle of insanity.

How do you decide if using an agency is right for you? First off, do you have more money or more time? If you don't have any money, you have to learn it yourself, and that's going to take time. That's part of life. Usually you master one platform, get some success, get a little bit of cash flow, and then you can more quickly pay to learn the others.

# Part 4: How to Build Influence

## The SPCL Framework

We got 32.7 million views on YouTube this month. Here's how. I posted 35,000 pieces of content this year. We did a $100 million book launch — over $100 million in sales in 72 hours.

The issue that most people struggle with is that they're not making enough content, and the types of content they're making are not attracting the types of customers they want. I want to show you a framework I call SPCL — how to build influence, not just get as many views as possible. It's a four-part framework, and I want to break down each component.

## S — Status

Status is someone who controls reinforcers in a given environment. Simply put: if you control the good stuff that people want, you will have status, no matter what it is. The simplest example: if you go to a busy bar and there's a bartender, and you have to get the bartender's attention to get a drink, that person in that moment has status. They control a scarce resource.

Status is not binary. Don't think of it as 'I have status' or 'I don't have status.' Think of it as a continuum. The more of something valuable you control, the more status you have relative to others.

## P — Power

Power comes from something in the behavioral dynamics world called say-do correspondence. If I say something, you do it, and then a good thing occurs, you are more likely to comply with a following request. If someone gives you ten stock picks and they all go up, how much more influential is that person compared to someone who just inherited money? That's the difference between status alone versus status and power combined.

If I had to pick only one of these four elements, I would pick power. Because power compounds. Every time someone follows your direction and gets a good result, your influence over them increases.

## C — Credibility

Credibility is third-party validation. The reason I had Guinness World Records judges on site at my book launch was because I wanted to validate that the books we sold and the revenue we generated were legitimate. A third party that most people respect — a corporation whose entire business is based on trust — verified that those records were broken. That gives credibility. You can observe it with your eyes. It's hard to fake.

## L — Likeness

Likeness is when you see someone and you relate to them — psychologically, you share similar values, you like their vibe, or they literally just look like you. Leila and I could talk about the exact same stuff, but she's going to have more women following her than me. I have an 89% male audience. It's partly the topic, but it's also likeness.

The advice on likeness is simple: just be you. There is zero ROI in trying to be or act in a way that is different from who you are. Most people are essentially running pre-recorded scripts — they adopt archetypes and never think about why they do what they do. What makes you unique is leaning into the nuances that make you you, and actually having a way to defend why you do what you do.

## How SPCL Applies to Your Content

If we know these are the four things that create influence, then in every video, we want to demonstrate that we control a scarce resource (Status); show that if they follow our directions, good things will happen (Power); provide third-party validation (Credibility); and simply be ourselves (Likeness).

I define influence as the likelihood of compliance with a request. The higher your SPCL, the lower the 'roll' you need to get someone to take action — whether that's subscribing, clicking a link, buying a book, or applying to a program.

## Why Live Streaming Is the Future

I had a conversation with a mega-influencer about a soccer game in the UK that featured celebrities and creators from different platforms. When traditional A-listers from movies and TV walked out, there was almost no applause. When short-form-only creators came out, there was a little more. When long-form creators — podcasters and YouTubers — came out, the crowd got way rowdier. But when the live streamers came out, the entire stadium erupted.

Think about how many reinforcing cycles you can have in 30 seconds compared to two hours. If someone watched two one-hour pieces of content from me, versus watching short-form videos — at an average of 15 seconds per short, they'd need to watch 480 shorts to have the same level of exposure. And think about the 'podcast election': Trump's three-hour podcast with Rogan was a hugely influential event. Audiences who were not sure got to spend three hours with a presidential candidate, and it nudged some of them.

The internet always moves toward truth. Traditional celebrity content is curated, polished, and scripted. As you move toward live streaming, it gets rawer. A three-hour podcast is largely unscripted. Streaming is fully live. That approximation of raw reality — of us just hanging out and going through this stuff together — is what unlocks the most influence, as long as you're still including the SPCL elements. That's the marriage. Do SPCL, and do it as many times as you possibly can. That's the idea.

Whether you like him or not, Joe Rogan has tremendous influence. Patrick Bet-David has tremendous influence. Dave Ramsey has tremendous influence. What do they have in common? They're putting out hours of content every single day. Volume negates luck. We just know that one out of ten shorts is going to be an outlier. How do I jam as much as I possibly can into that input-output machine?

Social media is turning into interest media. The algorithm is so good now — it knows what you're talking about, it judges your background, what you're wearing, who you are — and it will display your content to people who have a history of watching similar content. The content is the targeting. So if you want to attract the right avatar, make content for that avatar.

# Part 5: How to Monetize Your Audience

## The Four Ways to Monetize an Audience

In my second company, Prestige Labs, I built up over 5,000 affiliates that generated over $20 million a year in revenue. In Allen, my software company, we built up over 1,000 affiliates. For the '$100 Million Leads' book launch, we got 30,000 affiliates to promote it. I've also invested in companies that I've personally endorsed.

Most people don't know how to monetize an audience. After talking to a lot of creators, celebrities, influencers, and business owners who want to do deals with those people, I've found that many don't share a common framework for thinking through how to make those deals happen. So I want to outline the four ways I see it working.

The four ways to monetize an audience are: number one, affiliates; number two, sponsorships (some people call these endorsements); number three, partnerships, which usually involve equity; and number four, starting your own brand.

## 1. Affiliates

Affiliates is where you get paid after you make sales for a business. This is the fastest way to immediately make money. Most businesses with affiliate programs let you sign up automatically with predetermined terms. It's low risk for both parties — they don't pay you until after you make a sale, and you don't get capped on upside.

From an exit value standpoint, affiliate revenue streams are not typically that valuable on their own. They'll contribute to the sellability of a company, but the value depends largely on how reliable the underlying business is.

## 2. Sponsorships

Sponsorships is where someone pays you first, and then you make the endorsement. Usually this comes in two forms: you advertise to your audience, and/or through whitelisting — where they take your posts and run them as paid ads. I really like whitelisting because it's a genuine win-win: the business pays money to advertise your brand, making you bigger, while they get a higher conversion rate because you already have a warm audience that recognizes you.

Sponsorship deals are harder to come by than affiliate deals because most affiliate companies just have a standard program. Sponsorships tend to be more à la carte depending on the size of the influencer relative to the size of the company.

## 3. Partnerships

Partnerships usually involve equity — either a minority or majority deal depending on the relative sizes of both parties. When thinking through partnership terms, I consider: performance-based equity, time-based vesting, and some amount of equity upfront regardless of performance.

One critical consideration: the association you make with their brand is permanent. As soon as you make that public declaration, you transfer all of that goodwill to them. So if you're going to do a deal like this, my recommendation is that you want to have at least something upfront no matter what.

## 4. Starting Your Own Brand

The fourth option is starting your own brand and product from ground zero. You can either white label — go to someone who already manufactures something and put your logo on it — or do custom formulations and custom products. White label is faster and cheaper, but anyone else can sell something identical to yours, which undermines your pricing power.

When you start your own brand, you begin with 100% equity and start offering deals to other influencers. You're now on the other side of the table. The trade-off is that this takes the most work and capital, but if it succeeds, the upside is the greatest.

# Part 6: Business vs. Entertainment Content — The Data Behind the Six Shifts

## What Changed and Why

I made 35,000 posts over 40 months and grew a 7.8 million-person audience. Three weeks ago, I made six big shifts in my content. I want to show you what we found from those six shifts — with actual data — so you can use this to make more money in your business.

To recap the six shifts: one, edutainment to education; two, for us to for you; three, wide to narrow; four, views to ad revenue; five, shorts to longs; six, assume more to assume nothing.

## The Data

Right off the bat, RPMs — revenue per thousand viewers — went up by 68%. When I talk about deep business concepts like reducing churn, increasing the number of times someone buys, how we scaled a 40-person sales team, how to deconstruct and reconstruct a brand — those are deeper business concepts. And guess what? Business owners watch them. And those eyeballs are more valuable, 68% more valuable.

The next big change: 25% more comments per view. I see that as an engagement metric — way more people saying 'Wow, this was good' or 'I missed this style of content.' Despite having fewer absolute views per long-form video, we're actually getting more long-form views overall across the channel — up almost 30% from our six-week average before that. That's largely because educational content requires far less post-production editing, so we can make more of it.

Our subscriber conversion rate increased by 24.6%. So even though we had slightly fewer absolute views, our actual subscriber growth increased — which was an unexpected outcome for me. We also had 26% more email opt-ins per week since making this change. And the most surprising stat: despite having fewer views, our book sales doubled. Not up 20% — doubled.

## The Takeaways

This is both incredibly surprising and incredibly obvious. Making business owner content to get more business owners — well, of course that's what happened. But I'll say this: I see a lot of educators in this space slowly moving toward broader topics, more entertainment, things they don't necessarily have expertise in, because they chase views. If you can just get your logic hat on, you'll realize it's not about vanity metrics. It's about the bottom line.

Here's what we're doing next. First, volume negates luck — we will keep making as much content as we can with the right constraints on direction. Second, don't get the same scar twice — if we know high-fi production doesn't do as well with business owners, we're not going to do that again unless data proves otherwise. Third, show what only you can show and say what only you can say — do more work than anyone else so you can show and say things no one else can. Fourth, we're still doing wider short-form content, because I see shorts as primarily top-of-funnel brand awareness and facial recognition, which feeds into retargeting later.

The bottom line is simple: if you're an educator, toss out views as your primary metric. Focus on your customer. Make stuff for them. Likes ain't cash. Views ain't cash. Cash is cash. If you're trying to grow your business, make sure you're making content for the people you actually want to reach — not for your ego.
