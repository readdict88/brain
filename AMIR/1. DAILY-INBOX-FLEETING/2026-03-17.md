# The Ultimate Sales Blueprint

---

## Sales Multipliers

Over the last 13 years, I've personally closed 4,000 sales, and these are the six elements of The Ultimate Sales Blueprint. So let's begin with **sales multipliers** — these things, right off the bat, will make you more money.

**First sales multiplier: sell seven days per week.** Now, the reason this is so important is that a lot of business owners want to make more money in their business, a lot of salespeople want to close more sales, but they only choose to work five days a week. The interesting thing is that, depending on the industry, sometimes the off-hours from 5:00 p.m. onwards is when most of your prospects are available. In fitness, where I came from, I closed all my sales from 4:00 until 8:00 p.m. So if you're like, "Well, I only want to work between 9 to 5," guess what? No one's going to buy during that time. You can probably close more sales at the back end of the day, and on Saturdays and Sundays, than you would during the normal 9-to-5. This is especially true in retail and businesses where you sell to consumers who work normal jobs — you have to sell during the times that they're available.

The other piece of selling seven days a week is that there's actual math behind this. There are 52 weeks a year. So if you have 52 weeks per year times two extra days, that's 104 additional days of sales per year — which is a **29% increase** over just selling 5 days a week. So if you want to immediately increase your sales by 29%, sell on weekends.

I know I've tried to sell you on this, but I'm going to give you one more really good reason to sell over the weekend. If you've ever tried to book sales and skip the weekends — so you've got Friday, then Saturday, then Sunday, then Monday, and you skip those middle days — then that Monday gets stacked. You have all these people who are saying, "Oh man, I'm going to show up," so your sales guys are like, "Oh dude, Monday looks big!" But as soon as Monday happens, it's no-show, no-show, no-show, reschedule. And you're like, "What the hell?" Well, you didn't make yourself available at the time when they actually wanted to come, which is Saturday and Sunday. That's when they clicked on the ad, that's when they actually made the booking.

Believe it or not, from a lot of our portfolio companies, we get more people who actually schedule on Saturdays and Sundays for Monday — which means they are available right then to buy, because they've got time. So not only do you sell two more days per week (which is 104 days more per year, a 29% increase in revenue), but you also get higher show-up rates on those days for the people who wanted to schedule then, because that was when it was convenient for them. You get a multiplier effect across the entire business simply by adding two more days a year. So it's a minimum 29% — sometimes more.

---

**Next up: respond to leads in less than one minute.** You may not know the stats on this; some of the more experienced salespeople do. If you can contact a lead within 60 seconds of them showing interest, opting in, or giving you their contact information, you have a **391% increase** in the likelihood that you can close the prospect. That means if you did nothing else but simply put processes in place to contact leads within 60 seconds, you could 4X your business tomorrow.

So then you have to ask: what are all these other things that I'm spending my time on, and do those things have an immediate 4X increase on my business? If the answer is no, then you should probably stop doing those things and do this one obvious thing that can increase your business to such a large degree. Also, 50% of prospects will go with the business that responds to them first — not the one who's the best, not the one they did the most research for — simply the one that responds first.

I remember when Lila was scheduling something for her hair. She called a salon and said, "Hey, are you available right now to take me?" They said, "No, we're appointment only." She hung up, called the next one, same answer, hung up. The third one said, "Yes, we can take you right now." They're the ones who got the business. The first two owners maybe wonder why customers aren't coming — they just didn't make it convenient.

The thing is, people have huge motivation for a tiny period of time. When they knock on the door in that tiny period of time, you want to open it and welcome them in. Now, if you do greater than five minutes, the likelihood that you close them drops by **80%** — which means four out of five people you would have closed now becomes a 20% close rate on those leads. The whole idea that people think lead quality goes down isn't really true — it's completely dependent on how well you work the leads.

A lot of people like to talk about follow-up, and follow-up is important. You've got to make it personal, make it fast, do it multiple times over multiple days — for sure. But one of the most important follow-ups is the first one: how quickly you can show them that you've received their interest and are ready to help them. Customers will make a judgment on how good your business is, how buttoned-up you are, by how quickly you respond.

Just imagine: you opt into something and within 5 seconds your phone rings and someone says, "Hey, saw that you asked for more information about this — how can I help you?" Right there, you're like, "Man, these guys are on top of it." It casts a judgment over the entire business. That first impression is the tip of the spear, and most people are missing out.

*(All the data I cite in this video comes from two sources: one is a Harvard Business Review case study on different sales metrics across massive industries, and the other is my personal experience and data from Allen, a software company we own that handles 4,000+ appointments per day. We could see from click to close how opening up calendars was the single strongest correlate to overall throughput — more than increasing closing percentages by 10% or 20%, because you can't get 400% increases in closing the way you can by putting these sales multipliers in place.)*

---

**Next: time slots.** If you're going to have them book on a scheduler, how do you set up that schedule? A lot of people don't put any time or thought into this. We already covered the seven days a week, but what about the actual day layout itself?

Most people say, "Okay, I'm going to have one-hour time slots." What we found with Allen is that we could increase both the overall schedule rate and show-up rate simply by changing how the times appeared on the calendar. We set it up so we would have **15-minute slots** — meaning people could start in 15-minute increments. We know the next three blocks are used up once someone books, and then somebody else books a different slot.

Some of you might say, "Wait, that means my sales guys are going to have awkward times in between." There are two solutions. One: hire more sales reps. Two: you need something called an **off-the-call SOP** — a standard operating procedure for what your salespeople do when they're not on a call. Most people only address the on-the-call SOP and don't address the off-the-call, which is why their salespeople aren't nearly as productive.

By doing 15-minute time slots, people pick a time that is more precisely convenient for them, and they show up at a higher percentage. There's also a big misconception about wanting to make it look like you're busy by blocking off fake slots — that's dumb. It doesn't do anything except make it less convenient for your customer to book with you. Stop it.

Now, in the off-the-call SOP, what should salespeople be doing? They should be calling existing leads and pulling up appointments. When they have a conversation with a prospect, first they're qualifying them (which you should do anyway), and secondly they say, "Hey, I had an opening later today that just opened up — do you want the slot?" This takes a three-day-out appointment and makes it a same-day appointment. And anyone who's been in sales knows that **same-day appointments have way higher show-up rates** than future appointments.

If they can't move it up for whatever reason, you've still contacted them within just a few minutes of them opting in — so they know you're legit, you've already qualified them, and you've potentially freed up that slot for someone else. By simply changing what salespeople do off the call, you increase sales utilization, booking rates, and show rates — all from one thing.

---

**Next: feed the killers.** Now, this is very controversial, and a lot of bad salespeople are going to be against it, but the killers are going to be nodding their heads.

The best leads should go to the best closers, and the worst leads should go to the worst closers. The people who are the most closable, the most qualified, who have the most money should go to the people with the highest likelihood of closing them for the most money. This minimizes net waste within the business.

Here's a story. There's a guy who lives in my building who does about $3 million a year in personal income. He's the number one sales rep for a multi-billion-dollar timeshare company — and he's won best sales rep of the year, out of 3,000 reps, five out of the last six years. I had dinner with him and asked for his strategy. He told me that when he first won best salesman, he got an hour with the CEO. He used that hour to close the CEO on changing how they allocated leads.

Before, they just round-robined all leads to all salespeople. He said, "You're throwing money down the toilet — you're giving me people no one has a chance of closing, and you're sending really qualified people to dud closers." The CEO tested it at one location. That salesman's income went from $250,000 a year to **$3 million a year**. More importantly, it **5X'd the company** when they rolled it out nationwide.

The other side of this that a lot of people don't think about: if your top sales rep can make $3 million a year, do you know how many people will work for almost nothing just for the opportunity to make that kind of money? Salespeople will come in and work well below normal wages for their skill level just to be top dog. And for those who survive the churn at the bottom — getting the hardest leads and the toughest closes — they've now qualified themselves as closers who are worthy of better leads.

---

**Next: focus on show-up rates.** Most times it's harder to get an entire team to go up by 30% in close rates, but there are usually massive opportunities in show-up rates. One of the things we do is give kudos and shout-outs to the closers who have the highest show-up rates — not just the highest close rates. By calling those people out, we draw attention to how show-up rates are critical to the business.

If I gave you an option — would you rather increase your close rates by 20% or your show-up rates by 20%? A smart business owner would say they're the same thing. Then the follow-up question is: which one is more likely, given where your stats are? If your guys are closing at 35%, getting a 20% boost in that might be very difficult. But simply reminding them to work their leads and follow up on their appointments? That might be very easy. This can increase your overall sales more than sales training sometimes.

So what is the best reminder sequence for people to show up? First, have your automated reminders set up — but don't try to pretend automation is a real person. Everyone knows, and it looks bad. On top of automation, you layer **manual outreach**. Based on data from Allen, the three times that get the highest show-up rates are: **24 hours before**, **morning of**, and **1 hour before**.

At the 24-hour mark, you do something personalized. You want to show that you've done some prep for the prospect. For example: "Hey, I looked over your site, talked to my team about it — looks like we've got three things we can immediately help you with. Very excited to share what we found." It takes five minutes to do, but if it increases your show-up rate by 30%, it's clearly worth it.

Morning of: your prospect wakes up with their day blank, having forgotten everything from the day before. A quick, "Hey, just a reminder — I'm seeing you later this afternoon," gets them back on track. Then one hour before: "Meeting with you in 60 minutes — just making sure."

For these, I prefer voice memos or video messages whenever possible, rather than plain text. People still don't entirely believe it's a real person otherwise, and those richer formats work really well combined with the personalization you're doing at the 24-hour mark.

---

**Next: same-day, next-day availability.** We like to do same-day and next-day only in terms of booking availability. Show-up rates are higher for same-day and next-day. If you constrain people to those windows, you increase show-up rates. The key — and this is where most people mess up — is that if you can't fill all the appointments within same-day/next-day, that means you need to hire more salespeople. When you do that, you'll get more shows, which means you close more sales.

Those are some immediate things you can do to multiply your sales without even having to learn anything more about what to say on the phone or get good at sales itself. These are all the things you do *around* sales that still close far more deals than anything you actually say on the call.

## Sales Training

Now that we've covered sales multipliers, let's talk about **sales training**. I'm going to cover: daily huddles, one-on-ones, game tape review, role playing, and CS game tape weekly reviews.

Most people have no idea what they're doing when it comes to training sales. The vast majority of sales organizations don't even know how to train at all — they simply run churn factories. They bring in 100 people and see who survives. But if you know how to train sales as a business owner, you can compensate below market, because you can take someone who's not good and make them good, rather than relying on people who come in with existing skills. That arbitrage opportunity is yours as a business owner.

**First: daily huddles.** Daily huddles are where we do role-playing. Role-playing is probably the single greatest skill you need to learn in sales. A big caveat: every entrepreneur or sales manager is always afraid of looking bad or not being able to close something in front of their team, so they shy away from role-playing. But you need to set the frame up front: *"No one's perfect — that's why we practice. I'm going to mess up, you're going to catch me, and that's fine."* If you have any ego around this, you will not do it as much, because you'll be afraid of looking stupid in front of your team. Get over that as fast as humanly possible.

When you do role-playing in daily huddles, you actually have to get into it with them and give **fast feedback**. Here's how *not* to do it — and how most people do it: someone goes through the script, gives not-quite-real answers, and then at the end of 30 minutes you say, "Do this differently, do that differently." Two massive problems. One: you have to give one piece of feedback at a time — people can't handle multiple pieces of feedback at once, so it won't work. Two: the trainability of an organism is inversely correlated with how fast you give feedback. The faster you give feedback, the fewer times you have to repeat yourself before they get it.

So when someone does the role-play, the manager or the other person is only working on one thing. They state it at the start: "This is what we're working on." Then they begin the role-play, and as soon as the salesperson does something right *or* wrong, you give real-time feedback. You say "great, keep going" — they can see you nodding, giving a thumbs up, or hear you saying "great." You have three or four different ways to give positive reinforcement in real time. When they mess up, you stop them, say "try it like this," they try it again, you say "great," they try again — and once they do it correctly, you don't just move on. You have them do it two, three, four, five, six times in quick succession so it drills in. From a sales training perspective, you want to be working with them on one thing for sometimes a week or two weeks.

---

**Your best closer usually isn't your best sales manager.** This is a mistake I've made multiple times. The best closers are typically hunter-type people — and there's nothing wrong with that — but they're often not leadership-oriented, patient, or willing to invest in teammates. Most closers, before I explain this to them, want to be sales manager someday. But when you walk them through what the day-to-day of a sales manager actually looks like, they realize all the things they love about the job will stop, replaced by a whole new set of activities.

It's kind of like coaches in sports — it's not always the best players who make the best coaches. Sometimes it's the mediocre or average players who learned how to be decent that make the best coaches. So I set this stage with our sales managers: *"Some of you guys are better than me at sales, and that's fine. Phil Jackson wasn't better than Michael Jordan or Kobe Bryant at basketball, but they still respected his feedback. My goal isn't to out-close you — my goal is to help this team crush it as a team."*

---

**Competition: us versus them, not us versus us.** I used to be really hardcore and cutthroat about internal competition — let the winners win, losers lose. I still believe in competition, but in a different way. I don't want it to be us versus ourselves. I want the competition to be **us versus them** — pick a big external enemy. It doesn't even have to mean you're actually enemies with them. It could be a rival or a friend-of-a-friend, whatever. Say, "Every customer we're not selling, *they're* selling."

The reason the US got to the moon first was because we were trying to beat the Russians. Having a common enemy is a great thing to align the troops toward a big goal. It also quells internal conflict. If everyone's trying to edge each other out, you create a culture that doesn't actually produce the most total sales. You'll definitely have people who thrive in ultra-competitive environments, but you'll lose people who otherwise would have been good closers in a team environment.

---

**Game tape review.** You absolutely need to be recording every single call that happens with a customer, across the company, across the entire life cycle of the customer. If you're not doing that, stop the video and go do it now.

Assuming you are recording all calls, one thing that completely changed my business forever was getting **sales and customer success (CS) on the same call every week** to review the game tape. Why would you review sales game tape with customer success? Because CS can say, "Hey, when you say it *this* way, customers expect *this* — and it's really bad for the business." It elevates both teams to a larger perspective.

If you've ever had conflict where CS is like, "Sales is overpromising," and sales is like, "CS just needs to deliver what we sell" — we've all been there. When you get them both on the same call, that disappears. Sales people have often never seen an onboarding call. They've been selling for you for three or five years and literally have no idea what happens after they run the card. When salespeople see an onboarding call for the first time, they're like, "Oh dude, this is awesome — I'm going to use this in my pitch!" They get new data that arms them to be more knowledgeable and increases their conviction as a closer. And CS starts to understand why customers are coming in with certain expectations. Both sides improve. You create a seamless process between sales and CS, and you stop having those drop-offs and cold feet where you're losing 5–10% of sales from people who close and then back out.

---

**Recording and analyzing game tape.** There's no mysticism in sales. There's a lot of superstition — people add rituals to their sales processes that, long-term, actually erode their ability to sell. The biggest hack I can give you as an individual salesperson is this: there are times when you're **hot** — when you feel like you could close everyone. When you're hot, record everything and rewatch those game tapes, because you aren't hot by luck. You're doing things slightly differently. Your tone is a little different. How you open is a little different. How you transition to the close is a little different.

The big difference between good closers and great closers is that good closers try to *get* hot. Great closers **never get cold.** It's because they've systematized every single thing on their checklist that creates that "hot" closing state. So if you ever do get hot, know that you now have the potential to close at that rate consistently — and then analyze the living hell out of what it looks like: how you say things, where you pause, where your tone shifts, when you ask your questions, how you transition to the sale, how you frame your overcomes, how you're pushing back earlier in the sale to overcome objections before asking for money. Record everything, and when you're hot, analyze it.

---

**For sales managers in a slump:** start drilling everything, sure — but in my experience, reading **testimonials** can be one of the biggest things you can do to increase the conviction of the team. You can get someone's tone correct with drilling, but if they are convicted, they will always have the correct tonality because they genuinely believe in the product and genuinely believe it will help the customer.

There's nothing that replaces a salesperson who is actually convicted. It's way easier to find people who really believe in the product than to find sociopaths who know how to say everything the exact right way without caring at all. So read testimonials — and give kudos to the salesperson who closed the prospect who got the great result.

Imagine: you closed Sandy four weeks ago and had to arm-wrestle her into it. Four weeks later, your manager reads a testimonial from Sandy saying, "I'm so grateful I signed up — it's completely changed my life." And then your manager says, "Tom, I saw that call. I saw you arm-wrestle Sandy to get that deal. Awesome job. If you hadn't stayed in the pocket, she wouldn't have this outcome."

I learned this because my sales team had the highest — almost absurd — close percentages on one specific day of the month when I had my gyms. I wondered what was special about that day. It was **weigh-out day.** We'd run challenges every six weeks, and on Friday or Saturday we'd have weigh-outs — people coming in for 15-minute appointments, taking their after pictures, weighing out. After seeing 20 people lose 20 or 30 pounds in that time period, when someone came in between those weigh-ins and said, "I'm thinking about signing up," our salespeople had absolute conviction — because they had just, hours or minutes before, watched 5, 10, 20 people literally demonstrate results in front of them. That conviction got passed to the prospect.

I just like to remind you: **sales is passing conviction from one person to another.** It's an education process over a bridge of trust. If a prospect knew everything you know about the product, they should buy. If they knew everything you know and still wouldn't buy, and you get them to buy anyway — that means you deceived them. That makes you an unethical salesperson. Don't do that. You want to fill in only the gaps they need filled in order to get them over the bridge to a confident "yes."

## Before the Sale

Now that we've covered sales training, let's talk about what happens *before* the sale — which is absolutely part of the sale.

The first thing a lot of people miss is that **the sale begins the moment the customer engages with your business** — not the moment they get on the phone. How the interaction and communication happens between click and call will massively change your close rates, independent of what you say on the actual call.

For example, if you're a local business and when you call to book the lead you say, "Hey, before we get going — did you hear about us on the local city's best boot camp list, or that we won this award three years in a row? Is that how you heard about us?" — even if they say, "No, I heard about you from a Facebook ad," you still asking that question will influence how they perceive your business. The questions you ask can be educational and frame how you exist in the marketplace to a prospect before you've even begun selling them. Because you *have* begun selling them.

**The sale starts at the click.** The setting script, the ad they see — all of it influences whether they're going to buy. Having continuity and a seamless experience through that entire journey is what will overall increase your closing percentage as a team, even prior to the main sales conversation.

---

**Edify the closer.** Cialdini talks about this in his influence book. If I'm setting a call, I've already edified the business: "Hey, we were on [so-and-so's list]." A lot of people want to get on those lists because they think the list will get them business. No — the list won't get you business, but *telling people* you were on the list will. (And let's be real — usually you just pay a few grand to the magazine to get put on the "best of" list.)

Then when I'm qualifying the prospect I say: *"I'm going to put you on with Shawn. Shawn is unbelievable — he's helped 400 people just like you get the exact thing you said you wanted. He recently helped someone in your exact circumstances achieve X, and someone else achieve Y. You're going to love talking to him. Oh, I think he has an opening this afternoon — let me check... Yep, confirmed. 4:00 p.m. works. Awesome."*

Compare that to: *"Yeah, uh, I got you down for four — one of our team will be reaching out."* Imagine the difference in how that prospect has been pre-framed before they even talk to the closer. And the closer, when they start the call, has to maintain that frame. All of this creates one seamless experience: "We are on top of it. We are a legitimate business. We are someone you want to do business with."

---

**The qualification process: BANT.** IBM did a massive study and came up with the acronym BANT, which stands for **Budget, Authority, Need, and Timing.** For a lead to be qualified for a closer, they need to have: the budget to buy, the authority to make the decision, the need (the problem to solve), and the timing (they want to get started soon). You can qualify using an application before the call, and you'll probably still have to confirm it again on the closing call — but you want to make sure you've covered all four before you ask for money.

---

**One of my favorite outbound scripts** — I couldn't help but share this one. It's simple: *"Hey [business], I'm calling about [competitor]. Give me a call back."*

Think about this. Imagine you have a dry cleaning business — Tom's Dry Cleaners — and you get a voicemail: "Hey, this is Alex. I'm calling in regards to Jones Dry Cleaning. Give me a call back when it's convenient." You are so incredibly curious. Somebody left a message and wants to talk about your competition. The response rate on this approach — whether via email or voicemail — is dramatically higher than a standard sales pitch.

When the person calls back and asks what it's about, you can say, "Oh, it looks like they're kicking your ass," or "Hey, I see they're in the same industry and we've reached out to them as well" — which is a very easy bridge into whatever conversation you want to have.

---

**Scripting for calling local leads.** When someone opts in and I call them, I don't say, "Hey John, this is Alex, I want to see when you want to come in." That's not going to work. Even though they already showed interest, they often don't even know who you are when you're calling.

Here's how I do it: I call and I say, *"John?"* — not "Is this John?" — just "John?" with my tone going up and then a pause. He's going to say, "Yeah... who's this?" And I say, *"It's Alex,"* and then I pause. During that pause I can hear him thinking, "Who is Alex?" — and my tone implies he should know who I am. Then I steamroll into: *"I'm calling because you opted in on Facebook for our promotion — I just wanted to make sure that one, you weren't a crazy person on the internet* (ha), *and also to find a time that worked for you to come in — and obviously to make sure we weren't crazy people either."*

In that little opener I'm giving them three different mental cues about what they opted in for, because people don't remember. If you don't call within one minute, you might be calling two days later — and they're in a completely different universe. They may have opted into 10 things. So you remind them clearly, then move into: *"I've got 2:00 and 4:00 today — which works better for you?"* I give two options and let them pick. I present two times that both work for me, and they choose.

---

**Prep for five minutes before each call.** This one is so unsexy that no one does it — which is exactly why you make so much money when you do. Look up something about the person or their business before the call. You'd be amazed at how much of a genius you look like with just five minutes of prep. It's not because you did that much homework — it's because everyone else is so lazy that any level of effort looks amazing.

On top of that, all the notes from the setter should be in the CRM. When you get on with the prospect you want to say: *"So Jimmy was telling me you're X, Y, Z, and you've done this and this — is it fair to say this is where you're currently at, and this is what you're trying to achieve? Great — well, this sounds like it's going to be a good call."*

You never want a customer to repeat themselves — that's a negative. But you repeating *back* to them what they told you? That's exceptionally positive. It's a binary swing: one hurts the sale, the other helps it. And those notes, after the closer closes, get passed on to customer success. CS then says, *"So you talked to Steve and Jim, you decided on these things — I think we're going to be able to help you with [goal], and the way we're going to do that is [steps 1, 2, 3]. Let's get started."*

By setting this seamless experience — from click to close, from setter to closer to onboarding — you create an amazing process for prospects to go through. And you can solve so much of this and blow prospects away by just preparing for five minutes and reading the notes before the call.

---

**One more thing:** we have a **mini trampoline** in our sales room. I used to take 20–25 in-person sales consults per day, and on my biggest days I'd see 48–50 people one-on-one, every 30 minutes, for 12 hours. I'd start to get exhausted — mentally beaten down by having the same conversation for the 25th or 30th time. I started doing little trampoline jumps between sessions to keep my energy up and my mood up. Here's the thing: **you cannot be in a bad mood and jumping on a trampoline at the same time.** Try it. You literally cannot frown while you're bouncing. If you struggle to keep your energy or tone positive during a long sales day, I highly recommend it.

## The Sale Itself

Now that we've covered what happens before the sale, let's talk about the actual sale — starting with opening the conversation.

You need to, as quickly as possible, **set the agenda** of what's going to happen. I think about this in terms of **Proof, Promise, Plan** — just like we do for YouTube videos and short-form content. Humans are still humans; their attention works the same way. The opener of a sales call dictates how well the entire call goes, just like the opener of a piece of content dictates whether someone keeps watching.

Think of every single thing I'm going to cover in this section as things that increase the likelihood of someone purchasing. I'll share one perspective on how sales work: BF Skinner, the behavioral psychologist, said, "People say you can lead a horse to water but you can't make it drink." He disagreed. He said, "If I dehydrate the horse, bleed it out, salt its mouth, and put water right in front of it on a hot day when it's on the ground — I can veritably guarantee it will drink." I kind of see sales the same way. If you control every variable you possibly can, you can in theory close every single prospect. All of these things are under your control.

I start sales calls the same way I start content: **proof, promise, plan.** It takes only 15–20 seconds. On a sales call it sounds something like: *"John, I'm excited to get going with you on [big promise we're working toward]. We've done this with lots of other people just like you, and here's the process we're going to follow. At the end of the call, we'll see if it makes sense to move forward — and if not, no sweat, I'll still send you away with stuff that can help you with this problem either way."*

By doing that, you've set your proof, promise, and plan right at the beginning. And you've set the agenda — you've told them how this call is going to go. If you don't know how the call is going to go, you don't know what you're doing. Having an agenda also shows that you're a professional, that you've done this before. Especially in B2B — get to the point. Setting the agenda this way actually gives you control of the frame.

---

**Follow the script.** I say: follow the f***ing script. If your team does not follow the script, there is no point in having a script. Script adherence is the first thing you drill. I would rather have 100% of my team saying the script word for word and closing no one — because then if no one closes, I know it's a script problem. If people aren't following the script, you can't change anything because every fix has to be done one by one.

To be clear, the script applies up to the ask. After the ask you have loops and overcomes that may take you down different paths. But the first two-thirds of the sale should be pretty much word for word the same. Their *answers* will change, but the actual scripting remains the same.

There are different levels of following the script. The surface level is actually saying the words. But there's a lot more to communication than that. If I slow down right now, it sounds really important. If I speed up, it sounds like I'm excited and I'm transferring that energy to you. You might increase your pace as you approach the close, and then when you go for the ask, you might slow down and lower your voice.

You can change the meaning of a sentence entirely by changing how you say it. Consider: *"I didn't say he hit his wife."* Say that sentence placing the emphasis on each different word — seven different sentences, all the same words, completely different meanings. So you have: **emphasis** (where you pause), **tone** (going high or low, changing throughout the sentence), and **pacing** (slowing down when something is important, speeding up when something is exciting).

When you're writing the script, include a tone guide. Use formatting cues: underline means slow down, ALL CAPS means emphasize, italics means create space — four different cues with underline, italics, bold, and caps. When you're drilling, the first thing you're going for is saying the right words. Once your team can breathe the script, you start working on *how* to say it.

## The CLOSER Framework

I've used something called the **CLOSER framework** for a long time. It's an acronym people remember, and if people remember it, it changes their behavior. For transactional sales — one- to two-call closes — I usually follow this structure.

**C — Clarify why they're here.** If someone gets on the phone and says, "I just want more information" — no one gets on the phone to get information. People get on the phone because they want to solve a problem. So you clarify: *"Why are you here? What made you reach out to us? What made you opt in?"* These questions remind them of the actions they took to show up on your call. No matter what, every lead has done something to indicate interest — comment on a post, respond to an email, set a call on your calendar. Ask them what made them do that, and tie it to the problem they're trying to solve.

**L — Label them.** This is short but incredibly important. You label them with that problem: *"So just to make sure I'm clear — you're here, you want to get here, and this has been the issue holding you back."* You're establishing the gap between where they are now and where they want to be, and sometimes the obstacle they believe is standing in their way. Once you have that, you've set the table for making a sale. This is where you want to be, this is where you are, and we've got a big dragon — I've got the sword to slay it for you.

**O — Overview past pain.** I call this the **pain cycle**. Talking about people's past pain and what they've done before consistently gets more people to buy. Here's why: deprivation creates motivation. If I'm hungry, I'm motivated to eat. If I'm deprived of sleep, I'm motivated to sleep. If you're deprived of a result you desperately want, you are by definition motivated to end that deprivation.

What we want to do on the sales call is expand that deprivation — bring it to top of mind so that we can increase their motivation to solve it. This is why politicians and pollsters choose certain topics to make salient before elections: people vote based on whatever deprivation is most top of mind. Within sales, this means taking the action to buy.

My little ism for my sales team is: **the pain is the pitch.** We want to quantify how much they've spent in time and effort so far, what the impact has been on their status within their community, and how much it's costing them every single day to *not* solve this. This creates urgency and increases the likelihood they act.

In weight loss, for example, the pain cycle sounded like: *"What have you done so far to try and lose weight?"* Whatever they said — Weight Watchers, a gym, whatever — it didn't matter, because my three-pillar pitch could always identify what was good about what they tried and what was missing. If someone said they did Weight Watchers: *"Great — so they gave you nutrition and accountability, but I'm guessing you kind of bounced back? You lost the weight but gained it back because you lost muscle without the fitness component. That's why it didn't work. All we have to do is fix that one thing, and we can get you success."*

If someone said they already had a personal trainer, I once said, "He's obviously not doing a good job." She looked at me sideways. I said, "You're here." She said, "Fair." And I closed the sale.

Every business has a **three-pillar pitch** buried inside it. For the fitness example, the three pillars were Nutrition, Fitness, and Accountability. For a marketing company selling leads to mortgage brokers: Timely, Qualified, and Exclusive. When someone said they'd bought leads from Zillow before: *"Oh, they weren't exclusive to you, right? You were competing with 20 other guys. You had good quality leads — the right budget, interest, authority — but they weren't exclusive. If you'd had exclusive leads, do you think you'd have made money?"* "Oh my God, absolutely." Done.

Humans tend to think in threes. Even if you have a seven-step process, chunk up until you have three big categories. People remember it, and it keeps you out of the weeds.

**S — Sell the vacation.** We want to talk about Maui. We don't want to talk about the plane flight, TSA, taking your shoes off, the bumpy ride, the food, the person next to you who smells bad. We want to talk about what it will be like when they've accomplished the transformation — the vacation itself.

For the three-pillar pitch, you make the statement of what they need (e.g., "You need exclusivity"), and then you give a metaphor. A metaphor compares something they don't understand (your product) with something they do understand. This is, fundamentally, what teaching is.

Two of my favorites for explaining accountability: **The Toothbrush Close** — "When you were a kid, did you hate brushing your teeth? Do you brush your teeth now? Of course. What happened? In the beginning you had external accountability — your parent kept reminding you. Over time, that positive reinforcement created internal motivation to stick with it. What we're doing is being that external motivation you need in the beginning to create the habit." And **The Amazing Race Close** — a couple kept winning The Amazing Race until they had to be disqualified. Their advantage? They'd find a local person, put him in the back seat, pay him for the day, and say, "Help us get to this place." Because they had a local, they beat everyone else who was doing it on their own. *"I've been to where you're going. I've been in the backseat with a hundred people just like you. I can be your backseat driver — it'll give you an unfair advantage."*

A three-pillar pitch shouldn't be longer than two minutes. You're talking about the prospect, not the product. If I want to break into a new organization, I'll spend 90% of my time understanding the pains of the prospect. Once you know the pains, you can sell them the bridge to where they want to go. Most sales teams spend all their time training people on the product and not the prospect — then they sound ill-informed and ask questions that anyone who knew more about the prospect would already know the answer to.

**E — Explain away their concerns.** This is objection handling — the heart of closing. We'll get into this in the next section.

**R — Reinforce the decision.** This happens after you've gotten payment. Instead of a handoff, you do a **handshake** with customer success. I use the concept of **BAMFAM** — Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Every interaction with a customer, they should know exactly when the next time they'll be meeting and with whom. And that next person should know all of the conversation up to that point: the goals, aspirations, fears, and pains, so they can speak in those terms. When the CS person gets on: *"John, we're going to help you take care of [this specific thing]. The way we're going to do that is [steps]. It's been a pleasure talking to you — Sarah is going to take care of you."* And Sarah opens with a proof, promise, plan personalized to exactly what John said he wanted.

## Closing and Objection Handling

Before I get into tactics, I want to preface with this: **train sales back to front.** The first thing you teach a closer is how to collect payment and process it — because if they don't know how to do that, they have a 0% chance of closing. Then you teach them how to ask for payment. A lot of people start at the top of the script, but my preference is to start at the bottom.

**Closing begins once someone says no.** Just because they said no once doesn't mean they're saying no forever. If every person immediately said yes, you wouldn't really need sales — just record a video, put it in front of them, and put a buy button. Under the assumption that you're not a perfect marketer, people come in with more questions or concerns, and that's when closers step up.

**Before I get into all the things we can go for, here's the one time you shut up:** if someone says yes — shut up and close the sale. Do not sell yourself out of a sale. A lot of beginners get excited when the person says yes and start bringing up more details. Those new details increase the likelihood of turning a yes back into a maybe or a no. The moment you have agreement, shut your mouth, ask for payment information, and close.

And if someone says anything you aren't prepared for, it's because you are underprepared. **Prep equals smooth.** In one breath, you should know exactly what you're going to say back to someone for any objection. If someone says, "I have to think about it," or "Give me a day or two," or "I have to talk to my spouse" — you should already be inhaling to begin the next part of your close.

That said, you don't want it to sound like a pre-recorded objection overcome. That's why I use the **AAA method**: acknowledge, associate, ask. We'll go deeper on this in a moment.

---

**Rapid-fire series of questions when someone says no:**

First off — *"What's your main concern?"* If it's early in the sale and they say, "I need to think about it," you say, "Cool — what's your main concern?" And you're right back in the sale.

If you're further into the sale: *"What are you afraid of having happen?"* This gets to the heart of the issue. What you hear after that is usually the real obstacle — which you can actually overcome — rather than "I need to think about it," which you can't.

Other great questions: *"What are the variables you're using to make this decision?"* And my two favorites: *"What would it take for this to be a yes?"* and *"What makes this a no?"* — those questions help isolate whatever is actually standing between the prospect and a decision.

If they can't tell you what would make it a yes, you can say, *"Can I share some of the decision-making variables I often hear when I ask that question?"* And then you walk them through it. If they say, "I just don't know," you acknowledge and associate: *"Totally understandable. Believe it or not, you're a totally normal person — anyone making a decision this fast would be crazy. It sounds like you're just nervous about making the decision, which makes you just like everyone who ends up buying and getting amazing results. Because it shows you care. And if you care, you'll follow through."* Then you ask the next question — *"Ready to rock and roll?"*

---

**The AAA System (Acknowledge, Associate, Ask)** — I realized only years into teaching sales that this is what separated my top salespeople from my mediocre ones. The top performers did it naturally; I never caught it until I stopped to examine why.

**Acknowledge:** When someone says something, you repeat the key phrase back to them. *"Oh, so it sounds like..."* This does two things: it buys you time to think about what you're going to say next, and it builds rapport because it signals you're actively listening.

**Associate:** You associate what they said with something positive — a generic: *"That's a smart question,"* or *"Totally understandable,"* — or more powerfully, you associate with someone who used the product and got amazing results, or with an authority in the business. *"That's an amazing question. I actually just talked to our senior engineer earlier today and he told me something about this — can I share it with you?"* This lets you deliver a harsh truth without attacking the prospect's character. You're not saying it to *them* — you're telling them what you told *someone else*, and they can connect the dots.

**Ask:** After acknowledging and associating, you ask the next question and move the sale forward.

Here's a law: **he who asks the most times gets the most sales.** The reason this is so important is that asking the wrong way can lose you the sale — if they get into a bad mood, they don't buy. So how do you reconcile asking a lot with maintaining rapport? The AAA system is the bridge. It gives you unlimited shots on goal. You stay in the saddle. You can tell when someone's demeanor closes off — you've lost the sale. But by acknowledging what they say and associating with something positive, you can deliver harsher overcomes that move the sale forward two or three steps.

**Childlike curiosity.** The point is that you want to understand, not win. If someone doesn't immediately buy, your first instinct should not be to hard-close them. It should be: *"Huh — that's interesting. I wouldn't have expected that. Can you tell me more?"* That one head tilt and "huh" — it's not combative, it's curious. It buys you time, it maintains rapport, and it's not competitive. You become rich by making them right, because they believe nothing about what you say and everything about what they say. That's why salespeople ask questions — we need them to say it, not us.

**The person asking is the person closing.** There's a reason that in an interrogation, the person across the table says, "I'm asking the questions here." The questions guide the conversation; the answers do not. You want to be the spirit guide whose questions lead the prospect to the natural conclusion. Sales is a teaching process — same conditions, new behavior. We want them to now have a new behavior (buying), which we've motivated by pointing out the deprivation in their lives and presenting our solution as the bridge.

---

**All-purpose closes** are the 80/20 of closes — they work in pretty much all situations. Here are a few:

*"Do you think the reason you're on the phone today is because you've struggled to make this decision in the past?"* — This works no matter what the objection is. Whatever they say, just add: *"Do you think that's the reason you haven't succeeded yet?"* Whatever their thing is — it's the reason. They're on the phone because they haven't succeeded yet, and that's your advantage.

**The 1-to-10 close:** *"On a scale from 1 to 10, 10 being 'let's do this' and 1 being 'never talk to me again' — where are we?"* Doesn't matter what they say; you follow with, *"What would make it a 10?"* If they say a 6, also ask, *"Why not a 1?"* — and they'll tell you all the reasons they're already sold on you, without you having to say it yourself.

**Details are death traps.** The reason deals are lost in the details is not what you think. It's because you don't get to pick whether your answers are correct. If someone asks, "How many days a week are you open?" — that's a blind answer. You're rolling the dice that whatever you say is the right answer. So instead, you ask a question about their question: *"How many days were you looking for?"* or *"What days do you need it to be open?"* Based on their answer, you can frame your response in a way that increases your likelihood of closing.

Just like a lawyer never asks a question they don't already know the answer to, you as a salesperson should never answer questions you don't already know the answer to. If someone really presses on something, use what I call the **mechanic close**: *"I totally get it — but it would be like asking a mechanic what's wrong with your car before bringing it in. We've got to look under the hood before we can give you an honest answer. And honestly, if anyone gives you an answer before doing that — run, because they're just trying to sell you, not help you."* That gives you the moral high ground, and you're out of the details.

When someone asks a detail question, your first response should be to ask why they care about it: *"It sounds like the main reason you're asking is that you just want to make sure you're safe / you fully understand / you're making an informed decision. If I could make sure of that, would you feel okay?"* Then zoom out to the bigger picture.

A detail objection is usually a **preference objection** — they want your results, their way. The overcome: *"If you change the variables, you change the outcome. I can tell you that if you do things our way, we'll get you this outcome. And from what you've told me, you've tried a lot of other ways and they haven't been working. If you're willing to give this a shot, these are the numbers we can hit. Does that sound fair?"*

**The best close / worst close (zoom out):** When you're lost in the details, zoom out: *"Big picture — best case: we get you [result]. Worst case: you get six weeks of free service and your money back if you hate us. You've got two options — both are risk-free. You can do nothing, and that's guaranteed to keep you exactly where you are. Or you can take a shot here, also risk-free, with a chance of getting what you want. One is guaranteed not to help. The other has a chance. Both are risk-free. Which one do you want?"*

**Guarantees** hugely amplify the best-case/worst-case close. And if you don't have a guarantee, the **anti-guarantee close** works: *"Honestly, if you need a guarantee, this probably isn't for you. We can't control what you do. Would it be fair to guarantee work that we have no input in? It really comes down to this: the more control you have, the more we can guarantee. I can give you the guide. I can tell you that hundreds of people have succeeded. But I can't eat the food for you. Does that sound reasonable?"*

---

**Handling specific objections:**

**Cheap competitor:** *"If ours was the same price as theirs, which would you choose?"* They'll say yours. *"Why?"* They'll tell you all the things that make you better — without you having to say it. Then: *"Right. And that's why we're more. Because we're better, and you know it. That's why you're here."* And by the way — a price comparison question is usually a buying signal. You're close to the close.

**ID close (in person):** Rather than asking for payment, ask for their ID first. When someone takes their wallet out, a lot of objections disappear — "I forgot my card" doesn't work when their wallet's right there. There's also natural chitchat ("Oh, you're from...") that builds rapport and fills out the form correctly. Then: *"I'll trade you for whichever card you want to use"* — and you gesture toward the wallet. On Zoom: *"Can you put your ID next to the camera? We associate the right account profile with the right payment — just want to make sure everything's set up correctly."*

**Spouse / decision maker:** If it's a product that materially affects a household or business, try to get both decision-makers on the call upfront. If you can't, here's the process:

- *"What would happen if they said no?"* — One out of three times they say, "I'd do it anyway." Done. Close it.
- If they wouldn't do it without permission: *"What do you think would be their main concern?"* — They'll tell you their own objection through the mouth of this hypothetical person. Now you can close the actual obstacle.
- **Past agreements close:** *"Do they know you're struggling with this right now? Have you bought things like this before without needing to ask? If they already don't approve of your current situation, why would they not approve of you solving the problem they already don't like?"*
- **Support not permission:** *"I think you're looking for permission when what you really need is support. If you don't get this outcome, who are you going to blame? Is it fair to the partnership that you don't move forward because you relied on someone else's approval? Five years from now, if you're in the same place — who's responsible?"*

The decision maker objection is the hardest to overcome because the person is literally saying "I can't make a decision." Your job is to figure out which of these is true: (1) they actually have authority and are using the spouse as a smokescreen — say something and they'll reveal it, (2) they actually have authority but are afraid to tell you their real objection, (3) they don't have authority but you can show their partner has implicitly agreed based on past behavior, or (4) they truly don't have authority, and you work toward "support not permission." Work through these in order.

**"Give me some time / let me come back in a couple of quarters":** *"Awesome — so what are you going to be doing instead?"* They tell you. *"Do you think that's going to get you better returns than what we're talking about?"* If they say yes: *"Why?"* — and you can either reframe your value or validate that maybe they should pursue that first. If they say no: *"Then why are you prioritizing it?"* Then: *"Here's a question I asked someone earlier today and they said it was enlightening — do you think you'll ever *not* be busy again? And if you can't succeed when you're busy, then when you do succeed and get busy again, you'll fall off. So if you want this to be permanent, we have to make it work during all seasons. Does that sound fair?"*

**"Let me watch your free stuff first":** *"Absolutely — you should do that. And one of two things is going to happen: either you succeed, in which case you'll run into another problem I can help you solve, or you don't succeed, in which case I can help you solve the original problem. Either way leads to you doing this — so let's get you started."*

**Not having a guarantee:** Use the anti-guarantee close (as described above). Or say: *"This is really for people who are serious about doing it. Are you serious about doing it? Then let's go — I can't eat the food for you, but I can give you everything else."*

**Price objections:** Nothing is ever too expensive — it's just not worth it enough. If I was giving you a Ferrari for $5,000, you would find the money. So when someone says it costs too much, what they're really saying is they don't see the value. Zero in on value, not price. Secondarily: the issue isn't resources, it's **resourcefulness.** *"If you truly believed this was going to change your life, you'd find a way to make it work."*

**Never change price to close a sale.** My favorite response when someone asks for a discount: *"We could do it for more."* It sounds silly, but it has a near-perfect batting average for me. It re-anchors above the original price, they fear losing the current price, and most of the time they say, "No, no, I'm good — let's do it." You can change *terms* (payment plans, feature downsells) — those are very different from discounts given for no reason. If they see you change price once, they'll believe every price you have is negotiable — and they'll tell everyone.

## Sales Mindset

I hate the term "mindset" because it's vague. So I'm going to give you concrete behaviors instead.

**The person who cares most about the prospect wins the sale.** If they care more about themselves than you care about them, they win. Keep the human first. If this is no longer a good idea for them, tell them. There's nothing more powerful than learning to turn away prospects — it will increase your overall closing percentage because you'll know you're in this to help people. If you try to close everybody, it will wear on you. Either you become a sociopath, or your sales go down. Qualify your prospects. Never lie. If you keep the person at the center of the sale, you'll maintain rapport, and you'll close more sales because they'll feel that you genuinely care.

**Volume negates luck.** Sales is a numbers game. There's a relationship between volume and skill: the more you do, the better you get; the better you get, the more you do. Force-feed yourself through tons of volume early so you get through the terrible period as fast as possible. A master in any domain has more ways to reward themselves — when you're a master salesperson, you *enjoy* pulling a sale out of the depths, overcoming details, navigating the red zone. When you're a beginner, you're freaked out and paranoid. Follow the Kobe Bryant approach: if everyone works out once a day, you work out three times a day. Find the best closer on your team and do double what they're doing.

The skill curve goes like this: in the beginning, you learn not to suck — which is important — but the best returns in life come at the very end of mastery. People quit during the hard early phase and miss out on all the gains. You have to approach sales as a skill. The best salespeople love selling — just like the best editors love editing. When those best salespeople first started, they sucked. But they had enough reward cycles and forced enough volume that they started liking it. Once you like it, the reward cycle takes over.

**Sales résumés don't matter.** Twice in my career, our sales teams weren't growing at the rate needed to support demand. Both times I discovered that they were screening heavily based on résumés — and that was a mistake. Especially in transactional selling (setting or one- to two-call closing), you can quickly identify the right skills without looking at past experience. I prefer **group interviews.** Invite everyone onto a call and do one- to two-minute conversations with each person. The star salespeople will speak up, communicate clearly, and — the really good ones — will also encourage the nervous people on the call. They'll show team orientation. You can spot it immediately, without a résumé.

**Attitude vs. aptitude.** The real truth: **always hire for the lowest skill deficiency.** What are the skills they're lacking, and how long will it take to train them? You want the fastest ramp-up and highest ROI. "Hire for attitude" really means: there are many skills bundled under terms like "kindness" or "work ethic" — and if someone has zero of those sub-skills, that's 20 things to train. Versus learning to call leads, work leads, and follow a script — which might be two to three weeks. Training someone to be kind takes longer. But this flips at higher levels, where a very niche technical expertise is so rare that it might be easier to teach culture fit than the skill itself. Universal truth: always hire for the smallest skill deficiency.

**Compensation attracts great salespeople but doesn't retain them or change their behavior.** Compensation equals recruiting. It does not equal performance (with one exception: if you compensate based on cash collected, you will generally get more cash collected). What actually changes behavior is fast feedback loops — just like why you're addicted to your phone. The reward cycle is fast and frequent. In-the-moment Slack shout-outs work better than quarterly bonuses at changing daily behavior. Pro tip: create a Zapier trigger from your CRM when a sale is processed and have *that* post to Slack — not self-reported closes.

**Sales and marketing are one continuum.** I've always run them under one roof, under one "revenue generation" meeting — sometimes with a Chief Revenue Officer overseeing all acquisition. Since doing this, there's been essentially no conflict between sales and marketing in my companies. They understand they're on the same team, working together to close as many deals as possible.

Think of it this way: there's a certain amount of information required to get someone to buy. If they were perfectly knowledgeable, they'd buy on their own (that's basically what Tesla and Carvana have figured out). But there's always a larger pool of people who need more selling to fill the remaining gaps. Think of sales as **personalized marketing**: marketing might give a prospect pieces 1, 2, and 3 of what they need to know; sales gives them 4, 5, and 6. For the next prospect, they already have 1, 3, and 5, so sales covers 2, 4, and 6. Sales personalizes the information required to get *this specific person* to make a decision.

**Belief and trust are continuums, not binaries.** It's not that the prospect did or didn't trust you — it's that they didn't trust you *enough*. When you see it that way, you can identify all the small things that increase or decrease belief and trust, and stack them up. And here's a counterintuitive truth: the trust and belief required to make a *big* purchase is often smaller than what's required for a small one — because a bigger purchase requires a longer runway (more information, more touchpoints) to get the plane airborne. If you're getting a lot of three-, four-, or five-call closes, it usually means you're not doing enough education and warming up before the first call.

**Say less.** Roughly two-thirds of the talk time should be from the prospect, not you. That's because they should be answering your questions — not the other way around. Master interviewers let silence sit. People like to talk about themselves, and they'll expand if you don't immediately respond. Try this: instead of jabbing back, just let them keep talking. The more they talk, the more rapport you build — because people enjoy talking about themselves. They'll think you're amazing without you ever saying anything brilliant.

**"Don't be cute."** A serial private equity CEO told me this, and it stuck. He said a lot of CEOs try to run the backyard football equivalent of a super-secret double-reverse fake play — and they fumble and lose 50 yards. *"But you know what plays always work? Two fat guys in the middle, run to the right."* Don't be cute. The best salespeople say the script, follow up quickly, keep rapport, ask good questions, listen, and allow the prospect to close themselves. What separates good salespeople from great salespeople is that **great salespeople never don't do the basics.** They always prepare for each call. They always do the warm handoffs. They always refer to notes instead of making the customer repeat themselves. They always set the agenda with proof, promise, plan. Doing the fundamentals over and over again, never forgetting any of the steps — that's what creates mastery.

**No for now does not equal no forever.** Think about it like dating: if someone says they're not available and then, six months later, they're free — that original "no" doesn't mean you shouldn't reach out again. Some of the obstacles they gave you were legitimate: they were in the middle of a merger, or they had a higher-priority opportunity. You should not be ashamed of circling back every three to six months with a quick pulse check. Sending a fun meme or a casual line can make you a lot of money — because so many salespeople are afraid to follow up. If you genuinely believe you can help this qualified prospect, just circle back when the timing is better.

**Be a trash man; yellow equals gold.** If you're new in sales, ask for all the trash leads — the ones nobody else wants. There's usually more than the company can handle. Yes, you could call those leads a waste of time. Or you could call them **advanced-level bosses** — the hardest Prospects to close, which makes them the best training ground. If you can close a homeless person, a person with no job and no money, that means you've gotten really good at the skill. And then when the easy ones come along, they feel effortless.

**Objections do not mean no.** If someone says, "Wow, that's expensive" — that's not necessarily an objection. You could say, "Yeah, totally" — and keep going. There have been many times in your life you've thought something was expensive and still bought it. An experienced salesperson who starts immediately overcoming price at "wow, that's expensive" is wasting bullets on zombies that aren't there. Unless someone says they *don't want to buy*, don't assume they don't want to buy.

**We don't negotiate with terrorists — never change price to close a sale.** People talk. If customers know you discount for some people, everyone will expect a discount. You can change *terms* (payment plans, feature downsells). That's very different from discounts given with no rationale. My favorite response when someone asks for a lower price: *"We could do it for more."* Counter-anchors above the original price, re-instills the fear of paying more, and almost always results in them saying, "No, no — let's just go with this."

**People want to buy.** When you're browsing online, you want the thing. People like buying. The only reason they don't is because they have a history of being punished for buying decisions in the past. Your job is to help them justify making the decision. They want an excuse to buy — give it to them. Closers ask hard questions because they genuinely care. You want to be **kind, not nice.** Nice people don't want to offend anyone. Kind people genuinely want to help — which sometimes means calling someone on their BS, confronting hard questions before asking for money, and staying in the pocket of the sale.

If you come from the perspective of "I want the best thing for you," you will always be on their side of the table. As far as they're concerned, your incentives will always be aligned. And you will be able to ask the questions that are actually going to change their lives.
