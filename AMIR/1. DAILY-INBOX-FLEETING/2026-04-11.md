# **Real World Advice for Your 20s and 30s**

_Alex Hormozi — Transcript_

# Introduction

Give me the next 30 minutes and I'll help you make the most of your 20s and your 30s. I made my first million when I was 26. A decade later, my portfolio companies generated over $250 million in aggregate revenue last year. I'm 36 now. In this video, I'm going to give you the real-world advice that I wish someone had told me much earlier.

# 1. Take Asymmetric Bets

If you're under 30, you have no reason not to go 100% all-in on your goals. The downside is nothing and the upside is everything. Think of life as handing you lottery tickets. You scratch one off and if you don't win, what did you lose? Just the ticket. When you have nothing to lose, you have nothing to lose — and everything to gain. The objective when you have as little as possible is to take as many shots as possible, because your downside is zero. This is the only time in your career where you can take more shots than anyone else.

The reason people don't take shots — myself included when I was younger — is the belief that other people, who we don't even talk to anymore, would somehow judge us as a failure. But here's the thing: you never actually start from scratch ever again. Because every time after the first time you start over, you start with experience. You still always net a positive. You either get the big win, or — more likely — you learn something, and that learning compounds.

I had a misunderstanding of how success worked. I thought success was like jumping across a chasm — nothing, nothing, nothing, then suddenly something works. But more accurately, it's like laying bricks on a path. You install a brick. You fall. You fix it. You start again. You fall again. You fix that. You keep going. And then, all of a sudden, you've made it all the way across. That's what the 'overnight success' experience actually looks like, which is why so many people describe it as 'it didn't happen at all, and then it happened all at once.' The actual outcome of getting a system to work is binary, but the process of building that system is very linear.

I had nine failed businesses before I had one that really worked. And six of those nine, I was running at the same time. You have way more failures in you than you think. Life is an infinite game — the point of business is not to get into business, but to stay in business. And the only thing you have to do to stay in business is to choose to keep fighting. Once you realize that, you realize you become a success the moment you decide you are. When you're in your 20s, what you lack in experience, you have to make up for in effort and hours. Believing otherwise leads to a life of well-deserved mediocrity.

# 2. Don't Follow Your Passion

Passion is vague. It's very difficult to say, 'I like this more than that.' But what you can say is, 'I'm better at this than that.' Passion usually comes from competence — it doesn't create competence. We usually like things we get good at, rather than getting good at things we already like.

Enjoying something doesn't even guarantee that you're good at it or that you'll be able to make money from it. My dad used to tell me, 'If I were following my passion, I'd be a bartender at a ski slope. But if you can just make money, the rest of your life you can do whatever you want.' I think that was very practical advice, and I'm grateful for it.

Getting good at anything requires slogging through a period where you're probably not going to be passionate about it, because it's boring, monotonous, and repetitive. There's a difference between saying 'I like ping pong' and hitting 500 forehands and 500 backhands every morning and night. You might enjoy playing the game — but getting good at the game are two very different things.

Your passions will also change as you age. The things I was passionate about at 20 are not what I'm passionate about now. And I'm sure what I'm passionate about in my late 40s will be different again. This idea of finding 'the one thing' that will be perfect forever puts a ton of pressure on that thing. If it's not going to be perfect forever, why not be practical now so we can give ourselves options later? Fundamentally, all we're looking for are things you're already good at that people already spend money on — because that creates the highest likelihood of you building something you can exchange in the marketplace.

# 3. Get Good at One Thing

Think about the equal opposite of this: how would you get someone to stay poor? Get them to keep starting new things rather than getting good at one thing. Do you remember that guy who got rich from dropshipping, day trading, buying crypto, and wholesaling all at the same time? Yeah, me neither. You have to focus.

It's arrogant to think you can do multiple things at once and beat someone who does one thing with all their effort. Pick one thing, go all-in, and become the person who's hard to beat.

I said I had nine failed businesses. Here's the part most people don't know: six of those were running at the same time. I like to tell people I was a CEO with multiple companies — the way I am now. But now it's very different, because I actually have CEOs who run those businesses, not me. You can only truly drive one thing. I had a chiropractor agency, a dental agency, a gym turnaround business, and five gym locations of my own. It was too much. I was stressed out of my mind, spread thin, generating revenue but not making any money. The only thing it fed was my ego, not my bank account.

The turnaround happened when Leila came into my life and said, 'You're clearly really good at this one thing. Why don't we just cut everything else?' And that's exactly what I did. It was the hardest period of my life — I had to shut down all the other businesses and end partnerships with people who were relying on me. That was not fun. But I had to cut off the life I didn't want to have the life I wanted.

If you want to impress people who are poor, talk about how many things you do. If you want to impress people who are rich, talk about how good you are at one thing. Your ability to demonstrate excellence is the only thing that someone ahead of you will look at. You only fool the people who have no idea — and there are a lot of people who have no idea.

This is also where impostor syndrome starts creeping in. And the cure is simple: stop lying. When I say lying, I mean presenting something in a way you know will be perceived differently than reality. What you want to do instead is state the facts and tell the truth, period. And if those facts are not compelling enough to get you the status you want, change the facts — not by lying, but by working so hard that the truth becomes compelling. The hustle bros I knew in my 20s who were always posturing? A lot of them faded. They're still jumping from thing to thing, barely able to afford their house.

It's a choice: do you want to pretend to be successful, or do you want to be successful? Being successful means doing things that today don't look like success — they look like a very long string of failures. You will be rewarded far more in life for your determination than for your intelligence. But determination, in the short term, looks like a long string of failures until it works.

# 4. Stop Networking — For Now

There are seasons of life. In the beginning, you're in the exploration phase — tasting lots of things, figuring out what you're good at or have a proclivity toward. Later, you move into the exploitation phase — where you go all-in. You go broad in the beginning so you can figure out what to go deep on. The broad period is never the period where you're going to make money.

I'd say from when I started until I really graduated from the exploration phase was about five years. In the beginning, you have to say yes to everything because you're scared and inexperienced. You learn to say yes a little, then more, then more — until you're overwhelmed. The only way out of that 'yes trap' is to start practicing saying no. That means changing what you do. Welcome to entrepreneurship.

Here's a very tactical example. If you're debating between going to a networking event or lunch with someone who wants to 'synergize' and 'pick your brain' versus actually working — choose the work. Because when the work works out, those people will still be there to take your call. But if you never do the work, no amount of networking will get the work done for you. And here's the negative bonus: if you lose whatever success you have because you got distracted, they're not going to take your call in the future. But if you win — that door will be open. Not only that door, but a hundred other doors.

During this season: losers congregate, winners isolate. If everyone was supposed to win, they would have made the podium bigger. The top of the mountain isn't supposed to fit a lot of people. If all of this seems antithetical to what you've heard or what the people around you are doing, ask yourself: do those people have the lives you want? A great way to make a mistake is to ask someone who has no idea about your idea.

When you make the transition from exploration to exploitation, you go from many loose connections to a very few tight ones. I tend to be very transactional, but I think we all are — some people just admit it. Everyone does some version of the math: 'With this person in my life, do I get more benefits than costs?' If you want to raise the standard of your life, you have to raise the standard of the people you surround yourself with. The fastest way to change your life is to change who you're friends with. That reference group is ultimately what you'll compare yourself to, and if you raise that bar, you'll naturally feel the gap and work to close it.

The only way people let you into circles you don't yet deserve to be in is work ethic. There is no other way. It's the universal currency of respect across cultures, time periods, and generations. An old man will respect a young man who hustles and will always give time to someone they believe will execute. That was my secret, and it still is. I network with people above my weight class because they know I'll do the work. A lot of people are happy to help you. What they're not happy to do is waste their time helping someone who won't help themselves.

I've shed friends at every season of my life, and my close circle has always been very small. I think of life in seasons. You'll have friends for a season, and maybe you keep them for life — but maybe you don't. Giving yourself permission to say, 'That was that season, and they were amazing for the objectives I had then, and my objectives have changed' — that's freeing. It doesn't mean you owe each other anything less. It just means you can reinvest your time in people who will help you get where you're trying to go next.

# 5. Money Loves Speed

By the time you have all the information, the opportunity is gone. Sometimes you just have to make your bet. It's almost always faster to make a decision, make a mistake, and correct that mistake than it is to painstakingly deliberate on the decision. If you know how long it would take you to fix something if you're wrong, just make your best bet — because you can always fix it later.

If it takes you five minutes to decide and ten minutes to fix something if you're wrong, don't take two hours to decide. Make the decision in five minutes. If you're right, you're moving forward five minutes later. If you're wrong, you're moving forward fifteen minutes later. Either way, it's less than two hours. You're just not going to know everything. You have to get comfortable with uncertainty. Uncertainty is the job. The only way to pursue a certain dream is to pursue an old dream — one that's already been achieved. And if you go after something that's already happened, you're chasing something that won't happen again in the same way.

The heaviest things in life aren't iron or gold. They're unmade decisions. They take up all of your attention and brainpower. Right now, there are probably a handful of decisions you know you need to make that you're just not making. If you want to feel yourself accelerate, just decide. So few decisions in life are actually irreversible. Even the ones people treat as irreversible. Should I buy this car? You could buy it and flip it. The real cost of the decision is the delta between what you'd buy it for and what you could sell it for — and meanwhile, you're losing all the time you spent waiting to decide.

People slow down far more at the decision point than at the doing point. Doing usually takes very little time. It's the deciding that bogs people down. They feel like they're trudging through mud because they hit these walls and there's no action — just thinking and planning. There are so many variables you will never know. You have to get comfortable with the idea that you should just test and learn. You'll learn far faster that way than by deliberating.

You can move through life at seven times the rate of other people simply by changing when you say you're going to make a decision — from the end of the week to the end of the day. If you want to move even faster, decide at the end of the hour. And if you want to move faster than that — decide now. When my team says, 'Let's kick that to the next meeting' or 'Let's go offline on that' — I say no. Let's confront it. Which vendor are we going with? Which website design do we like? What headline are we using? Just pick. All of this comes down to bets. If you want a future you want, you have to get comfortable with risk.

# 6. Pay Off Your Debt — Especially Ignorance Debt

There are many kinds of debt. There's financial debt, obviously. But the biggest and most expensive debt is ignorance debt — the cost of what you do not know but should. I still pay off ignorance debt every day. It's the debt in skills and knowledge that I should have to move faster toward where I'm going, but I don't yet have. The only way to acquire it is to fail until you get it. And if you don't fail, you'll never get it.

I am willing to pay in looking bad, in money lost, in time lost, in people judging me — to make bets. An expert is simply someone who has made all the mistakes you can make in a very narrow field. You have to learn how to lose before you can learn how to win. The first rule of losing is: you didn't lose — you learned. You paid down the most expensive debt.

So the question is: if you know you have to pay this debt off, why aren't you? I think it's because you don't realize how expensive not paying it off really is. Know the price of inaction. Do you think the reason you're in the situation you're in right now is because you've struggled to decide in the past? How many times have you run that cycle — thought through a decision, gotten all the way to the edge, and then chose not to act? Is the life you have the life you want as a result of those actions?

If you genuinely believe you're going to make this bet sooner or later, you might as well do it sooner so you can start enjoying the benefits now. You're going to enjoy those benefits either way. Why delay them? The real reason you delay is because you want to delay the potential costs of failure. But the voice in your head telling you not to act — whose voice is that? It might not even be yours.

I almost didn't sell Gym Launch because I thought $46 million wasn't enough to impress a specific colleague of mine. Real talk. I was very hesitant to sell the company, and the main reason was that I thought this one person would think it wasn't that impressive. When I actually listened to that voice and asked, 'Whose voice is this? What are they saying? And is this the person I want to give control over my entire life?' — once I saw it clearly, I thought, 'That's ridiculous.' These voices are usually subconscious. You don't notice them unless you listen for them and name them. Am I really going to let John be the reason I don't get married to this girl? Is Joe really going to be the reason I don't raise my prices? Is Sarah really the reason I won't sell this thing? Ridiculous. But people do it every day. Myself included.

# 7. Solve Bigger Problems

If you want to make more money in your 20s and 30s, solve bigger problems. If you want to make a million dollars, you have to be willing to endure a million dollars' worth of pain. If you want to make $10 million, you have to endure $10 million worth of pain. The nature of the pain changes over time — in the beginning, it's not knowing what you're doing; later, it's people judging you; later still, it's lawsuits. But not knowing is a constant. Trying to solve for not knowing is silly because it never goes away. You have to make uncertainty your friend.

Stephen Schwarzman, the founder of Blackstone, put it this way: big goals and small goals are usually just about as hard. A common observation among venture capitalists is that running a really successful local restaurant might require 80-hour weeks, managing dozens of people, and dealing with constant problems. Building a billion-dollar company might require those same things. Both are hard. So if it's going to be hard regardless, you might as well go big.

This reframe has been very helpful for me, because one of my core beliefs is that suffering is a constant. If we're growing, I'm in pain. If we're plateaued, I'm in pain. If we're declining, I'm in pain. So I'm pretty much always in pain. Thinking that something is wrong with pain misses the point entirely. It's a constant — it shouldn't be a reason to do something or not do something, because it's just always there.

Competition for big goals is actually much thinner. People believe the big goals are so unrealistic that they don't shoot for them — which means fewer people to compete against. It is probably harder in some ways to have a really successful local restaurant than to build a large business, simply because so many more people are trying to do the former.

Most problems are solvable, which means most things are knowable. The reason you probably haven't gotten where you want to go is that you haven't actually started trying. You're going to have to be obsessive about it. It is normal to be different from your normal friends if you want extraordinary outcomes. If we do the same things as the people around us, it would make no sense to expect a different result. Living differently is a green flag, not a red one.

# 8. Get Over Yourself

Everyone's childhood was difficult. Get over it. And if you want to win the award for hardest childhood — congratulations, I'll give it to you. You win. Feel better? No one cares about what happened to you then. Only about what you can make happen now. The only person satisfied with whatever your reason is, is you. Everyone else calls that reason an excuse. Do you want to get to the end of your life having everyone say, 'He wasn't successful, but he had a lot of really good reasons'? Wouldn't it be more powerful to say, 'He had every reason not to succeed — and he succeeded anyway'?

Those reasons actually give you more fuel. No one cares when the silver-spoon kid succeeds. The underdog story is the one that inspires people. All of the reasons you tell yourself you shouldn't do it are sometimes the best reasons why you should.

If you had disadvantages, I agree — it's harder to be successful if this thing happened to you, or if you were born into this circumstance. But despite the disadvantage, you only have one choice: what are you going to do? You can take action anyway and become proof to other people like you that it's possible to overcome. Or you can do what the vast majority do — protect the ego and blame and complain. You can do whatever you want. But only one of those decisions will make you better. Losers define themselves by what has happened to them. Winners define themselves by what they can make happen despite what's happened to them. Where one person sees an excuse, another sees an origin story. Every champion, every hero in every story ever told, has a hard past. Your hard past just makes you like every other superhero.

Joe Rogan said this, and I love it: every one of us can wake up today like it's the first day of a video game. You got transported into this body. You've got a wife, some kids, a job you hate, a business you don't love, or that nagging feeling that you can do more but aren't. You have all this stuff around you — and now you can choose to make today your spawn point. Everything behind you stays behind you. Whatever frame of mind you need to be in to take action, find it. Then do it.

# 9. Obsession Is the Price of Entry

Obsession is the ticket of entry. And the thing is — it's not really obsession. It's just trying. No one else tries. Normal people call what I consider sane behavior 'obsessed.' But from my perspective, sane people consider everyone else insane, because they do nothing and waste their lives. We have to be comfortable reasoning from first principles: I am only going to live here for not that long, and I am only going to have a certain number of hours to do the things I want to do. Why on earth would I not do them because of what some other person thinks about how I'm living my life?

Almost all insults and judgments can be boiled down to: 'You do not live your life in a way that I would prefer.' And the response is: 'You're right. And that's why you live yours and I live mine.' When people hate on you, it usually means they're jealous and believe you are somehow undeserving of some part of your life. They might think life has unjustly given you benefits they believe they rightfully deserve. They might be right. But life isn't fair.

People who obsess about work-life balance are typically mediocre at both. Obsessed people apply their obsession to everything and just call it life. I get criticized all the time — 'Alex, you don't have any hobbies.' I don't want any. They're saying, 'You live your life in a way I would not prefer.' And that's why they're not in my position. Why would I sacrifice the things I prefer to do in order to satisfy someone else's definition of work-life balance, which I don't accept?

Work-life balance is a wonderful goal for other people. It's just not compatible with being the best. You've probably heard 'work smart, not hard.' That only works when you're competing against people who aren't smart. If you compete against other smart people, the only thing you'll have left is to work hard. If you work smart but not hard, you will be beaten by someone who works smart and hard. I'd rather be that person.

# 10. Work Hard and Smart

If you extrapolate what a normal career looks like — maybe someone makes $1 to $2 million over 40-plus years — there's a certain total amount of work that must be done to generate income. I'd rather cram that 40 years of work into four, and then have 36 years to do whatever I want with far more resources than I would have otherwise.

Leila and I had this conversation early on — I think on our first date. She said, 'I just want to help people.' I said, 'Do you think it's possible to help people and make money?' She said yes. I said, 'Do you think you could help more people if you made more money?' She said yes. In that conversation she shifted from wanting to just give everything away from a place of scarce resources to understanding that if you want to help a lot of people, you have to learn the game. And to win the game, you probably have to outwork a lot of people — and you have to be called names by people who live lives you don't want.

# 11. Accept Boredom

If you want to be creative, you must first learn to be bored. If you want to achieve a goal, you'll either have to accept boredom or pain — and the bigger the goal, the more of both you'll get.

Think about a marathon. Where do people gather? At the start and at the finish. Where does the winning happen? Alone, in the middle, where no one is watching. No one cheers for you for not drinking for a day, for skipping the club, for not overeating for one night. No single workout or meal is ever impressive on its own.

The reason so few people achieve success is that consistency never looks impressive in the moment — only at the end. Consistency is very hard to visualize because you can only ever see someone do something once. The only way to witness consistency is to be around consistent people for a long time. That's why so few people are able to truly internalize it. And yet consistency is the most important success trait, because it is very hard to lose if you show up every day. And showing up every day is boring.

# 12. Accept Sacrifice and Make the Trade

We have to give up some things to get others. All we ever have are trade-offs. You have everything in your life right now that you're going to have to trade for the things you want but don't have. Everyone trades. Even when you had nothing, you had things — your time, your attention, your energy — and you traded those for what you wanted.

There's no perfect way to live your 20s or your 30s. You either live them up and become an underskilled 30 or 40-year-old, or you work them up and become an underlived 30 or 40-year-old. You just have to figure out which you'd rather be and accept the trade-offs. There are no do-overs. And that's okay.

No human will ever get more than 24 hours per day. Everything you spend time on that is not pursuing your goal, you are implicitly deciding is more important than your goal. If you look at every hour honestly — the Netflix, the doom-scrolling, the hanging around doing nothing — that is what your behavior says is actually more important to you. Is it really a sacrifice to give that up for what you want? A year from now, you won't look back on today and think, 'Man, those clips I watched really changed things for me.' Research shows people remember about 11% of short-form content just 24 hours later.

Seneca said it: it's not that we don't have enough time — it's that we waste the time we have. Is it really a sacrifice, or are you finally just saying, 'I believe this is my priority, and therefore I'm going to trade out everything that isn't helping me pursue it for things that will'? If you like what you're going to get, why would you not make the trade?
