# **Nine Things Brilliant Salespeople Do Differently**

_Alex Hormozi — Transcript_

# Introduction

There are nine things that brilliant salespeople do differently, and in this video I'm going to share the stuff that I've learned from building multiple nine-figure sales teams, scaling teams from zero to 40 guys, zero to 60 guys, and personally closing over 4,000 sales myself.

If you boil it down, a salesperson has three jobs to do. They have to maximize the number of opportunities they have. They have to convert the highest percentage of those opportunities. And — this is the important part — they have to do it consistently for a very long period of time. The nine things they do differently will fall elegantly into each of these three buckets.

# Bucket 1: Maximizing Opportunities

## 1. They Work the Most Hours

Right off the top: I have never seen a salesperson who does the most in a company also have the lowest amount of hours worked. Every company I've owned, and every company I've looked at, the person who works the most hours is the one who sells the most deals. Maximizing opportunities comes in a number of different forms. Number one: they have the most total hours available per day, because you should be available when the prospect is available. That means sometimes working long hours, sometimes working weekends. Businesses also pay rent on Sundays, which means you can make sales on Sundays.

My software company, ALAN, scheduled 4,000-plus appointments per day across a number of different industries. We got to see all the way from click to close which companies were selling the most people. We looked at all the different data — times of day, number of days per week, speed between text responses, number of characters in a text response, all of these different variables — because I had two data analysts trying to figure out how to maximize throughput for any business. The single greatest lever on throughput across all companies was the total number of available time slots. Availability was the strongest predictor of total sales.

A simple example: when Leila and I were traveling, she wanted to get her nails done. She pulled up Yelp, found the one with the best reviews, called them up, and they said, 'Sorry, we don't do same-day appointments — we can book you in two days from now.' She said, 'Nope,' and called the next one. Same thing. Called the third one. They said, 'Yeah, we can take you right now.' And boom — she went in. Both of those first two business owners lost money. And the next time she comes to that city, she'll probably go back to the one she went to, as long as they did a good job. Trying to be egotistical about availability rather than accommodating customers just means you lose money.

## 2. They Pull Up Calls

The next part of maximizing opportunities is getting as much of their day as humanly possible filled with the best leads. The best salespeople pull up calls. If a call is booked for Thursday and today is Monday, and you have an open time slot, you grab that Thursday appointment and drag it into today — because same-day appointments always have higher show-up rates. If you pull the call up, the prospect is even more interested, you can close at a higher rate, and you free up that Thursday slot for someone else.

A related tactic: if you look at your calendar and someone hasn't confirmed their upcoming appointment, the best salespeople reach out and say, 'Hey, I didn't see that you confirmed — why don't we do the same time tomorrow?' They push it out by one day and try to fill that original slot with someone who is more responsive. This increases the total number of calls per day. The perfect day for a salesperson is back-to-back calls with the best, hottest leads available.

I learned that pull-up tactic from one of our portfolio companies in solar sales. One rep was massively outperforming everyone else. What he was doing: every time a new appointment booked, he immediately called to qualify the lead. That checked two boxes. First, he could assess whether the person was qualified to buy. If they were, he had two decision paths: if they had time right then, he went straight into the sale — because he was already on the phone with them. If they said no, the stated reason for his call was that he had an opening later that day and was trying to fill it. So he either turned that moment into a sale, or he filled another slot later in the day with a sale. This guy was selling four times more units than the second-best rep on the team. They adopted it company-wide. Then I adopted it across my entire portfolio.

Here's why it's so powerful: not only does the salesman have two opportunities to sell — now or later that day — he also opens up the slot later in the week for another customer who can't make it today. What we found with the software was that if you have fewer available time slots, people may still book with you, but they're not booking at the most convenient time for them. You'll see schedule rates stay flat, but show rates drop, because it's not an ideal time for them. A prospect who is stressed about something happening right after the call is very different from a prospect who has Sunday afternoon completely free, zero urgency to hang up, and is in full receiving mode.

## 3. They Have Two SOPs: On-Call and Off-Call

One of the things the best salespeople have — and something that companies should provide but often don't — is two SOPs, or standard operating procedures. There's an on-call SOP: what you say when you're talking to prospects. And there's an off-call SOP: what you do in between calls. The vast majority of companies don't have an off-call SOP, and they're losing enormous amounts of money because of it. They just say, 'Work the leads in the meantime,' when the total output of a salesman is so closely correlated with the number of opportunities they generate during their off-call time.

The best salespeople have two separate checklists. As soon as they're off the call, they flip to the off-call checklist: following up with this person, dragging this lead forward, canceling this no-show appointment and filling it with someone else. They stay in high activity at all times.

## 4. They Always Book a Meeting from a Meeting (BAMFAM)

I learned this acronym from my friend Sharran Srivatsaa: BAMFAM — Book A Meeting From A Meeting. The best salespeople never finish a call with a prospect without knowing when the next conversation is going to happen. Prospects should never fall into no-man's land, never fall through the cracks with a vague 'let's circle back.'

If you get to the end of a call and someone says, 'Yeah, let's connect offline and find another time,' you address it right then. Because if a time objection is still unresolved, you can actually resolve it immediately — you both have your calendars open, you can both make the decision. If someone says, 'I'm not sure,' you address the concern: 'Isn't this a problem for you? How much are you losing every day by not implementing this solution? Why isn't this something we'd be doing sooner?' If you just let them off the call without booking the next step, you lose more sales. Always book a meeting from the meeting.

## 5. They Don't Take Rejection Personally, and They Ask for Referrals

Three more things the best salespeople do. First: they don't take rejection personally. They reach out in more volume than mediocre salespeople. On any CRM, you can see how many outreach attempts and follow-up attempts a salesperson makes between calls, and it correlates directly with the number of calls they take. When someone says something mean before they've even scheduled a call, the best salespeople don't spiral — they just keep going, because they know it's a numbers game.

Second: the best salespeople keep a 'kill list' — a separate, visible list of their highest-value prospects that they give extra attention to every day, in addition to tracking them in the CRM. It's an everyday list: 'I've got to get back to these two guys. These are high-value deals — I'm putting extra attention here.'

Third: they create their own opportunities through referrals. At the end of every call or at the right moment, instead of asking, 'Do you know anybody?' — which is a yes/no question — they say, 'Who do you know who is as awesome as you?' That forces the prospect to answer with a name, not a yes or no. It's also a compliment built into the question: 'You're amazing — who else do you know who's like you, who would love to do this with you, who could benefit from this?' It's a compliment sandwich around a referral request.

If they say no one, you can say, 'Why do you hate me so much? I can only assume you hate us, because you don't want anyone else to know about this amazing thing — and you probably hate your friends, because you want them to suffer.' And if it's because they don't believe in you, then let's talk about that. Otherwise, why aren't we getting your friends in here? Because I can promise you, you'll be far more successful if you have people you know who are in it with you.

I had a rep at one of our portfolio companies who was 50% above the number-two guy — and he was brand new. We called him and asked what he was doing differently. He was multiplying his leads. At the end of every call, he was simply asking who else would want to come. 'Who else would you like to bring?' He was increasing his sales by 50%, because referral close rates are 80–90%. If you take four calls, close one, ask for referrals on all four, and only one gives you a referral — but that referral closes — you've gone from one sale out of four to two out of five. Your cost to acquire a customer just dropped by a third. As advertising becomes more expensive, the businesses and salespeople who do this will be the ones who win.

# Bucket 2: Converting the Highest Percentage

## 6. They Prepare — and They Take Notes

You would be amazed at how much of a genius you sound like if you just do five minutes of research before a call. My rule of thumb: if it's something you do regularly, spend about 10% of the meeting time on prep. So for a 60-minute call, that's six minutes of prep. For a 2-hour call, that's 12 minutes. For something you do rarely — like a quarterly meeting — it reverses: six hours of prep for a one-hour presentation.

Salespeople do the same call over and over, but the one thing that changes each time is the specific prospect they're speaking with. So even five seconds of research makes a difference. If you're picking someone up at their house and you look up what their dad does — and then when you meet him you say, 'Hey, Mr. So-and-so, I see you work at X Engineering — I noticed they just had a press release, do you have any involvement in that?' — that person will respect you immediately. Not because of how you look, but because you did them the honor of taking some time to learn about them. You approximated friendship. You approximated a relationship. Think about what it takes to close someone: know, like, and trust. In the first 30 seconds, you've already built know and trust — just from five minutes of prep.

When I was selling to gym owners, I had no reputation yet. I found their social profiles, pieced together a profile of who they were, and when I got on the phone I'd say things like, 'I looked at your floor layout from the photos you have online — I think if you moved this piece of equipment over here, you could potentially double your usable square footage and fit in more sessions.' I came in with the problem identified and a solution ready, because I thought about it before the call. I didn't want to waste their time figuring things out live. The small guys win through preparation. The big guys have their reputation. The small guys have their prep.

The best salespeople also take notes on every call. This prepares them for the next one. If you hop on a follow-up call and say, 'So you've got your daughters Sarah and Jessica at school in Colorado, and you've got X, Y, Z happening in the business — is that right? Just reviewing my notes from last time.' They'll think: 'I don't have to retell my whole story. This person actually listened. When they make a recommendation, I can trust it's within the context of my situation.' This switches you from being a salesperson to being a consultant — from someone trying to close to someone trying to help.

## 7. They Listen More Than They Talk

This is what people get wrong all the time. Moms in high school tell their talkative kids, 'You should go into sales!' No — that's what bad salespeople look like. The best salespeople don't come off as salespeople at all. They just close. People don't feel sold — they feel like they bought. Think about going to a restaurant: when the server asks if you want dessert, that's a sale. If they do it awkwardly, you feel sold and you're annoyed. If they do it naturally, you think, 'I'm so glad they mentioned that.' You never felt sold — you felt like you bought. The reputation of salespeople is terrible only because you remember the bad ones.

We did a big study across our portfolio using AI call-analysis software to measure talk time versus listen time. The best salespeople listen twice as much as they talk.

The rule I use: the person who's answering the questions is the one being interrogated. You don't want to be interrogated. Whenever you answer a question, you give the other person something to attack. So you want them to be the one answering — you want things to pick apart and work with. I want to be like smoke — they can't catch anything I say. If someone asks, 'What makes you better than your competition?' I say, 'Well, what things are you looking for?' Right back to them. They tell me. Then I say, 'Why are those things important to you?' Right back again. I can do this all day. Because they believe nothing that you say. They believe everything that they say. I need them to be the one saying it's a good idea — not me.

When I started selling weight loss, I realized I had an agenda of what I wanted to tell people. But I didn't have 15 minutes to re-educate someone on their last 10 years of experience. What I needed to do in that moment was get them to buy. So I started asking what they'd done before, what they liked, what they didn't like — rather than picking apart their past decisions. I learned quickly: just let them tell me what they liked, and then say 'we're like that, and we're not like that.' Educate the prospect so that they come to their own conclusion. The only gaps you fill are hard information — and only after you already know it's the right answer. Just like lawyers don't ask questions on cross-examination that they don't already know the answers to, you should never ask for the sale unless you already know they're going to buy.

## 8. They Breathe the Script and Kill Zombies Up Front

The best salespeople breathe the script — they can say it without thinking, just like breathing. You can't truly listen to a prospect if part of your brain is trying to remember what comes next. You need to know the script so thoroughly that you can be 100% present and listening. You need to know it like the Bible, or like something you've had memorized for years.

The first time I really understood this was watching an exceptional sales trainer drilling a rep on just the opening 30 seconds of the script. He would make one correction, then have the rep repeat it three times in a row correctly, and then move on. Yep, yep, yep — pause harder on that word. Do it again. Nice. Do it again. Nice. One more time. Nice. Keep going.

Word concision also matters enormously. You don't want to be the kind of salesperson who takes 100 words to say something you could say in five. It also makes your calls longer than they need to be. A mediocre salesperson does the script, closes the deal, and happens to cough right before they ask for the close. The next call, they add the cough. The call after that, they close again, so they add a second question before the close. Over time, the script keeps expanding because of random positive reinforcement — even though none of that extra stuff was responsible for the close. That person might have been buying no matter what. Keep the script as concise as possible. The less time you're on the phone after making the sale, the more time you have to fill your calendar and close more deals.

For sales managers, the three tactics are: rehearse the script every morning, run role plays on one specific part of the script at a time, and give feedback on one thing at a time. Nobody can improve multiple things simultaneously. A basketball coach might see 100 things wrong with your game, but they'll focus on one at a time until you clean it all up. Only then do they move to the next.

## Kill Zombies Up Front (Diffuse the Bomb)

Number ten — and yes, I said nine, but I have way more than nine. We call this 'killing zombies up front,' or 'diffusing the bomb.' An obstacle occurs before you've mentioned the price. An objection occurs after. It's much easier to address something before you ask for money than after. Once you've mentioned the price, the clock is ticking — the bomb is sensitive, the zombie is right on top of you.

Common obstacles that need to be killed up front: 'I need my spouse on this decision,' 'I won't have time to implement this,' 'There are elements of this program that might not be a fit for me.' Address all of those before you ever mention the price, so that by the time you go for the close, everything has already been cleared.

The obstacles and objections people raise will almost always fall into three categories. First, circumstances — time, money, things about the specific program or implementation. These are easy to cast blame to; they don't require much thinking. Second, other people — my spouse, my business partner, my employees, someone else has decision-making authority. If someone raises a person, it means they would buy but this other person is in the way — which actually means you're one layer closer to the real decision-maker. Third, self — they doubt their own ability to follow through, or they're not sure the approach is right for them.

The best salespeople have key stories and metaphors to break the specific belief around each obstacle. For example, with the spouse objection: 'Let me tell you about Susan. She was just like you. She had the same issue — she didn't do anything without her husband's consent. She walked out. Her husband said no. I ran into her a few years later. She was still in the exact same spot, and when I asked why, she said, 'My husband never lets me do anything.' And so what I think you're actually asking for is permission, when what you really need is support.' Then you play it forward: 'If we don't change direction, five years from now you'll have gained another 25 pounds. Let's play that out. Is that where you want to be?' One out of three times when I said that, the person said, 'Well, then I'd do it anyway,' and we signed right there. The other two times, I could get into the real conversation.

For time or money, the key line is: resourcefulness, not resources. 'Is this a seasonal thing? Because if it's a seasonal thing, that means you're going to be less busy at some point. But do you want this to be something that lasts? Because if you do, you should learn it while you're busy — because that's when you need the support the most. And when the busy period is over, it's easy coasting. And when you get busy again, you already know how to stick with it.'

One of my favorite ways to get to the hard question is: 'What are you afraid of happening?' Then follow it up with: 'Let's just play it out.' People often don't even want to look at what's actually going to happen. Force them to. I had a sale once where a woman said, 'I follow Dave Ramsey, I have cash envelopes, and if I give you this envelope, that's my grocery money for the week.' I asked what she was afraid of. She paused. I said, 'Is it that you won't be able to afford food?' She said yes. I said, 'But you're trying to lose weight — worst case scenario, you can't afford food and you lose some weight. Win-win.' I said it with a good tone, so she laughed. Then I got serious: 'Have you ever bought something before that you weren't 100% sure you could afford? And where are you now? You made it work. You'll make it work this time too. The question is: do you want to look better in every outfit you already own, in six weeks?' Best case, worst case. I've probably closed more sales using the best-case/worst-case framing than any other single technique I have.

## 9. They Ask for the Sale Again

If you do not ask for the sale, you will not get it. That is a guarantee. And if you ask again after addressing a concern, you'll increase the likelihood they buy. There's a right way and a wrong way to do this. Simply asking again after a 'no' — bad. But if someone says no, you ask why, you resolve the why, then you ask again — that's the process. It's called looping.

You resolve the concern and you ask again. You can do this literally unlimited times, as long as you keep resolving the actual concern and then asking. If someone says, 'I can't make Tuesdays,' you say, 'No worries — if we could do it on Thursday, would that work for you?' They say yes. You say, 'Awesome, so you want to move forward?' And you ask again. All you're doing is resolving the concern.

Leila will tell you that on Sundays — when I usually had fewer calls — or on the last appointment of the day, she always knew I would close that sale, because I would just keep going. I had unlimited time, so I would ask again and again and again. A partner of mine once said, 'You're like a pit bull on someone's ankle — you just will not let them leave.' I'd say, 'Of course, because they need it. They came in because they're trying to solve a problem. We just have to figure out how to get there.'

Usually what you're unpacking is layers and layers of excuses built up from every time they've been unsuccessful in the past. They came in because they want to solve the problem. But when confronted with the actual decision, they get scared again because it triggers those past traumas — the programs that didn't work, the feeling of failure — and they start associating that failure with you in the moment. Your job is to say, 'Hey, calm down — we're just going to have you eat some chicken and walk a little bit. It's going to be okay.'

The best closers operate from a frame of helping the prospect. Whoever cares the most about the prospect wins the sale — including the prospect themselves. If you care more about their well-being than they do, you will win. You have more context than they do — you know more about the product, you've seen a hundred people in exactly their situation walk out the door and come back a year later in the same spot or worse. You're not going to let their limiting beliefs get in the way. If you keep the human at the front of your mind, you'll never come across as pushy — because they can hear the intention in your voice.

# Bucket 3: Doing It Consistently

## Meta-Skills — Sustaining Performance

The things most salespeople struggle with are trait issues. They're on, then they're off. They get motivated, then demotivated, then motivated again. If you feel this way, it's a skill deficiency — you just don't know how to sustain performance for a long period of time. The difference between champions and everyone else is that champions can sustain performance. Being consistent is a skill. Maintaining a high level of enthusiasm — both for the role and in the moment on each call — is a skill. You whistle while you work. You learn to like the parts you don't love, so you can do the thing you really love, which is selling.

## Kill for Sport

The best salespeople kill for sport. I heard this analogy from Mike Ary, and I love it: there are three types of salespeople — dogs, horses, and tigers. Dogs: you have to feed them, rub their belly every day, and the moment you stop, they die. Horses: they'll gallop as long as you keep whipping them, but the moment you stop, they drop to a trot. Tigers: a tiger can eat a full meal, but if a bunny crosses its line of vision, it'll kill the bunny for sport. You want tigers.

The best salespeople I've ever met love sales. They love the thrill of the close, the hunt. When I was selling weight loss, sometimes people would come in who were clearly not wealthy. I had two ways to take it: brush through the call to get to the next one, or treat it as free practice. What's crazy is how many times I judged someone by how they looked and turned out to be completely wrong. A lot of them told me they appreciated that I treated them well, and I'd say, 'Of course — this is a business, and your money spends the same.'

You either practice the skill or you close. Either way, you win. It's like lifting weights — if your warm-up reps look different from your working reps, you're not actually practicing. You're wasting opportunities to sharpen the sword. The best salespeople treat every single call like it matters, because it does.

Jacob started with me when he was 15 and has been with me for seven years. When he was at the bottom of the totem pole, he'd get handed all the least-qualified leads. He said, 'I'll be the garbage man. I'll take anybody's leads. I just want to practice.' He took more reps than everyone else — and guess what? He got better than everyone else. He treated every call as a gift. Someone gave you the time to practice a high-stakes skill, the business paid for the lead, and you still have a chance of actually closing. People who refuse to take 'unqualified' leads are refusing free practice with real stakes. That's ridiculous.

## Track Data

The best salespeople are meticulous about the data they track. The skill level of any person in any endeavor can be judged by the quantity and quality of the metrics they track. If someone says, 'I'm good at sales,' I say, 'Cool — tell me about the metrics you track.' If they say, 'Oh, just close rate,' that tells me a lot. Close rate is an outcome — it gives you no leading indicators. What percentage of leads are you booking? What percentage of scheduled calls are actually showing up? Of shows, what percentage are you making offers to? Of offers, what percentage are you closing? What's your average number of calls to close? What's your average cash collected upfront?

Shout this from the rooftops: if you can get your schedule rate up by 20%, it's just as valuable as getting your close rate up by 20%. The best salespeople control all the variables in the funnel that are actually under their control.

The dogs are like, 'I only want these time slots, spoon-fed to me, and if you don't pat me on the head, I'm going to quit.' Tigers are like, 'I want to be available to close whenever because I love the hunt. I'd do this for free — I'm just happy I get paid.'

## Give the Best Closers the Best Leads

Pro tip for running a sales team: give the best closers the best leads. To do that, you first have to score your leads — which means you have to track data. Once you know which characteristics predict the highest close rates, you match those leads to your best people.

I knew a guy who was the top sales rep at a billion-dollar-plus timeshare company, making $3 million a year in commissions. He had 3,000 reps on his team. When he won the annual sales competition, he got an hour with the CEO. And he spent that hour saying: 'Give me the best leads instead of wasting them on new guys who don't know what they're doing. I will make you so much money.' The CEO experimented with it. That rep 5x'd his income. They rolled it out nationwide and 5x'd the business.

There's a massive opportunity in every business to give the best closers the best leads. The best guys make more and more money, which creates a survivorship bias that attracts high-quality salespeople to the organization. Even though entry-level people make less to start, the upside is so visible — like the lottery — that people show up willing to earn less at first, knowing they can reach the top.

The other benefit: you train your new reps on the hardest, least-qualified leads. You lose the least money as a business, and they develop extraordinary skill. When they eventually get a qualified, high-intent prospect, it feels easy — because they've been closing people with no money, no credit, in the middle of a divorce, with a complicated situation. When someone like that finally gets a clean, qualified lead, they think, 'Oh my God, this is amazing.' At that point, the business isn't bearing the cost of their training anymore — the salesperson is. And they develop skills that will compound for the rest of their career.

## Never Blame Circumstances

My biggest pet peeve — and the reverse of my favorite quality in the best salespeople — is blaming circumstances. Whenever I have a salesperson who says, 'The leads are bad,' or 'These people aren't a good fit,' or 'I don't like the commission structure,' I know exactly where they are in their development. Focus on what you can control.

The guys I love the most never complain. They pick up extra shifts. They call the leads the most. They work them the hardest. They say, 'These leads are amazing — I'm so grateful to get to practice and get paid to do it.' If they have feedback, they give it one-on-one with their manager, not in the group call. They say something like, 'Hey, I've noticed the credit scores on the leads seem lower than a couple months ago — I don't know if anything's changed on the marketing side. I'll keep approaching them the same way, just thought it might be useful data for the marketing team.' That's very different from: 'These leads are trash and I'm not taking these calls.'

The worse the leads, the better the opportunity to flex your skill. The best salespeople take 100% ownership over every outcome. The best marketers do the same. When everyone on the team takes absolute accountability, guess what? You win.
