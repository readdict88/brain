# Wealth, Work, and the Principles That Separate the Rich from the Poor
*Alex Hormozi — Transcript*

## Introduction

I crossed $100 million in net worth at age 31. I'm 35 now —
significantly higher than that. And I have the privilege of talking to a
lot of people who are at a billion dollars and above. In this video I
want to break down a handful of themes that I've seen repeated that can
make you significantly more successful.

## The Rich Buy Time. The Poor Buy Stuff.

The rich buy time. The poor buy stuff. Ambitious people buy skills. And
lazy people buy distractions. As much as these seem like four different
groups, they're really two pairings: rich people use money to buy time,
and ambitious people use time to buy skills.

The reason it's 'ambitious people' and not 'rich people' when it comes
to skills is because generational wealth isn't transferred through
assets or stuff — it's transferred through education, through skills.
There's a Sanskrit proverb I love: if you have a good son, you don't
need to accumulate wealth; if you have a bad son, there's no point in
accumulating wealth. The idea that you're going to pass on your wealth
to a child who doesn't have the skills makes the wealth itself
irrelevant — because if the son has skills, whether you transfer the
wealth or not doesn't matter. And I find there are so many things like
this in wealth creation that you don't understand until you're on the
other side of it.

The answers you're looking for are not in your newsfeed. They are not in
distractions. The vast majority of lazy people I've encountered continue
to distract themselves because the difficulty of looking at themselves
in the mirror and saying 'I am inadequate' is too painful. So instead
they watch content that makes them feel good and tickles their ears
rather than confronting reality. They consume stuff that says 'I am
worthy, I deserve these things' — in other words, that they're entitled
to them. But if you feel entitled to something, when you get it you are
never grateful, because you thought you deserved it to begin with.
Gratitude increases your subjective well-being — you enjoy life more
when you're grateful for what you get. And gratitude, by definition,
requires you to expect nothing. Which means you're entitled to nothing.
You are worthy only of the things you currently have, because you have
already proven you can get them.

You can be both ambitious and distracted — and that keeps you poor.

## Focus Is Measured by What You Say No To

When I started out, at my peak I had nine businesses. I'd heard that
seven income streams make you wealthy — but it doesn't. Seven income
streams just spread you seven ways. I had nine. I thought, 'Well, if
seven is good, nine must be better.' To give you context: a chiropractor
agency, a dental agency, a gym turnaround business, and six gym
locations. That's nine. And I had partners in each of those businesses —
but I was still the one required to rainmake, the one required to sell
customers into all nine.

If you are required to work inside of the business, you can only really
do one job. When people hear that someone has seven income streams, it's
because they own assets that generate that income. But in order to gain
the cash to buy the assets that create cash flow, it requires time and
focus. Focus is a very amorphous term, so let me make it real: focus is
measured by the amount of things you say no to. Inversely, you could
measure it by how few things you say yes to. The most focused person
says yes to only one thing and no to literally everything else.

## 100x, Not 2x

The next thing that separates the rich from the poor is this concept:
it's 100x, not 2x. I had dinner with a friend of mine who is a public
company CEO. I asked him what he'd done to grow the business from a
roughly $200 million market cap to $1.2 billion in less than two years —
absurd growth. He said, 'I'm shaking hands, kissing babies, going to
events all the time.' I asked how many. He said 66 events this quarter.
In person. And he was traveling to them, not having them come to him. He
has a wife. He has kids. He's making trades. If anything, I would hope
people watch this and say, 'I wouldn't make that trade' — great, also
don't complain about not having what someone who made that trade has.

One of the big misconceptions is that people want a certain outcome
their way. They want to have their cake and eat it too. They want to not
change how they eat, not change how they live, but have a six-pack. They
want to keep their current work schedule but have hyper growth. These
are price tags. When you go to a store, you either want the shoes and
are willing to pay the price, or you're not. You can't pick the price
for the shoes. You pay it or you don't.

## The 12x30 Challenge

My telephone to my past self is that I thought I worked a lot when I was
younger — and I did spend a lot of time working. But I made so little
progress because a lot of that time I wasn't actually creating output. I
encourage people to do something I call the 12x30: you work 12 hours a
day for 30 days straight, no days off. By the last day you realize six
things: one, you aren't made of glass; two, how much more you could be
working; three, how much faster you could be achieving your goals; four,
how much better you could be getting every single day; five, you don't
need to work like this forever — but if you need to for a season, you
have that gear; and six, there are people working more than you who are
beating you.

And you get all of those lessons for the low price of getting more done
in a month than you probably did last quarter.

## What Real Volume Looks Like

I define work as volume times leverage — how many times you do
something, multiplied by how much you get for each time you do it. In
the beginning you need to do a lot more volume. Through that volume you
gain skill. The leverage also increases over time. So you get more
output for the same input.

When I paid a well-known brand builder for insight into growing my
personal brand, he pulled up my social media profiles one by one next to
his and said simply, 'You're not doing enough volume.' He had genuinely
been doing ten times more than I was. So I did ten times more volume. We
got better at doing it. We're still doing that same level of volume now
— and getting even more output. I later had a similar conversation with
a billionaire friend who was getting into content. I showed him his
Instagram next to mine, his TikTok next to mine, his YouTube next to
mine, and said, 'I'm making 450 pieces a week. You're making seven.' He
immediately got it — because he had already learned the 100x lesson in a
different domain.

## Work Itself Is a Skill

I remember being a legal intern at a firm in France called Areva. I was
working hard — or so I thought. I had one exchange with a senior legal
partner. She looked at me and said, 'That's cute. You don't even know
what work is. You spend time here, but you don't know how to work.' I
understood what she meant, especially now: you're spending time here but
not getting anything done. It takes time and reps to get better at
working itself.

When I work, there are many times where you feel like you need a break.
For people who aren't as good at working yet, that break period might be
the rest of the day. The better I've gotten at working, the more I can
whip my head back in and keep going. You realize there's a five to
fifteen-minute period where your output slows a little, but then you
catch the next draft and you're in again. That muscle — the ability to
pull yourself back in — takes practice. And the only opportunity you get
to practice it is to work yourself to the point of fatigue, to the point
where rest would be beneficial, and then choose to keep going anyway.

It's like dieting: there are moments where you get cravings and feel
like you're starving. But if you set a timer when the craving hits and
wait 20 minutes, it almost always passes in five to ten minutes. The
same thing happens with focus. But most people just give in — they order
the pizza, they take the rest of the day off, they go watch Netflix. I
will tell you: you're not going to die. There were people in history who
were forced to work for years on end with far less than we have today.
That proves it's possible. You are just alive and awake — you might as
well keep nudging things forward.

I know the idea of 'work on what you love and you'll never work a day in
your life' is out there. I just don't think it's true. There are so many
things I don't enjoy doing that I have to do. My saying has always been:
I will do what is required. How I feel about it is relatively
irrelevant. The things that have come from doing what is required have
made doing the requirements something I know will pay off. I feast on
past achievements as fuel to keep doing things that may be uncomfortable
or unpleasant in the moment.

## They Cannot Copy Your Proof

People want to gain reputations. They want to be known for certain
things, to get status. But people can copy your promises. They can copy
your services, your logo, your name. What they cannot copy is what
you've done.

I sometimes have younger entrepreneurs reach out and say they want to
build a big brand and start making content. I advise them to only make
content if they're truly documenting — and not to transition into
teacher mode. The thing that will build your brand is the stuff you do.
Look at the business titans: Elon Musk, Bezos, Sam Altman, Jensen Huang
from Nvidia. Steve Jobs, Bill Gates, Warren Buffett — considered the
leaders of the business world in terms of thought leadership. But did
they get there by building personal brands, or by building personal
businesses? By building big businesses, and by extension, people wanted
to know who was behind those businesses. They built their credibility
before they built their likeability.

In a world where anyone can say anything and get instant reach, trust is
the scarce commodity. The way people trust you is that you do what you
said you would do. Mr. Beast's videos worked because he used them as
proof. He didn't say 'I am super knowledgeable' — he said 'look what I
did that no one else was willing to do.' His first viral video was
counting to a million or something equally committed. He did something
other people weren't willing to do, and he happened to document it. If
you're wondering how to get proof when you're young — just do epic
stuff. People will say, 'Wow, he did epic stuff.' And it still requires
epic amounts of work.

The reason there's such a misconception about overnight success is that
there's a threshold over which you are good enough to go viral — and the
public only sees the moment of breakthrough. What they see is the
output. What they don't see is everything that came before it. And this
is why it's so difficult for people to understand: they want to model
what they see, not what occurred. Most people get rich and then get
weird — and then other people try to copy what they do after getting
rich, not what they did to get there. They model the plateau, not the
rise. You don't get rich by flying on private jets. You get rich, then
you can fly on private jets.

## Act with Urgency

Rich people — and really, entrepreneurs specifically — act with urgency.
You can tell in some ways how successful someone is going to be by how
quickly they do things. The gap between thought and action tells you a
lot about a person's potency. You can improve how powerful you are by
decreasing the gap between when you think something and when you
actually do it.

The word 'decide' comes from the Latin decadere, meaning to cut off. So
the question is: what are you eliminating by making the decision? A very
simple tactical habit: whenever you say 'I'll get this done by the end
of the week,' ask yourself, 'Can I get it done by the end of today?'
That seems like a small hack, but it increases your pace of progress by
7x. Move things from end of month to end of week — that's a 4x increase.
End of week to end of day — that's a 7x increase. End of day to end of
hour. And if it's a five-minute task and you have five minutes, do it
now.

There is a big falseness propagated by much of the social media world
because it feels good and gets lots of shares — it gives people
permission to be lazy, permission to tell themselves they're great just
the way they are. I wholeheartedly reject that. 'You're young, you've
got time' is false. Tomorrow isn't guaranteed. I looked at my newsfeed
this morning and there was a guy I knew, my age, who just died of
stomach cancer. Into fitness, really in shape — just a freak thing. The
assumption that you've got time assumes you're going to live forever,
and we know that's not true.

Three out of 10,000 people live to 100. More realistically, you'll die
around the median — which is 74 for men in the US. That means at 37
you're at the halfway point in years. But if you adjust for the increase
in perceived speed of time — when you're five, one year is 20% of your
entire life; at 40, one year is 2.5% of your life — the midpoint isn't
37. It's closer to 21. This is also why when you look back on your early
years, so much seems to have happened: those years were literally half
of your life at the time.

## Hard Is for Now, Not Forever

Things are going to get really hard really fast. But hard is for now,
not forever. The good thing about bad times is that they end. And the
bad thing about good times is that they also end. When you're in a hard
season, that can give you the necessary fuel — you can see the end of
the tunnel. There are only three outcomes: you quit, it gets easier, or
you get harder. No matter what, it always ends. But you only lose when
you quit before you see it through. Two out of three outcomes get easier
— either the external circumstance changes, or you become harder and it
no longer feels as hard.

Think about the problems you were stressed about five years ago in your
business. The things you were dealing with then — they're probably a
joke to you now. Not because those things became less hard, but because
you became better. And here's where this gets really interesting: it
just means that nothing is inherently hard to begin with. We are just
always inadequate for the challenge in front of us.

I remember when I had five employees and was stressed about managing so
many people. Or how to manage cash flow. Or how to get new customers. Or
how to sell higher-priced things without someone complaining. All of
these feel completely irrelevant to me now. And I can only imagine how
inconsequential the problems I deal with growing portfolio companies
will seem compared to what someone like Elon Musk deals with managing
multiple hundred-billion-dollar companies.

Here's something that will also give you hope about hard times: these
hard times are also your best times — but only in reverse. Think about
when you were younger, in love, and poor. You probably say now, 'Those
were the good old days.' The thing is, when you were in that moment, it
was stressful. You didn't know how you'd make rent. But those hard times
became the good old days. We only see the truth when we look back. You
have to go through them to get the perspective. So I try to remind
myself regularly: I'm going through the good old days right now. If
they're going to be the good old days in the future, they might as well
be the good old days today.

If you are consistently growing, you are consistently in pain. That's
why they're called growing pains, not growing joys. And if you have
stress from growth, remember: the alternative is stress from stagnation,
or stress from decline. Hardship and stress are facts of life. The
biggest problem of all is determining that having problems is a problem
— believing that being stressed means something is wrong with you. Being
stressed just means you're alive. Having problems just means you're
actually doing something.

## The Parable of the Creator

Imagine you talk to the Creator of the universe before birth and pick
the person you get to become. You say: 'I want to be the most
courageous.' The Creator replies, 'Then I will give you monsters to
terrify you, so that you can conquer them.' You say: 'I want to be
patient.' The Creator replies, 'Then I will make you work harder and
longer, so you can learn to wait.' You say: 'I want to be wise.' The
Creator replies, 'Then I will give you failures that will crush your
spirit, so you can learn the value of judgment.' You say: 'Sounds like a
rough life. Can you give me a good life?' The Creator replies: 'Just as
I measure the quality of a blacksmith by the strength of the steel, I
measure you by what you are at the end — not the fire and the hammer it
took to make you. A good life isn't a life that's easy. A good life is a
life that makes you into a good person. And that, my son, is a hard
life.'

When I sold Gym Launch, there was about a year that I more or less took
off. I had all these existential crises. All I had been doing was
working, and I hadn't put my head up to look around and smell the roses.
The problem was, when I smelled the roses, I was miserable. The thing
that was most reinforcing in my life was the work. Not having that
created a huge loss. That's when I formed the thesis I have for my life:
hard work is the goal. Emptying the tank every day is the point — I got
that from Jesse Itzler. For me, those are the days I feel most fulfilled
looking back. In the moment you feel tired, bored, like you don't want
to do another rep. But another rep, another edit, another two hours is
what is required — not what you want, but what is required.

## Act with Urgency — Shrink the Gap

The gap between thought and action is the measure of your power.
Decrease it. Move end of month to end of week. Move end of week to end
of day. Move end of day to end of hour. If it's a five-minute task and
you have five minutes, do it now. Leila is a master of this — she hears
an idea and it's already been delegated before I finish the sentence.

When you don't have any more information to collect in order to make a
decision, make the decision. Eliminate the alternatives. The word
'decide' literally means to cut off. What future are you eliminating by
making this choice? Once you do, move immediately.

## Investment in Education — The Only Asset That Compounds Forever

In my life, I've spent more money than I could possibly imagine on
acquiring skills and relationships that would make me better. The most
expensive check I wrote was $350,000 to have dinner with someone worth
$7 billion. I'd offered $100,000, then $200,000, then $250,000, then
$350,000 — and at $350,000 he finally said, 'Fine, I'll have dinner with
you.' I got exactly what I wanted from that dinner. It was incredibly
valuable.

Pick whatever your income goal is. Let's say it's a million dollars a
year and you're currently making $100,000. The value of the education
needed to get from $100K to $1M per year is the difference between where
you are and where you want to go. That's why if there's a way to spend
$50,000 to learn a million-dollar-per-year skill set, every year you
don't do it, you lose $950,000 in what you would have made. We use poor
people's language when we talk about education. We should use investor
language. It's not an expense — it's an investment in an asset. The
asset just happens to be you.

The only thing more expensive than education is not being educated.

The process I use with every program or coaching engagement: first, do
everything they tell you to do — to the letter, the first time. Then
take what works and discard the rest. You can learn something from
everyone, but only take in that which makes you stronger. A lot of
people have negative experiences with coaches, consultants, vendors. I
just ask myself: in what way will this make me better? Rather than
enumerating all the things they did wrong, I simply ask: what can I
learn from this? What in this experience will make me stronger? That
frame has been one of the most valuable I've ever had.

## It Costs What It Costs. It Takes What It Takes.

Bad entrepreneurs make preference excuses, trade-off excuses. Privacy is
the price of fame. Loneliness is the price of ambition. Pleasure is the
price of discipline. Novelty is the price of loyalty. Discretion is the
price of trust. So many people want the benefits of these traits but
aren't willing to pay the cost. They want someone to be loyal to them
while they want the thrill of something new. They want the benefits of
discipline while trading away the short-term pleasure. They want fame
but are startled when they have no privacy.

Each of these trade-offs is not binary. It's not 'discipline' or 'not
discipline' — it's how much discipline. It's not 'loneliness' — it's how
lonely, and for how long. Most people believe they're doing 10-out-of-10
work right now. But they're actually doing 1-out-of-10 work relative to
what it takes to reach their goal. They might be doing 10-out-of-10 work
to earn $100,000 — but 1-out-of-10 work to earn a million. A lot of that
gap comes down to accurately assessing reality and seeing what your
actual discrepancies are.

Most outcomes don't occur until you've met all the conditions — like a
pipeline with valves. If you've checked three out of four boxes, the
water still doesn't flow. You might have done everything right on three
conditions and still not gotten the outcome because one condition is
unmet. That's not the universe being unfair. That's just a condition you
haven't met yet. Find it and meet it.

You can't wish for strong character and an easy life. The price of each
is the other. Think of yourself as both the steel and the blacksmith.
The Creator gives you the fire. You can stand far enough away that it
just keeps you warm, or you can go into it and hammer out the impurities
— and become better and stronger every time.

## The Exception Cannot Expect Acceptance

Buried in the word 'exceptional' is the key point: you cannot expect to
be accepted if you want to be exceptional. To become exceptional by
definition, you have to become the exception — which means you have to
do what other people don't agree with. If everyone agrees with you, you
can be sure that it's the wrong path.

When I quit my job, every single person in my life told me it was a bad
idea. I had graduated from Vanderbilt, had a great consulting career
lined up, was set up to go to an Ivy League graduate school. For me to
quit and become a personal trainer for minimum wage — that was not happy
news for almost anyone I knew. I've had that same conversation multiple
times. When I left the gyms and started the turnaround business, every
person said, 'You finally made this thing work — now you're going to
throw it away?' It was right at the point where they accepted me again
that I wanted to do the next thing.

Don't listen to the people who are closest to you. Listen to the people
who are closest to your goals. People think they compare themselves to
their mentors and heroes — but they don't. Who you actually compare
yourself to is who influences your decisions. The voices you hear when
you're about to make a big move — those voices are your actual reference
group. If those voices are not close to your goal, don't listen to them.
If anything, sometimes the best advice is to do the opposite of whatever
they did to end up in their life.

## Masters Have More Ways to Win

The thing that separates masters from beginners is that masters have
more ways to win. You can measure expertise in anything by the number of
ways you can measure progress equaling the number of times and ways you
can win. If you're an expert at making videos, throughout the entire
process of a long project you have many wins along the way — you know
all the little progress markers that create some big outcome. When
you're a beginner, you only see the output, and all you see is 'I'm
doing stuff and nothing is happening.' You just aren't able to measure
progress well yet.

To increase your expertise, you need to increase the number of ways you
can measure progress — which increases the number of wins you have in a
given time period. And that is what makes you enjoy your work. That is
how the work itself becomes reinforcing.

It happens slower than you expect — and then faster than you can
imagine. I was building an outbound team at Gym Launch and it took 90
days to get our first sale. My executive team staged what felt like an
intervention at one point, using my own language against me, saying it
was a 'shiny object' and we should cut our losses. But I kept thinking:
this is what hard feels like. We just need to keep inching through. Six
months later, outbound became 50% of revenue.

If I told you that in one year I could double your business, and all you
had to do was invest $100,000 — most people would say yes. But what
actually happens is you lose money month one, month two, month three.
Then suddenly month four you make five times what you lost. It happens
all at once. But people quit in month two and say 'ads aren't for me.'
The score doesn't show up on the way up, only at the end.

## The Final Point: 19 Drafts, Not 2

Lazy people distract themselves because distraction makes them believe
the lie that they can try a hundred things and figure out which one
works. In reality, any of them can work. But none of them will work
unless you pick one and then make it work. The vast majority of things,
with enough iteration, can become successful. People just underestimate
how many iterations are required. It's 19 drafts, not two. It's 500
minutes of editing, not 50. It's making a thousand phone calls every
week for two years, not two months.

Most people get stuck believing they should be better, that it should be
easier, that they've already paid enough. That's the same as entitlement
with a price tag attached. It costs what it costs. It takes what it
takes. You have to be willing to make the trade. And if you're not —
fine. Just don't complain.

If you want to become the exception, make the trade-offs, invest in
skills, invest your time, do the repetitions, gain leverage, and
continue pulling your reality forward at a faster and faster pace — then
you're on the right path.
