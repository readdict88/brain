## Obstacles vs. Objections

An obstacle is a thing that blocks one's way or prevents or hinders progress. When you disagree with someone before you've asked for the sale, that is an obstacle. An objection is when they disagree after you've asked for the sale and presented the price.

It's much easier to confront and destroy obstacles before you ask for the sale. Once you've asked for the sale, you switch to handling objections. Example of an obstacle: 'I'm not here to find out more about the program — I'm just looking around.' You handle that up front. Example of an objection: 'I don't want to buy right now.' That comes after you've presented the price.

Learning how to talk through these high-stakes decisions with yourself or others is the purpose of this presentation. As a result, you'll become more powerful.

## The Three Core Distortions

There are three sources that people cast their power to, and it took me a very long time to figure this out. I went back to Dr. Albert Ellis, who invented cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT). He originally had 11 distortions of reality, then dropped it to nine, then to seven, then to three. The three stuck. And the three are what I'm about to share with you. The objections that people offer you — where they cast their power — are distortions of reality.

Distortion one — Circumstances: 'I must get what I want when I want it. I must not get what I don't want. If I don't get what I want, I can't stand it.' That's when people blame their emotional disturbances on circumstances.

Distortion two — Others: 'Other people must treat me fairly and kindly, and if they don't, they are no good and deserve to be condemned and punished.' That's your animal brain.

Distortion three — Self: 'I must do well, or else I am no good.'

These are the three core distortions that upset people. They are not real. If you can understand — not memorize, but understand — this, then you can have high-stakes conversations knowing exactly which distortion someone is suffering from, and you can show them how to overcome it.

## The Onion of Blame

People start with the outside — they blame circumstances: time, money, other things. Then the next layer, they blame other people: my spouse, my kids, my employees. And finally they say, 'It's me. It's my fault.' You might have to overcome multiple layers as you peel back the onion.

If someone just says 'I have to think about it,' you're already talking to someone who's in power and is the decision-maker. You just need to get them to say yes. If they say 'I don't have the money,' they're casting their power away. Based on which objection they give you, you can know where you are and how close you are.

These distortions manifest in five ways: Time, Value/Price/Money, Fit ('I'm a special snowflake'), Authority ('I don't have the authority to make the decision'), and Self/Avoidance ('What if I just let life make the decision for me?'). You go from surface level to core. Ideally, you never get stuck anywhere on this equation if the person truly wants what you have and you truly believe in what you have to help them.

# Part 2: Overcoming the Five Objections

## Objection 1 — Time ('I'm Too Busy')

The manifestations of a time objection: macro ('It's a busy season for me'), micro ('I don't have any time in my day'), and 'when/then' ('When I have time, I'll start').

As always, we never disagree with prospects — because you're not in a fight. So: 'Totally get it. Do you want this to be something that lasts for the long term? Do you think you're going to be busy again in the future? Well, then don't you think it'd be best to start now, when you are busy? Because if you learn how to do it when you're busy, you'll be able to do it forever. If you can only learn it during a perfect circumstance, you're going to fall off when it gets busy again. And isn't that the time you'd want to have the most support?'

For the micro version: 'You know what, I had the same issue. I used to complain all the time about how I didn't have time to do the things I needed to do. My wife got so sick and tired of me saying this that she pulled out my phone and said, 'Look — I just found your time.' I was like, ah, damn. And here's the reality: the first thing any good program is going to do is cut out the 90% of the stuff you're doing that's causing you to feel overwhelmed — which is probably the real reason you feel like you don't have time. Jeff Bezos has the same amount of time as you. It's just about what you're doing that he's not. It's not about adding things to your plate; it's about removing the stuff that isn't working. Clearly, everything you're doing that's filling your time is not making you more money. So you're doing the wrong things.'

For the 'when/then' fallacy: 'I totally understand — I was stuck like this for years.' Then walk them through the epiphany. The when/then fallacy simply states: 'When I have X, then I will do Y.' But it flips the sequence. It's like saying, 'When I get better, I will go to the hospital.' Or, 'I'll start saving money once I'm rich.' Or, 'I'll pay for the program that will make me more money once I have more money.' That's the point of the program. It's a distortion — it's not real.

## Objection 2 — Money ('I Can't Afford It')

Framework one — 'Why a lot is good': Ask, 'Is this a lot of money to you?' If they say yes: 'That's the exact reason you're going to be successful. There are other people who buy this thing and they're not all in on it. But the question is: do you want to draw the line in the sand and go all in? Do you want to step over the line and be the person you want to be? The best stories always come from people in your exact situation. Don't let that be a reason not to do it — that should be the reason to do it.'

Framework two — Relative value: 'It's a lot in absolute terms, but in relative terms? If all this does is add $10,000 a month to your income, is it worth it? If all this does is get you to your high school weight, is it worth it?' Once they say yes, it's not about the price anymore — it's about whether they believe you. So you can sidestep it.

Framework three — Comparison: 'There's no four-year degree for doing this — that's why the alternative education industry exists. Some of those degrees cost $50,000 to $200,000 and take four years, when you've got something that can help you make that entire amount in half the time for a tenth of the price. When you think about it that way, it's actually a great deal.'

Framework four — 'What's money good for anyway?': 'You're going to spend this money either way over the next 12 months. Not only that — you're going to buy the program. The question is whether you're going to pay for it in money or pay for it in time. You can learn every lesson in this program on your own — whether it's losing weight, getting leads, selling online. You're going to learn these lessons. But do you want it to take 12 weeks or 12 years? Are you going to be able to live a thousand lifetimes, or are you going to gather the lessons from the thousand people we've already helped and use that? So the question isn't whether you're going to buy it or not — it's just how do you want to pay.'

And a classic: 'Who here has had an unexpected bill come up — taxes, something? And who was able to magically pull money out of nowhere and figure out a way to do it? Here's what's crazy: you have the ability to be resourceful when it's for someone else, but not for yourself. It's because you choose to be powerful when someone else needs it, but not for you. If you functioned like you had to make payroll tomorrow, you'd think differently. Everyone here just proved the fact that you can be resourceful when you choose to be — so just choose to be.'

## Objection 3 — Fit ('I'm Not Sure It's for Me')

Framework one — New identity, new priorities: 'We vote with our dollars about the things we care about. If you show me what someone spends their money and time on, I can tell you what their future looks like. You've had priorities that are aligned with your old identity. There's the you that goes out, the you that spends money on things you shouldn't. And then there's the you that you want to be. Right now, we can draw a line in the sand. Do you want to keep walking down the road you've been walking? If not, you have to step into a new identity. And with that new identity come new priorities. People who want to be successful spend money on themselves, on education, on their skill sets.'

Framework two — Change the change: 'What you've been doing has been getting you what you've been getting. You've got to change the change. I remember going through meal plans with people and they'd say, 'Well, can we change the breakfast? Can we have what I normally eat?' And I'd say — that breakfast is why you look the way you do. The way you spend your first four hours of the day is why your bank account looks like it does. It's going to hurt to change, because change hurts. The question is whether the pain of staying the same is greater than the pain of change.'

Framework three — Hypothetical: 'If this were perfect, would you do it?' You get a hypothetical agreement. If they say yes, flip it: 'Then what's the difference between perfection and what we've got?' People often can't even articulate what's missing, because they don't actually know how to generate all the things a program can deliver on the spot. Most of the time they'll say, 'I don't know.' And you say, 'Right — because it sounds like it has nothing to do with the program. It's all about you. So let's talk about that.' One step closer to the truth.

## Objection 4 — Authority ('I Have to Talk to My Spouse/Partner')

The spouse or decision-maker is not there. So you try to sidestep it by isolating the objection and casting aside the partner, because the partner isn't there — so how could they object to something they know nothing about? Ask: 'What do you think they wouldn't like?' Then they will tell you. And then you just overcome that thing, which has nothing to do with the spouse anyway.

The process: 'Do they approve of your current struggle? Are they happy that you're struggling? No. Then why would they not approve of something that's going to fix something they already don't approve of? They're not happy with how you're currently doing — why would they be against something that's already making them unhappy?'

Then: 'If the roles were reversed, and your husband or wife needed this program to make their dreams happen, would you support them? Then why wouldn't you support you?'

The kicker: 'I think the real problem we're dealing with here is that you're asking for permission instead of support. Because it is your life, not theirs. If you give that power to them, and two years from now you're still in the same spot — who are you going to blame? I'm not saying you shouldn't explain the decision to them. But the way you explain it is this: 'If I don't do this for me, I'm going to end up resenting you. And I don't want that. Instead of complaining to you every day about how we don't have money or I don't like my body, I'm now doing something about it — and I'm joining a community of people just like me. I'm not asking your permission. I'm just asking for your support.''

## Objection 5 — Avoidance ('I Need to Think About It')

Now that we've exhausted all outside reasons, we're finally talking to someone who is in power. That is what a good coach does — we don't make decisions for people, we help them make decisions.

Past frame: 'This isn't a fast decision. You've been making this decision for the last six years. All we're deciding is whether you're actually going to do something about it. How long have you wanted this? A long time. So this isn't a fast decision at all.' Then go to sunk cost: they've already done so much to get here. They bought a ticket, flew in, registered, listened for hours. That's not a fast decision.

Then: 'Don't let a bad decision burn you twice. Once when you made your poor investment — but a second time when you let that bad investment stop you from a good investment. It would be like having a bad eighth-grade boyfriend or girlfriend and deciding, 'You know what, I'm done with relationships.' There are good coaches and there are bad coaches, just like there are good and bad partners. If you stop because the bad one hurt you, you get burned by the bad one and then again by letting the bad one control your next decision.'

Another angle: 'Do you think you might be in this position because you've struggled to make decisions in the past? Have you been in this conversation before and said no? Do you think that's why you're here? Do you think that might be the reason you should change that? Okay, let's change it. One decision can change your life.'

And: 'Are you tired of another year of almost? Almost hitting your goals, almost getting to $10K a month, almost retiring your wife or husband, almost moving into the neighborhood you wanted? If you're tired of another year of almost, then we can't keep doing what we're doing — because we're going to get another year of almost. The question isn't what it will cost you. It's how much has it cost you not to decide up to this point? The cost of inaction.'

Present frame: 'Let's walk through what thinking about it actually looks like. You're not going to go home, sit in the rocking chair, smoke a cigarette and think, 'Hmm, should I do this weight loss program?' Of course not. You're going to get in your car, realize you have to pick up Timmy from soccer practice, get groceries, cook, clean, do the laundry. Three, four, five days from now you'll put on that pair of jeans, it won't fit, and you'll think, 'Damn.' And just like that, you'll have made the decision to keep living your life. So let's just make the decision now. It doesn't take time to make a decision — it takes information. And I'm the only source of information you've got. So let's talk.'

The three questions: 'Do you believe this product or service is going to help you achieve what you want — yes or no? Do you trust me to fulfill my word — that I'm going to help you, make these calls, do these workouts? Do you think it'll work for you?' If they say no to the last one, ask why not, and they have to defend why not. Then: 'Do you have or have access to the money to get started?' And if they believe in all three, it's your moral obligation to do everything in your power to help them get started.

Future frame: 'Let's consider the options. Option one: you do the thing and get the result. Option two: you don't do the thing and don't get the result, because you didn't do the thing. Option three: you do the thing and don't get the result. Because we have a guarantee, all of them are essentially risk-free except one. Only one has a true guarantee of not getting you where you want — and that's walking out the door. So which risk-free option do you want?'

And: 'You're not going to struggle forever, right? You're going to do something about this eventually. Well, if you're going to fix it eventually, you might as well start now so you can start enjoying the fruits of that labor sooner. Would you prefer making more money faster, or slower?'

And finally: 'Do you think making this decision is going to get you closer to or further from your goal? That's it. Because if we keep making decisions that get us closer, we will get there eventually. We don't need to be snipers — we need to be directionally right. There's no one program that changed my life, but the decision to buy education changed my life forever.'

## The Bonus Close

And a bonus sixth: 'The reason you are telling yourself not to do this is the reason you need to do it. How long do you want 'I can't afford this' to be on your list of problems? # Part 4: Nine Things the Best Salespeople Do Differently

## Overview

Over the years of having hundreds of salespeople work for me, I've seen nine things that the best salespeople do differently than everyone else. A salesperson has three jobs: maximize the number of opportunities they have, convert the highest percentage of those opportunities, and do it consistently for a very long period of time.

## Bucket 1: Maximizing Opportunities

I've never seen a top salesperson in a company who also has the fewest hours worked. The guy who works the most hours is the one who sells the most deals, every single time. Maximizing opportunities means having the most hours available per day, because you should be available when the prospect is available — and that sometimes means working long hours, working weekends.

My software company, Allen, scheduled 4,000-plus appointments per day across different industries. The single greatest lever on total throughput across all companies was the total number of available time slots. Availability was the strongest predictor of total sales.

The best salespeople pull up calls. If a call is booked for Thursday and today is Monday and you have an open slot, drag that Thursday appointment to today. Same-day appointments always have higher show-up rates. If you pull it up, they're even more interested, and you can close at a higher rate. You'll also have two or three calls by the same Thursday time slot, increasing your sales velocity.

If a prospect hasn't confirmed and the appointment is coming up in a few hours, the best salespeople reach out: 'Hey, I didn't see that you confirmed — why don't we do the same time tomorrow?' Then they fill that slot with someone who is responsive. The perfect day for a salesperson is back-to-back calls with the best, hottest leads.

I learned this from one of our solar sales companies. One rep massively outperformed everyone else. Whenever a new appointment booked, he immediately called to qualify the lead. If the lead had time right then, he went straight into closing. If not, he used that contact moment to try to move them to a same-day slot. He was selling four times more units than the second-best guy on the team.

The best salespeople also have two SOPs — standard operating procedures. One for on-the-call: what you say when you're talking to prospects. One for off-the-call: what you do in between. The vast majority of companies don't have an off-the-call SOP, and they're losing enormous amounts of money because of it.

Book a meeting from a meeting (BAMFAM). The best salespeople never finish a call without knowing when the next time they're going to talk to the prospect is. If you get to the end of the call and they say 'Let's circle back,' you have to address it right then. Because you both have your calendars up, you can both make the decision. Never let a prospect fall through the cracks.

Three more things the best salespeople do: First, they don't take rejection personally — they work their leads harder. They reach out in more volume, they follow up more, and they keep going because they know it's a numbers game. Second, they have a 'kill list' — a separate visible list of their highest-value prospects that they give extra attention to every day. Third, they create their own opportunities. At the end of every call, they ask for referrals. My favorite phrasing: 'Who do you know who is as awesome as you?' It's a compliment that forces a name, not a yes/no.

## Bucket 2: Converting the Highest Percentage

Understand the value of preparation. You would be amazed at how much of a genius you sound like if you just do five minutes of research before a call. My rule of thumb: if it's something you do all the time, spend about 10% of the meeting time on prep. For a 60-minute call, that's six minutes of prep. For something you don't do often, it reverses: six hours of prep for a one-hour presentation.

The best salespeople take notes. This prepares them for the next call. If you hop on the phone and say, 'Oh yeah, so you've got your daughter Sarah and Jessica, and they're at Colorado, and you've got X, Y, Z happening in the business — is that correct? I'm just reading over some past notes.' They're like, 'Wow, I don't have to retell my whole life story.' This switches you from being a salesperson to a consultant.

The most brilliant salespeople listen more than they talk. The best salespeople listen twice as much as they talk. The person who's answering the questions is the one getting interrogated. You don't want to be the one getting interrogated. Whenever you answer questions, you give the other person something to attack. So you want them to be answering so that you have things to work with. If someone asks, 'What makes you better than your competition?' say, 'Well, what things are you looking for?' — right back to them. They believe nothing that you say. They believe everything that they say. So I need them to say that it's a good idea, not me.

The best salespeople breathe the script. That means they can say it without thinking, just like you breathe without thinking. You can't listen to a prospect if you're waiting to talk because you're trying to remember what you're going to say next. You need to know all of the script so thoroughly that you can be 100% present with the prospect and listening.

The best salespeople keep their word concision as tight as humanly possible. A mediocre salesperson will do the script and then add a cough right before they ask for the close, and then the person closes. They get positive reinforcement for coughing. On their next call, they add the cough in. And if they close again, they add another question before the close. Over time, the length of the script expands and expands — even though none of it was necessary. You want to be as concise as humanly possible. The less time you're on the phone after making the sale, the more time you have to fill your calendar and close more deals.

The three tactics for sales managers: rehearse the script every morning, do role plays — focus on one specific part of the script per session — and when you give feedback, focus on one thing at a time. Nobody can improve multiple things simultaneously. Skills coaches work on one thing at a time until they clean it all up, and then all of a sudden the person becomes exceptional.

## Kill Zombies Up Front

This is what we call 'killing zombies' or 'diffusing the bomb.' An obstacle occurs before you've mentioned the price. An objection occurs after. If you come on the phone and someone says, 'I have a special snowflake thyroid issue,' and you don't address it up front, it will blow up on you at the close. It's much easier to address things before you ask for money than after.

The things that will blow up on you are always the same: circumstances (time, money, fit), other people (spouse, business partner, decision-maker), and self (doubt about whether they can do it or whether they'll be successful).

One of my favorite ways to get to the hard question is simply asking, 'What are you afraid of happening?' Then I follow up with, 'Let's just play it out.' Best case, worst case. I've probably closed more sales on best case/worst case than any other close I have.

Ask for the sale again. If you do not ask for the sale, you will not get it. If someone says no and you ask why, then resolve the why, then ask again. You can do this literally unlimited times as long as you resolve the concern and then ask again. The number of sales you make is directly proportional to how many times you ask.

## Bucket 3: Meta-Skills — Doing It Consistently

The things most salespeople struggle with are trait issues. They're on, then they're off. They get motivated, then demotivated. If you feel this way, it's a skill deficiency — you just don't know how to sustain performance for a long period of time. The difference between champions and everyone else is that they can sustain performance.

The best salespeople kill for sport. As Mike Ary says: there are dogs, horses, and tigers. Dogs — you have to feed them and give them constant attention, or they die. Horses — they'll gallop as long as you keep whipping them, but the moment you stop, they go to a trot. Tigers — a tiger can eat a full meal, but if a bunny walks across their line of vision, they'll kill the bunny for sport. You want tigers.

The best salespeople track data meticulously. The skill of any person in any endeavor they practice can be judged by the quantity and quality of the metrics they track. Close rate is really just an outcome — it gives you no leading indicators. What percentage of leads are you booking? What's your show rate? Of shows, what percentage are you offering? Of offers, what percentage are you closing? What's your average number of calls to close? What's your average cash collected? There are so many metrics that give you a clearer picture.

Pro tip for running a sales team: give the best closers the best leads. Score your leads, identify the characteristics that predict high close rates, and match the best leads to the best people. A top salesperson at a timeshare company — with 3,000 reps and $1 billion-plus in sales — won the sales competition, used his one-hour prize with the CEO to make that exact request, and 5x'd his income. They then rolled it out nationwide and 5x'd the business.

The best salespeople never blame circumstances. Whenever I have a salesperson who says 'the leads are bad' or 'these people aren't a good fit,' I'm not interested. The guys I love the most never complain. They pick up extra shifts, call the leads the most, work them the hardest, and say, 'These leads are amazing — I'm so grateful to practice and get paid to do it.'

# Part 5: Doubling Sales with the Diagnostic Process

## The Case Study

Three young founders were not making the amount of money they wanted. Before I show you the data, let me explain what each term means. Show rate: the percentage of people who have an appointment and actually show up. Offer rate: the percentage of people we actually make an offer to. Close rate: the percentage of people who are offered who actually buy. Cash collected: the percentage of the full sale amount collected upfront. And units sold: the total output.

Before our changes, they had a 49% show rate, a 83% offer rate, a 27% close rate, and a 47% cash-collected rate, yielding 56 units sold per month. If you don't know these numbers in your business, you should — so you can improve them.

## Problem 1: Low Show Rate

Our benchmark is 70%. Their number was 49%. That delta is a 40% difference — a massive opportunity. The number one factor affecting show rate is total available time slots. But one of the other big factors was the ad targeting itself. They were targeting 18-to-24-year-olds when their ideal customer was 25-to-35-year-olds who were gainfully employed. The reason: the media buyer was optimizing for the lowest cost leads and lowest cost appointments, rather than cost per sale. We had to cancel 75% of appointments before we even got to the 49% show rate — the sales guys were spending most of their time just looking up appointments and canceling them. That's the closest thing to literally burning money.

## Problem 2: Low Close Rate

Our benchmark for this type of sale — a two-call close for a mid-priced consumer service — was about 40%. They were at 27%. Two main issues: first, their discovery was surface-level. They were only asking 'How much money do you want to make?' — one question, surface level. The deeper questions are the 'why' questions: Why do they want this? What changes as a result? How will your life look different? What can you not do now that you would be able to do? Who else in your life would that affect? Understanding why someone is there makes it much easier to get them to agree to getting there.

Second issue: lots of objections were coming up at the close because the discovery was wrong. They were talking at people rather than listening, and the salesperson was talking more than the prospect — a reliable indicator that things are off.

## Problem 3: Organizational Structure

The CEO was the sales manager because he was the best closer — but he wasn't a good sales manager. That's one of the most common issues in sales teams: companies promote their best closer as a sales manager, when those are often two completely different skills. The setting team was also multitasking — trying to both call leads to book appointments and do follow-up nurturing at the same time. Those are two completely different activities, and switching between them kills efficiency.

## The Fixes and the Results

Fix one: we hired a sales director first. If we'd tried to do everything else without fixing this, it would have fallen flat. This is the most common error business owners make — they see the 'what' and not the 'who.' The sales director we brought in had been a trainer for a similar type of sale in a consumer context, was metric-driven, and had the right demeanor: even-keeled, servant-oriented, enthusiastic about pouring into the team.

Fix two: we fixed the ad targeting. The media buyer was optimizing around the wrong thing, and we had to let them go. We hired someone new and fixed the targeting back to 25-to-35-year-olds who love their jobs.

Fix three: we reduced the sales team and reset expectations for the setting team. We cut the lowest-performing salespeople, which raises the overall close rate of the team. We also increased the setting expectation from two sets per day to four. Salespeople get into a rhythm when they have enough calls — if they don't take enough, they become too desperate to close, too hard, not listening enough. When salespeople have more consults, they sell from the back foot: they're open-minded, asking questions, feeling good.

Fix four: we promoted one setter to a lead-nurture specialist, whose job was to bridge the setting and closing teams — coordinating, reminding people of their appointments, and doing personalized voice or video messages to prospects on the day of their appointment.

Fix five: we rewrote the sales scripts. We deepened the discovery, front-loaded the obstacle handling (kill the zombies before you talk about price), and focused the script far more on what the prospect would experience afterward rather than the features of the service. We also drilled the team on looping — overcoming the objection and asking again.

Results: show rate went from 49% to 70% — a 40% increase in sales from this alone. Close rate went from 27% to 41% — a 50% improvement. Cash collected went from 47% to 82% — almost doubled. And total units sold went from 56 to 93 per month — from just these few fixes on one part of the organization.

# Part 6: The CLOSER Framework

## Overview

After pouring over hundreds of scripts, I realized there were always key milestones along the way that, when I hit them, I ended up closing the sale. This process works for B2C and B2B. It works for $500 tickets as much as $100,000 tickets. The process is the same. This is the CLOSER framework.

C — Clarify why the person is on the phone with you. 'Why are you here? What's the problem we're trying to solve?'

L — Label them with a problem. 'What I'm hearing is that you're struggling with this — does that sound about right?' They have to say it. They have to own it. You can't cure cancer until someone admits they have it.

O — Overview their past pain. 'Tell me what you've done so far to try to solve this. How long did you do that for? How did that work out? What else have you tried?' We call this the pain cycle. Repeat it until they've exhausted all options. At that point, you explain how it's not their fault — they were just missing one or two pieces of the equation that you're going to provide.

S — Sell them the vacation, not the plane flight. Don't talk about the modules, the macros, the workouts, the funnels, the URLs — that all sounds like work. Sell Maui, the final destination. There are only three things that ever come out of your mouth on a sales call: questions, restatements, and anecdotal stories to break a belief. Use a short 30-second story for each of the three pillars of your program. For example, if I'm selling accountability: 'When you were a kid, did your parents tell you to brush your teeth? And you'd go brush your teeth — you didn't want to, but they told you to. Now you're an adult. Do you brush your teeth? That's because external accountability turned into an internal habit. That's what we're going to do for you here.' I never mentioned the coaching calls, the posts, the check-ins. I just told them what they're going to experience.

E — Explain away their concerns. The big three: price, decision-maker, and stalls. For price — it's a value issue; they don't see the value because you didn't articulate it. For decision-maker — rely on past agreements ('Does your partner approve of your current struggle? No. Then why would they be against something that fixes something they already don't approve of?'). For stalls — help them confront the decision and make a logical choice. Ask: 'Do you believe this product can meet your needs? Do you want to work with us? Do you have access to funds or know someone who does?' Those are the only three things you need to know.

R — Reinforce the decision. This is something we added later, and it made a huge difference. The customer is making their decision about whether they like your business within the first 48 hours after the sale. Send a personalized video from the CEO. Send a handwritten card if someone bought something expensive. Do a three-way intro between the setter and the closer. How you handle someone in the first 48 hours after they give you money determines whether they stay long-term.

# Part 7: The Conviction Framework

## Why Conviction Beats Skill

After I reworked all our scripts using the CLOSER framework, some of my guys were immediately crushing it while others still weren't selling anything. The biggest thing I got from studying the best sales trainers was the concept of tone. Words — the logic — talk to the logical brain. How you say them talks to the emotional brain. The emotional brain is the one making the decision. How you say what you say is what you say.

Here's an example. If my wife said, 'Alex' with a rising tone, or 'Alex' flatly, or 'Alex' with a falling tone — very different meanings. Very different afternoon for me. That's just one word. Imagine a 30-minute dialogue where each word can be packed with exactly the meaning you need them to feel.

It takes time to develop conscious control over tone. But here's the shortcut: conviction will correct your tone. If you believe in what you sell, you will say it the right way. You won't need all the sales skills — you'll do it right because you actually want to help the person.

The actionable tactics: reread testimonials out loud daily in front of your sales team. If the business you're working for doesn't have testimonials, find another business. Fix everything you possibly can about the product, and never blame a customer for lack of success. And never stop improving. If sales dip, bring your head of customer support into the sales meeting to walk the team through exactly what they do for customers after the sale — the sales team will be reenergized.

Training schedule: 60 minutes a day, 5 days a week. First 25 minutes: read the script and questions out loud with the correct tonality. If they mess up, they start again. Five minutes: drill obstacle overcomes — 'I need to think about it,' 'I have to talk to my spouse,' 'This is too expensive.' The remaining time: listen to call recordings and do game-tape reviews. What went right? What went wrong? What will you do differently next time? Focus on one specific correction, drill it three times in a row, and move on.

# Part 8: The Diagnostic Sale

## The Framework

I cross $100 million in net worth by age 32. I sold my first big company for $46.2 million. Now I buy companies at a lower price, grow them, and sell them. One company we bought had 14 locations. Since then, we've grown to 32 locations. The reason: we focused on the sales process. Specifically, something I call the diagnostic sale, which allowed us to 10x the recurring revenue of the business across all locations.

The diagnostic sales process has seven steps. First, a pre-sale questionnaire — this gets more information that arms the salesperson and increases the prospect's own awareness of the problem. Second, collect their information and credit card — this is key, and I'll explain why. Third, understand their current situation: where are they at today? Fourth, understand their desired state: where would they like to be? Fifth, identify the obstacle: what's in between current and desired? Sixth, present the desired state and your vehicle to overcome the obstacle, and tie your price to the way you're going to get them there. Seventh, give them an incentive to prepay.

The magic is in steps three through five. In weight loss: 'You're currently 200 pounds. What's your goal weight?' She says 140. You say, 'Okay, so you have a 60-pound difference. We don't want people to lose more than one and a half pounds per week. So 60 divided by 1.5 is 40 weeks. We currently charge $99 a week, so that means it's $3,960 to get you from 200 to 140. That means when you pay this amount, you can count that weight as gone. I'll keep working with you for free until you do — provided you follow the steps. You're going to follow the steps, right?' You're not selling a membership anymore. You're selling exactly what they want and putting a price tag on it.

The key insight: we didn't change what we delivered. We only changed how we presented it. When we applied this to the chain we bought, the average revenue per customer went from $200 to $800 — from one change in the sales process.

## Price Presentation and Step-Downs

Always present the price at the highest rate first — the full payment plan price. Then offer a prepayment discount. For example: full price is $4,500. If they prepay in full, they save 20% — that's $3,600. If they put half down, they save 10% — that's $4,050. This way, you get the benefit of a price anchor and you get to be the good guy for giving them a benefit when they prepay, rather than the bad guy for charging them more because they can't pay upfront.

The step-down structure: first, offer prepayment in full with a 20% discount. Second, offer half down with a 10% discount. Third, offer third-party financing. Fourth, offer an in-house payment plan. Fifth — the floor — book the next appointment. Because 'everyone buys something.' There's no reason someone should leave without buying something.

## Transition to Recurring Revenue

A friend was selling a $300/month membership but couldn't get people to stick past three months. He stopped selling it month-to-month and started selling it as a $10,000 program with 36-month zero-interest financing. People who bought were on a payment plan — so they were paying the same $300/month — but their average LTV went from 3 months ($900) to 8 months ($2,400). He didn't change anything about what he sold.

The key: people are far less likely to churn out of a payment plan than out of a month-to-month recurring charge. And if you put an automatic recurring at the end of the payment plan at the same price, you move from a one-time sale to recurring revenue — without the customer even feeling the friction of 're-subscribing.'

The results for the chain we bought: recurring revenue went from 9% of that product line to 60%. Memberships went from near zero to 30%. Average visits per customer went from 2 to 4-6. Revenue in that product line went from $20,000 per month to over $250,000 per month — more than 10x — by following the diagnostic process.

# Part 9: Scaling a Sales Team from 0 to 40 Reps

## The Context

We brought in mercenaries — an outsourced sales team — because the founder had never sold anything and I thought it was too risky to start from scratch. There were three main problems with the outsourced team: we were paying them 20% of revenue as commission, they were getting less than 30% of buyers to ascend (buy the next thing), and they existed outside the business, which is a material issue for the sellability of the company.

Before our changes, the funnel looked like this: 74% of scheduled prospects showed up to call one, 53% of those scheduled call two (the close call), 63% of those showed to call two, and 80% of those closed — yielding about 20% of the original 100 who bought the product ascending to the next thing. We wanted it at 30%.

## The Five Steps to Building an In-House Team

Step one: hire a sales director first. Before hiring 40 salespeople, hire the one person who can then hire and train the 40. Look for someone who has 'been there, done that' — sold this type of product in this type of industry and scaled a big team. They must be metric-driven: the quality and quantity of metrics someone explains about how they do their job is directly correlated with how good they are at it. And they need the right demeanor: even-keeled, humble, servant-oriented, enthusiastic about pouring into the team and watching people improve.

Step two: rebuild the compensation structure. We wanted to go from 20% to under 10%. We comped both setters and closers — because a 20% improvement in show rate is just as valuable as a 20% improvement in close rate, so they're equally important to the business. We built ratchets: close 50% and you keep your job; close 60% and you get a pat on the back; close 70% and your commission goes from 10% to 15%; close above 80% and you get 25%. Winners win, losers churn — and that structural churn is expected and fine.

Step three: rebuild the sales process. We improved every number in the funnel except one — and it was okay, as I'll explain. Show rate went from 74% to 91%: we made messaging less aggressive (the outsourced team was using false urgency and false scarcity), tightened the booking window to same-day/next-day, and implemented personalized video or voice-memo reminders. Schedule rate for call two went from 53% to 94%: we aligned setter incentives with business throughput (not just closer satisfaction), stopped dequalifying people early, and focused setters on confirming the problem and ability to pay — not telling them the price. Show rate for call two stayed strong using the same personalization approach. Close rate went from 80% to 42% — which sounds like a drop, but: we had 1.21x more showing to call one, 1.77x more scheduling call two, and 1.40x more showing to call two. Total, we had 3.5x more volume going to the closers, and 3.5 times 0.5 (the close rate) is still 1.75 — a 75% increase in total throughput. End result: from 20% of the original 100 ascending to 32%. And we paid 9% commission instead of 20%.

Step four: scale the team. We recruited everywhere — outbound, content, and professional recruiters. We saved the director's time by having managers and recruiters do initial screening. We automated onboarding training with an internal course, game tape reviews, and role plays. And we implemented a strict 14-day performance standard: if someone isn't showing results within two weeks, let them go. No special snowflakes — even one person who doesn't follow the process undermines the entirety of the system and kills the culture.

Step five: get rid of the outsourced team. With the outsourced team, we did $7 million in cash collected in Q1, paying $1.4 million in commissions (20%), leaving $5.6 million. With the in-house team the next quarter, we did $12 million, paying $1.08 million in commissions (9%), leaving $10.92 million. The difference was $5.4 million in a single quarter — times four for the year, that's $21.6 million in additional profit. Not revenue — profit.

# Part 10: The Scaling Framework — Six C's

## Overview

These are the six things I use to scale sales teams. This framework helped us build a 3,000-person affiliate base in our physical products company, adding $35 million in sales.

## 1. Closure Sequence

Make sure you have a question-based framework that follows the CLOSER milestones. It should be almost entirely questions. If someone goes off track, you just say, 'Thank you so much for sharing that — that must have been so hard. So what do you think that brought?' And you go right into the next question.

## 2. Consistent Daily Training and Conviction

This is the number one thing. Our team trains 60 minutes a day, five days a week. Twenty-five minutes of reading the script out loud with correct tonality — if they mess up, they start again. Five minutes drilling obstacle overcomes. Thirty minutes listening to a call recording: what went right, what went wrong, what will you do next time. Simple, but so simple that no one does it.

## 3. Call Recordings

If you don't record your calls, you have no game tape. Everyone's tracking mouse behavior on web pages — why aren't we tracking what's happening on calls? Record everything. We use Gong, which is excellent — it will tell you exactly how many minutes people are talking versus listening, how many questions were asked, and much more.

## 4. Communication Cycles and Feedback

If you're fixing a golf swing and you give someone ten things to correct at once, they can't fix any of them. Give them one thing to work on. Same in sales. When you review a call, ask what went right, what went wrong, and identify one thing to improve. Then: one thing at a time. Daily training, daily wrap-up to pump them up, weekly team meetings, bi-weekly one-on-ones for more experienced reps.

## 5. Cut the Fat

If a salesperson can't close in the first one to two weeks, they're probably not going to. It's much easier to take a six and get them to a nine than to take a two and get them to a five. If you have a salesperson you've been waiting for to turn the corner for the last six months — they're not going to. Cut quickly, cut cleanly, and set that expectation up front.

## 6. Competition and Career Path

Salespeople are competitive — they should be. Run competitions on six-week cycles: long enough that they believe they can accomplish something, but short enough that they believe it's going to happen soon. We split people into small teams of four to keep the competition going without making it too cutthroat. Add minor milestones on the career path — 50 deals closed, 100 deals closed, 150 deals closed — with tiny increases in commission at each level. The fact that something is visibly changing keeps them engaged and reduces anxiety in one of the most repetitive jobs in the world.

And remember: separate inbound and outbound, and separate setters and closers. As soon as you can afford to specialize these roles, do it — you'll get dramatically higher conversion rates.
