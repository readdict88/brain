# Stop Caring What Other People Think

*Alex Hormozi — Transcript*

# Introduction

The easiest way to lose in life is to try to avoid looking bad. So you
have to stop caring about what other people think of you.

I'm Alex Hormozi. I own acquisition.com — a portfolio of companies that
generated over $250 million in aggregate revenue last year. Along the
way, I've had to work and push through a lot of things that other people
disagreed with. I had to lose friends, people who didn't agree with or
approve of my lifestyle. Family, too. So this video goes out to anyone
who resonates with that — or who's in a period of life where they know
they need to separate from the pack in order to create the new pack they
want to be a part of.

What I want to do is give you some reframes on how to change your
perspective on how other people see and think of you. This won't
necessarily make it easier to not care about what they think — but it
will allow you to operate anyway. Because at the end of the day, the
work just needs doing.

# 1. Ignore the Opinions of Losers

Richer people won't judge you for saving your money. Fitter people won't
judge you for cutting calories, packing your food, or deciding to eat a
certain way. Higher-status people aren't going to judge you for cutting
bad friends. It's the people going nowhere who have the most to say —
because they have nothing to do. Take the fact that they have something
to say as an indication that they're not doing anything else with their
time. Not that it's some sign you need to change direction.

You're probably on the right path. Their hate, their snide remarks, the
little 'you're missing this' or 'you're not doing that' — those are
actually positive indicators, despite the fact that they sting. They
sting because you're still transitioning your programming from the way
you used to be to the way you want to be.

If you're trying to be successful and you're facing big life decisions,
don't listen to the people closest to you. Listen to the people who are
closest to your goals. And if there's no one around you who is close to
your goals, that means you're going to have to consume information from
the outside. It's almost like you have to separate your brain from your
body. Your consumption of information needs to come from people who are
where you want to be. Your body can stay, for now, in a place it doesn't
want to be — but in time, once your reality catches up, you can actually
move your body to a place that makes it even easier to keep consuming
what you need.

Fundamentally: if you don't want someone's life — if you wouldn't swap
places with them, if you don't envy them in any domain — why would you
value their advice?

These concepts aren't things I'm saying from a pedestal. They're the
foundation that allowed me to do everything else I did in business. I
couldn't have done any of it without this base-level programming first.

## The Hardest Decision I Ever Made

The hardest decision of my entire life was quitting my job. It sounds
basic, but for me, that job was the culmination of everything I had done
up to that point. I was thinking about college admissions in middle
school. I was a three-sport varsity athlete, literary magazine
editor-in-chief, vice editor of the newspaper. All of that to get into
college. Then I went to Vanderbilt, graduated in three years, was vice
president of the rugby team and president of my fraternity — and landed
a white collar consulting job. That job was the culmination of
everything.

And here's what really sucked: my dad was happy. He and I would get
lunch a couple times a week. He'd brag about his son, the businessman.
He'd say, 'You're following the plan — do this for a few years, go back
to business school, get your MBA, do the next thing.' It was always the
next thing. But it was a path.

I knew that getting off that path literally meant forsaking basically
all the friends I had locally — guys who were still following the same
path. Me stepping off that path was, unspoken, an implicit critique of
what they were still doing. And I think that's the really nasty one: if
you start doing something different from everyone else, they assume
you're attacking them for their choices. I don't know why people do
this, but they do. A lot of them want you to fail — because your failure
confirms that the risk they weren't willing to take was the right call.
It protects their ego.

It took me a long time to figure that out. Once I did, I was able to
boil down almost all criticism from other people in my life to this one
statement: 'You do not live your life in a way that I would prefer.'
That's all it is. Every snide remark, every 'why are you doing this?',
every side comment — it's just: you live your life in a way I wouldn't
prefer. And the answer to that is: of course not, because it's my life.
As soon as I got that translation, the volume of the world turned down
for me.

# 2. Extreme People Get Extreme Results

This one is near and dear to my heart. My father is a big believer in
astrology. He has all these thick books on what your life is supposed to
look like. My dad's a smart guy — he just likes it. I'm a Leo 3, he's a
Libra. Libras are the balances. And if there was one thing my dad
repeated to me more than anything throughout my entire upbringing, it
was: balance. 'You're so unbalanced. You're too extreme. You're too
hardcore. Too all-in. Too obsessive about everything.'

I honestly had to fight to be me. And I'm making this video because it
is not common to be unbalanced — it's more common to be mediocre. The
people who obsess about work-life balance tend to be mediocre at both.
If you want something, you have to go get it. The world will try to drag
you down and make you part of the average. It will try to dilute who you
are, make you fit into the mold it accepts. You have to be willing to be
extreme to get an extreme result.

Think about it. You want to make a million dollars a year? It's not a 1%
goal. The top 1% in the US starts around $600,000. A million dollars a
year is more like one in two hundred, one in three hundred. And you have
that goal, and you somehow think you're going to behave like the other
299 people and get there? That's not normal. That's unbalanced. You have
to take unrelenting, obsessive amounts of action for a disproportionate
period of time — often with zero reward, and often with disproportionate
punishment for doing so. And you'll question your own sanity half the
time. 'Am I just being crazy? Is it insane to have these aspirations?'
And everyone around you will say yes — because your failure protects
their ego.

People demand success but refuse to work weekends. People want
opportunity but won't talk to strangers. People claim ambition but sleep
in every day. We are the result of our actions, not our aspirations. So
many people have the same goal as you — very few will do the same work.

# 3. Raise Your Standards

Standards come down to beliefs about what you think is possible. And the
beliefs you have about what's possible create the construct within which
you live your life. We live inside prisons of our own making — based on
our belief system.

A belief system is just a different way of saying 'things you know or
believe to be true.' These are just the facts you live on. And the thing
is, we can't double-check every single belief we hold — it would take
too long. So the vast majority of things we take as true were just given
to us by other people. We are so hesitant to question our beliefs, when
in reality almost none of them are our own. They're someone else's.

Working around a super high achiever shifts your worldview. You see a
different way of operating, and a lot of it comes down to what that
person demands and expects of reality. I'm relatively good friends with
Jimmy — Mr. Beast. I've also read Elon Musk's biography by Walter
Isaacson and Steve Jobs's biography. The billionaire friends I have —
they all share a streak: a level of unreasonableness around their goals
and what they believe to be possible, where they just will not accept
less.

Charlie Munger said something I really love: 'Never underestimate the
man who overestimates himself.' Munger hated big egos and crazy
aspirations — he thought they were risky. But he also said: they tend to
win. And I think there's a huge element of truth in that. When you
witness someone say 'I need this done tomorrow,' and then one by one
systematically dismantle every person in the room who tells them why it
can't be done — and after hearing 20 different reasons why their idea
can't work, they've addressed every single one — they just say, 'Great.
So, tomorrow, then.' That is something I've seen consistently in every
truly high performer I've ever known.

## Determination Over Intelligence

Paul Graham, the founder of Y Combinator, had a tweet I really love. He
said: imagine intelligence and determination on two axes. You have 100
points to allocate between them, like building a video game character.
If you keep taking away determination while keeping intelligence, you
very quickly end up with a very smart person who does nothing and
convinces themselves that nothing will work. But if you keep taking away
intelligence while keeping determination — all the way down — a
100-out-of-100 determined person still tends to be very successful.
Determination, far more than intelligence, is the determinant of
success.

So how do you operationalize determination? Behaviorally, it's an
extinction curve. Someone knocks on a door and nothing happens — that's
a null outcome. Some people knock again. A smaller percentage knock a
third time. A significantly smaller percentage knock a fourth time. And
there are the few who will stand there knocking until they break the
door down — because they believe someone is on the other side. If no one
is, they'll just pay to get the door fixed. That is determination. How
many times can you continue to act without seeing the results of your
action? That is determination.

The goal doesn't change. How you get there — you don't care. You knock,
you knock louder, you shout, you look through the window, you go around
back, you call the phone, you text, you find a mutual friend, you track
them down. How many doors will you go through or break down to get what
you want? That is determination. And it's the one trait I've seen
consistently in every exceptional performer — the unwillingness to shake
from what they believe to be true.

This isn't permission to act like a jerk. But it is permission to say:
if someone cannot disprove why what you believe is possible is
impossible, you can hold on to that with white knuckles and say, 'We
will figure it out. Tell me what obstacles are in the way, and one by
one we will dismantle them until all that's left is the goal.'

Sometimes other people's dreams have to die in order for yours to live.
I remember having to say that to myself — because all it is, is that
they have aspirations for my life that are smaller than my own. Never
take advice from someone who has smaller dreams for you than you have
for yourself.

My dad had higher fear around risk — not malice, not because he didn't
want me to succeed. He didn't want me to fail more than he wanted me to
be a mega success. From a parent's perspective, your child is the thing
they worry about 24 hours a day. And what they want is simple: that
you're a productive member of society, that you're happy, that you can
feed yourself, that you have a decent family and something you don't
hate that makes you money. That's it. But your dreams might be higher.
And if they are, so too might be the risk you're willing to take — and
they have a different risk apparatus than you do. It makes sense. But
the good news is: unless you die, you can always get back up and try
again.

# 4. Pain Is a Prerequisite to Progress

I said this once and got a bunch of comments saying, 'As soon as this
guy said pain is progress, I was out.' And I already know those people
are not going to be successful. Not because I want anyone to go through
pain — I don't think anyone wants that. But you want someone to grow.
The reason they call them 'growing pains' is that as soon as you stretch
past your current potential, as soon as you get into an area you haven't
operated in before, it's uncomfortable. You need the stress to get the
adaptation.

You can beat 99% of people without being smarter or luckier — just by
being willing to endure pain and uncertainty for longer. Because the
fastest growth periods are often the most miserable. If you want
progress, get used to pain.

Paul Graham said: if you want to earn a million dollars, you have to
endure a million dollars' worth of pain. A lot of that pain comes from
uncertainty. They did an experiment with mice — a mouse dropped in water
will paddle for a while, then drown. If they rescue the mouse right
before death, dry it off, and put it back in — it goes 30 times longer.
Not 20% longer, not 50% — thirty times longer. Because now it knows
there's a chance of survival.

The first mouse gives up because it doesn't know anyone is coming to
pull it out. You don't get that either. You don't know if you're going
to make it until you make it. You just have to have faith that if you
keep paddling and keep your head above water, you'll eventually get
there. That's the uncertainty we have to battle.

I'll be honest with you: 2025 has been by far the hardest year I've had
in the last eight years. Out of the last thirteen, it's my fifth worst.
But out of the last eight, it's a local minimum — the hardest year I've
had in close to a decade. I bring this up because of something called
hedonic adaptation: you get used to pleasure very quickly. But what
people don't talk about is that you also get used to pain. You
habituate. The nasty part about entrepreneurship is that you do not
adapt to pain if the pain changes in intensity or variety. Life is
exceptional at varying both. Life doesn't fight fair. And neither does
business. You have to fight regardless. And you have to win.

## Ask for Help

I recently had a friend come out to visit me. He'd started a business in
an industry I know well — but he never once reached out to me for help,
and the business ended up closing. When he came out, I said, 'Dude, what
happened?' He said, 'If I was going to fail, I wanted it to be my own. I
didn't want anyone else to have a part in that. I wanted to own the
outcome regardless.'

I heard that and thought: what a terrible way to see the world. My
perspective is entirely different. I want to win no matter what. If I
need to ask a hundred people for help, it doesn't matter — it's still my
fault whether I win or not. And it's also my responsibility to do
everything in my power to win. I get no points from anyone for losing on
my own. At the end of the day, you never did it on your own anyway. You
were born from someone. My parents are first-generation immigrants.
Everything I have is because of them. I'm also born in America. I'm a
white guy. I have good genetics. There are a hundred things out of my
control. But if you don't play every card you have — what are you doing?

My dad told me a story that really impacted me. I came out of academia
carrying the guilt of privilege they try to instill in you — what a
handcuff, by the way. I shared this nonsense with my father. He said,
'Do you think Shaquille O'Neal said, 'I can't play basketball — I'm
7'1", it's not fair to other people'?' He just looked at me and said:
'Play the cards you have. You can't play basketball. You're not 7'1".
But you can do this, so do it.' If you're good at something or have a
natural predisposition — by every means possible, knock on every door.
Bang the door down if you have to. What's your downside? Shame. Someone
wagging their finger at you for breaking a rule they made up to make you
feel bad. You're going to die, and so are they. So get what you want.

# 5. Success Demands Courage

Peter Thiel said: courage is in far shorter supply than genius. There
are a lot of really smart people. There aren't a lot of very courageous
people. A man with twice the courage and half the advantages will always
beat a man with twice the advantages and half the courage.

There's an interesting observation: why are there so many neurodivergent
founders who build these massive companies? Thiel's thesis — and I agree
— is that many of them simply miss social cues. Because they're less
affected by the social criticism and social rules that other people
choose to adhere to, even though those rules are entirely arbitrary and
made up, they're able to operate in a world largely of their own making.

Right now, the things causing you fear are probably rules you made up,
or rules other people told you that you chose to believe. The way to
conquer those rules — at least the way that helped me — is to play it
out. Fear exists in the vague, not in the specific. When someone says
'I'm going to be a failure' — that's vague. What does it actually mean?
It means you tried something and it didn't work. Is that what you mean?
Because that happens all the time. Okay, so what is it really? Other
people would know you tried something and it didn't work? Does that mean
you give up forever? Because if you kept trying again, would you still
be a failure? If you kept trying, no — you're still trying. You're not a
failure. You're still trying.

What if you lost all your money? About 60% of Americans have less than
zero dollars — they owe more than they have. But even so: sleeping on a
couch, living in a worse neighborhood, driving a worse car — does that
make you a failure? By most of the world's standards, your worst-case
scenario in a developed country is free housing and free food. The true
downside is believing their lie and living the rest of your life as a
slave to someone else's dreams.

# 6. Be Okay with No One Cheering for You

No one cheers for you for not drinking for a day. No one cheers for you
for skipping the club, or not overeating for one night. No single
workout or meal is ever impressive on its own. The reason so few people
understand success is that consistency never looks impressive in the
moment — only at the end. People only root for people who don't need it:
once you've already won, they say 'he's amazing,' but that's the last
time you need it. You always have to be the person who roots for you
before everyone else does.

When about a year out from selling Gym Launch, I wanted to build a
second acquisition channel. We ran a lot of paid ads and I wanted
another way to get customers. I read a book on outbound and we went for
it. 90 days in, we had done exactly one sale. The entire executive team
called an intervention. They said I was spending too much time on
outbound, that my focus was needed elsewhere, that we should do more of
what was already working. They were using my own frameworks against me.

I remember that moment so clearly. I thought of the scene in the movie
Troy — Brad Pitt as Achilles, about to fight a giant. A boy hands him
his helmet and says, 'He's huge. I wouldn't want to fight him.' Achilles
looks down and says, 'And that is why no one will remember your name.'
First off, savage. Second off, it's normal to not want to keep moving
when no one is cheering for you. People only root for others at two
moments: at the beginning when you start, and at the end when you
finish. The problem is, neither of those is when you need it. You have
to master the middle — the boring, soul-crushing, unending, relentless
middle.

After that intervention, I said: 'No, we're doubling down on this.' Over
the next nine months, piece by piece, we got it to work. Eventually,
outbound became 50% of our sales. Having two very different acquisition
channels made the business significantly more stable — and ultimately
sellable. That decision to tell everyone, 'Yeah, you're all wrong' —
took a lot of conviction. Because I also felt bad. I wanted to seem like
a listening leader. But sometimes you just have to be that person.

# 7. Hold Yourself to a Higher Standard

This is different from raising your standards around what you believe is
possible. This is taking that same idea and applying it only to
yourself. Self-love, if there is such a thing, isn't a wine and spa day.
It's holding yourself to a higher standard than anyone else does.
Self-love is doing whatever you can to bridge the gap between who you
currently are and who you believe you can become. The voice inside your
head that's never satisfied with your achievements isn't your insecurity
— it's the part of you that knows you can do more. Don't silence that
voice. It's knocking on your head saying: we can be more. We don't have
to accept this reality.

The primary way humans learn is through modeling — we see other people
do things, and then we do them. If you want everyone in your
organization to rise to a higher standard, you have to hold it yourself
first. Leila had a realization that she wanted people in the company to
dress better on Zooms. Then she looked in the mirror and realized she
was in sweats with no makeup. She started dressing up and looking
presentable. She didn't say a word. The rest of the company followed.
Model the behavior you want to see.

At acquisition.com, just about everyone works six days a week, and
probably half the company works seven. Anyone who hears that and thinks
it's extreme — the people on the other side of the globe who are trying
to beat you are happy to work seven days a week. The seven-day work week
as a concept only became standard around the 1940s. This isn't some
ancient human necessity. You're going to be okay.

This is also where the idea of 'one of zero' came from. At our book
launch, our content strategy was 'one of one' — only make stuff that we
can make, don't make stuff anyone else can make. As the launch
approached, it got better and better, and we decided we were going to
give everything away. When that happened and people went crazy for it,
someone on my team said, 'Dude, that wasn't one of one — it was one of
zero.' It wasn't just doing what only you can do. It was doing things
you didn't know you could do yet. Doing things you didn't know were
possible, but believed were within your potential. I want to live a
one-of-zero life — not just a life only I can live, but the life that
the best version of me, the version that can do things I can't do yet,
would live.

# 8. Accept Hard Trade-offs

There is no perfect way to live your 20s. You either live them up and
become an underskilled 30-year-old, or you work them up and become an
underlived 30-year-old. You just have to figure out which you'd rather
be, accept the trade-offs, and know that there are no do-overs.

In my experience, I've met far more people dissatisfied with having
lived up their 20s than those who regretted working them up. In your
20s, most of the time you don't really know what you want — but what you
need to do to grow and move forward is usually pretty straightforward.
You take the known path, and along the way you gain perspective on what
actually matters. And often you realize there are far fewer things that
truly matter than you originally thought. 'Living it up' in your 20s
might have been chasing your mom's dreams or your friends' dreams — only
to wake up in your 30s having done neither.

And here's another hard truth: what you think will be your 'live it up'
moments in your 20s — your 40-year-old self doesn't value any of that.
But your 40-year-old self absolutely will value the skills you acquired.
The skills stay with you. They compound with time.

I remember when I had six gyms and knew what it would take to get to ten
locations. I looked at what little discretionary time I had left in the
week — watching the Ravens, fantasy football, Netflix series — and asked
myself: am I willing to give those up to get to ten locations? I decided
yes. Some of you aren't willing to give up rec dodgeball or beers with
the boys. And that's fine. Just make sure you're making that trade-off
consciously, not by default.

# 9. Be Willing to Look Like an Idiot

You have to be willing to look like an idiot while you figure it out.
Because once you figure it out, everyone else looks like the idiot for
doubting you. The easiest way to lose in life is to avoid looking bad.

Sam Altman wrote a blog post about the power of being misunderstood. His
point was that you should trade being short-term low status for being
long-term high status — which most people seem unwilling to do. A common
way this plays out is by eventually being right about an important thing
that most people don't agree on. You see something other people don't
see. They bet against you. And all asymmetric returns in life come from
betting against conventional wisdom.

Conventional wisdom is usually right. And so it makes sense for people
to bet against you. But you are n-equals-one, and you have a much longer
history with yourself than they do. You know you more than they do. If
you say you're going all in, you're going all in. Opportunity only looks
like opportunity looking backwards. Today it looks like risk.

# A Few Final Thoughts

When losers go through hard times, they think: 'Poor me.' When champions
go through hard times, they think: 'Poor everyone else who's going to
have to go through this too — and most of them aren't going to make it.'
Despite all the hard that you're going through, you just have to believe
with all your heart that you're going to make it. Because as long as you
keep trying, you are making it.

You become successful the moment you decide you are. There's always
going to be another goalpost. And your current self today is living a
life that your past self once dreamed of. You've accomplished goals you
said would make you happy. You've accomplished things you said would
make you successful. Success is very much in the eye of the beholder —
including your own. That means you can be both successful and
dissatisfied at the same time. They're not mutually exclusive. And I
think that's okay.
